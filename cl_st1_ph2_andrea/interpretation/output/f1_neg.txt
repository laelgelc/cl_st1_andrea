## Interpretation of Factor f1 (negative pole)

### Suggested label

**Contemporary transactional–technological promotional discourse**

This is the strongest overall label for the negative pole. The pole appears to capture a late-modern style of television-commercial language centred on **specific offers, eligibility conditions, digital/technical products, information access, workplace platforms, and institutional service branding**.

Possible alternative labels include:

1. **Modern offer-and-technology advertising**
2. **Eligibility-based deals and digital-service promotion**
3. **Late-modern informational product/service discourse**
4. **Corporate-platform and promotional transaction discourse**
5. **Tech-enabled solutions and sales-event advertising**

These labels foreground slightly different aspects of the same pole: the loading words point both to **transactional offer language** and to **technology/service-platform language**, while the examples show these tendencies especially clearly in 2020s commercials.

---

## Main discourse encoded at this pole

The negative pole is characterised by commercials that sound relatively **contemporary, information-bearing, and service-oriented**. They often promote products or services through:

- concrete offer conditions: **employee, current, card, select, deal, lease, holiday, hurry**
- digital/technical terminology: **ai, computer, technology, wireless, search, video, network**
- corporate/institutional service language: **information, support, team, join, present**
- product-category or sector-specific lexis: **prescription, gas, toilet, sink, bacon, nylon**
- branded entertainment/product references: **nintendo, kenner, rock**

The pole is therefore not simply “technology advertising”, although technology is highly salient. It is broader: it represents a form of commercial discourse in which the advertisement gives the listener **actionable product, service, eligibility, or access information**. The language is often tied to modern consumer systems: financing, leasing, subscriptions, platforms, workplace tools, AI, information networks, branded events, and corporate campaigns.

---

## Evidence from the factor loadings

The highest-loading words strongly support a transactional and institutional reading.

### 1. Offer, eligibility, and deal language

The strongest loading is **employee** (-.87), followed by **current** (-.77), **card** (-.70), **select** (-.70), **deal** (-.64), and **lease** (-.54). These words are typical of commercials that specify who qualifies for an offer, what model or plan is included, and what the consumer must do now.

This kind of discourse is especially common in automotive and retail-finance advertising:

- “current eligible lease”
- “employee lessees”
- “select models”
- “great deals”
- “holiday event”
- “hurry in today”

This is not merely persuasive brand language; it is **conditions-and-offers language**. The commercial becomes partly an informational notice, giving restricted terms, pricing, event names, and eligibility categories.

### 2. Technology and platform language

Another major lexical cluster is digital and technical:

- **ai** (-.64)
- **computer** (-.62)
- **technology** (-.62)
- **search** (-.61)
- **wireless** (-.63)
- **video** (-.51)
- **network** (-.40)

These loadings suggest commercials for contemporary digital products, connected services, platforms, AI tools, communication systems, and smart devices. The pole therefore captures a vocabulary of **technical affordance**: what a system can do, how it connects people, how information is accessed, and how technology solves a practical problem.

### 3. Corporate, organisational, and institutional language

Words such as **information** (-.65), **team** (-.61), **join** (-.55), and **support** (-.39) point to institutional and organisational discourse. These are common in commercials for workplace platforms, corporate initiatives, public-facing brand missions, and community-based services.

This gives the pole a somewhat **corporate-service** flavour. The advertiser is not only selling a discrete object; it is often inviting the consumer into a system, programme, platform, team, network, or event.

### 4. Product-specific and sector-specific vocabulary

The loadings also include words such as **prescription**, **bacon**, **gas**, **toilet**, **sink**, **nylon**, **Nintendo**, and **Kenner**. These indicate that the pole is not exclusively digital or automotive. It also includes highly specific commercial categories: pharmaceuticals, household products, food, toys, gaming, utilities, and domestic technologies.

This supports a broader label such as **late-modern informational product/service advertising**, because the pole seems to capture commercials that rely on **specific product-domain vocabulary** rather than only general emotional appeal.

## Evidence from the example excerpts

The excerpts, all from the 2020s, illustrate several major realisations of this pole.

### 1. Automotive lease and holiday-event offer discourse

The strongest example is the Jeep commercial:

> “Jeep Wrap-Up the Year sales event for great deals… Hurry in today… well-qualified current FCA employee lessees can lease select 2025 Jeep Grand Cherokee models…”

Loading words present: **employee, current, select, deal, holiday**

This is a very clear instance of transactional promotional language. The commercial includes:

- a named sales event
- model restrictions
- employee eligibility
- lease terms
- payment amounts
- time-sensitive urgency

The GMC and Cadillac excerpts work similarly:

> “GM employees with a current eligible GMC lease…”  
> “GM employees and eligible family members with the current eligible GM lease…”

Here the discourse is built around **eligibility and finance**, not just product desirability. This strongly justifies labels involving **offer**, **deal**, **transaction**, or **eligibility-based promotion**.

### 2. AI, workplace, and platform-service discourse

The Zoom excerpt contains **ai** and **technology**:

> “I use Zoom AI Companion… I use Zoom Contact Center… Use Zoom to translate… Technology should confuse us, not help us!”

Although the ad is humorous, the loading words indicate a recognisably contemporary platform discourse: virtual meetings, AI assistants, contact centres, translation, and work communication.

The Microsoft Viva excerpt is even more explicit:

> “a new employee experience built on Microsoft 365 that works with Microsoft Teams… AI gives you access to your company’s collective knowledge…”

Loading words: **employee, information, ai, team**

This is a prototypical example of **corporate digital-platform advertising**. It promotes knowledge management, collaboration, AI, workplace connection, and organisational productivity. This strongly supports the interpretation of the pole as involving **corporate technology and information systems**.

### 3. Information, teams, and institutional affiliation

The Honda excerpt includes **information** and **team**:

> “Honda is proudly supporting Team USA’s Olympic and Paralympic teams. For more information, visit…”

This example shows the pole’s institutional-branding side. The commercial is less about a single immediate product feature and more about corporate affiliation, sponsorship, mission language, and directing the audience to further information.

The word **team** also appears in the sports-related loyalty excerpt:

> “I’ve been on the same team for 17 years…”

This reinforces the presence of organisational or group-affiliation vocabulary at the pole.

### 4. AI search and networked community action

The Ring excerpt includes **ai, search, join**:

> “Search Party from Ring uses AI to help families find lost dogs… Join the neighborhood…”

This combines several key themes:

- AI-assisted search
- app-based participation
- neighbourhood network
- invitation to join
- service availability

This is highly consistent with a label such as **tech-enabled solutions and networked service promotion**.

### 5. Product technology in everyday consumer goods

The Garnier and Shark examples show that **technology** is not limited to software:

> “Patented water resistant technology…”  
> “StainForce technology combines…”

Here, “technology” functions as a product-feature term. The discourse presents ordinary consumer goods as technically enhanced solutions. This supports the broader interpretation that the pole encodes **technicalised product promotion**, where goods are marketed through named systems, features, formulas, and performance claims.

## Decades driving this pole

Because this is the **negative pole**, the relevant decades are those with the **lowest mean Factor 1 scores**.

The lowest-scoring decades are:

| Decade | Mean fac1 |
|---|---:|
| 2020s | 0.864 |
| 2000s | 1.078 |

The 2020s are the clearest driver of this pole, and all provided high-scoring negative-pole excerpts come from the 2020s. The 2000s and 2010s also score relatively low, suggesting that this discourse is not confined to the 2020s but is part of a broader late twentieth- and early twenty-first-century tendency.

The diachronic pattern is striking: the mean scores decline from the 1950s to the 2020s.

- 1950s: 5.456  
- 1960s: 2.942  
- 1970s: 2.010  
- 1980s: 1.612  
- 1990s: 1.450  
- 2000s: 1.078  
- 2010s: 1.252  
- 2020s: 0.864  

This suggests a long-term movement toward the negative pole over time, with the 2020s showing the strongest association. The slight rise in the 2010s compared with the 2000s does not disrupt the overall tendency: commercials increasingly adopt the kinds of language represented by this pole, especially language of **technology, information, platforms, offers, eligibility, and corporate service systems**.

---

## Final interpretation

Factor f1 negative represents a discourse of **contemporary commercial specificity**: advertisements that promote products and services through technical features, eligibility conditions, institutional information, branded platforms, and concrete transactional offers.

The pole is especially associated with recent decades, above all the **2020s**, and its examples show modern commercials speaking in the idioms of:

- employee and lease eligibility,
- holiday sales events,
- AI and digital platforms,
- workplace collaboration tools,
- information access,
- corporate teams and sponsorships,
- app-based services,
- named technologies and product systems.

A concise label for this pole would therefore be:

> **Contemporary transactional–technological promotional discourse**

or, more compactly:

> **Modern offer-and-technology advertising**.

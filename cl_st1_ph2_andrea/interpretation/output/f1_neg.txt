## Interpretation of Factor f1 negative pole

The negative pole of Factor 1 appears to encode a **recent, institutionally specific commercial discourse centred on technology, service infrastructures, eligibility-based offers, and informational/functional claims**. It is strongest in the most recent decade, especially the **2020s**, with the 2000s and 2010s also relatively close to this pole.

### Possible labels for this pole

1. **“Contemporary techno-service and offer-eligibility discourse”**  
2. **“Institutional information, technology, and qualified-deal advertising”**  
3. **“Modern service/technology claims with transactional offer conditions”**  
4. **“Corporate-tech support and eligibility-based promotion discourse”**

The best overall label may be:

> **Contemporary techno-service and eligibility-based offer discourse**

This captures both major strands visible in the loadings and in the high-scoring examples:  
1. technologically mediated services, information systems, teams, AI, support, networks; and  
2. transactional/conditional offers involving current customers, employees, leases, cards, deals, prescriptions, and selected models or plans.

The excerpts reinforce this: all ten high-scoring negative-pole examples come from the **2020s**. This does not mean the pole is exclusively 2020s discourse, but the examples indicate that the most prototypical texts in this sample are recent commercials.

## Lexical evidence from the loadings

The loading words cluster into several overlapping semantic domains.

### 1. Institutional roles, eligibility, and transactional conditions

Highly loaded items include:

- **employee** -0.88  
- **current** -0.77  
- **card** -0.70  
- **select** -0.68  
- **deal** -0.64  
- **prescription** -0.64  
- **lease** -0.54  
- **hurry** -0.48  
- **holiday** -0.51  

These words point to commercials that specify **who qualifies**, **what plan or model is included**, and **what transactional condition applies**. The language is often not simply persuasive in a general way; it is administratively precise. It invokes categories such as current employees, eligible lessees, selected models, prescription status, card use, holiday events, and lease terms.

This is strongly illustrated by the Jeep, GMC, and Cadillac excerpts:

> “well-qualified current FCA employee lessees can lease select 2025 Jeep Grand Cherokee models…”

> “GM employees with a current eligible GMC lease use it to get this low mileage lease…”

> “GM employees and eligible family members with the current eligible GM lease…”

These are highly formulaic offer texts. The discourse is not only about the product but about the **conditions of access** to the deal.

### 2. Technology, information, and digital infrastructure

Another major cluster includes:

- **information** -0.68  
- **ai** -0.65  
- **computer** -0.63  
- **wireless** -0.63  
- **search** -0.61  
- **technology** -0.61  
- **network** -0.40  
- **video** -0.50  
- **team** -0.62  
- **support** -0.42  

This cluster suggests a discourse of **digital systems, platforms, connectivity, and technical functionality**. The Microsoft Viva and Zoom examples are especially clear:

> “Introducing Microsoft Viva, a new employee experience built on Microsoft 365 that works with Microsoft Teams.”

> “With Viva Topics, AI gives you access to your company's collective knowledge…”

> “I use Zoom AI Companion… I use Zoom Contact Center…”

These examples show commercials presenting products as **platforms**, **systems**, or **workplace infrastructures** rather than merely physical consumer goods. The language is oriented toward productivity, teams, access, knowledge, information, and technological assistance.

The Shark and Garnier examples extend this technological claim-making beyond digital products:

> “StainForce technology combines two ultra-concentrated formulas…”

> “Patented water resistant technology to block out frizz…”

Here, **technology** functions as a general commercial warrant: the product is legitimated through technical innovation or proprietary formulation.

### 3. Support, care, and functional benefit language

Words such as:

- **support** -0.42  
- **daily** -0.52  
- **prescription** -0.64  
- **information** -0.68  

also suggest health, wellness, pharmaceutical, or service-support registers. This appears in the Culturelle and Danimals excerpts:

> “supporting your family's immunity is with Culturelle's probiotics scientifically proven for 24-hour immune support”

> “Help support their immune system with new Super Danimals.”

This is a contemporary functional-benefit style: the product is framed as supporting a bodily system, daily routine, family need, or health-related outcome.

### 4. Product-specific and brand/category naming

Some loadings point to more specific product categories or brand domains:

- **nintendo** -0.71  
- **kenner** -0.55  
- **johnson** -0.40  
- **bacon** -0.64  
- **toilet** -0.41  
- **nylon** -0.40  
- **gas** -0.42  
- **sink** -0.34  

These items suggest that the pole also includes commercials with **specific branded product categories**, including toys/games, household goods, food, hygiene, and utilities. Since these words are not prominent in the supplied 2020s excerpts, they should not be overinterpreted as the main centre of the pole. However, they indicate that across the full subcorpus the pole is not limited to software or automotive offers. It also includes highly concrete product-domain vocabulary and named commercial categories.

## Evidence from the excerpts

The high-scoring excerpts display three recurrent discourse patterns.

### A. Conditional automotive deal discourse

The first, second, and fourth excerpts are dominated by leasing and eligibility language:

- **employee**
- **current**
- **select**
- **deal**
- **holiday**

These texts contain exact financial conditions:

> “no monthly payments for 90 days”

> “$280 a month, with $280 due at signing and zero down payment”

> “$169 per month”

> “$499 per month”

This is a highly transactional register. The commercial message is built around the offer mechanics: price, lease status, employee eligibility, selected models, dealer stock, and event timing.

### B. Corporate, team-based technology discourse

The Microsoft, Zoom, and Honda excerpts use words such as:

- **information**
- **team**
- **support**
- **AI**
- **technology**

Microsoft Viva is especially representative of the technological/institutional side of the pole:

> “a new employee experience built on Microsoft 365 that works with Microsoft Teams”

> “AI gives you access to your company's collective knowledge”

> “stay connected as a team”

The discourse presents the advertised product as an infrastructure for work, knowledge, connection, and organizational efficiency.

Honda’s Olympic/Paralympic example is less overtly digital, but still uses institutional values:

> “teamwork”

> “supporting Team USA's Olympic and Paralympic teams”

> “For more information…”

This aligns with the pole’s emphasis on teams, support, and institutional affiliation.

### C. Technical product-performance claims

The Shark, Garnier, Culturelle, and Danimals excerpts show technology/support vocabulary used in everyday consumer goods:

> “StainForce technology”

> “rinse-and-ready technology”

> “Patented water resistant technology”

> “immune support”

Here the discourse is not necessarily about digital technology. Rather, it uses technical or semi-scientific lexis to frame the product as an engineered solution to a practical problem.

## Diachronic interpretation

The negative pole appears to become more important as television advertising moves into the late twentieth and especially twenty-first century. The decade scores show the lowest mean in the **2020s**, with the **2000s** and **2010s** also relatively low. This suggests a diachronic shift toward commercials that are more likely to mention:

- digital and information technologies;
- AI, networks, wireless systems, computers, video, search;
- employee or customer eligibility;
- leases, cards, current plans, selected offers;
- support services, teams, and information access;
- technical product claims and institutional credibility.

The 2020s examples show this very clearly: several are built around contemporary workplace technologies, AI-enabled platforms, employee experience systems, lease eligibility, and support/health claims. This pole therefore seems to capture the **modernization and institutionalization of commercial discourse**, where ads increasingly present products as parts of systems: financial systems, digital platforms, workplace infrastructures, healthcare routines, or technical solutions.

---

## Summary interpretation

Factor f1 negative is best interpreted as a discourse of:

> **Contemporary techno-service and eligibility-based offer advertising.**

It is characterized by lexical emphasis on **employees, current eligibility, selected deals, leases, cards, information, AI, technology, teams, support, computers, wireless networks, search, prescriptions, and branded product categories**. The strongest examples come from the **2020s**, especially automotive lease offers, corporate software/platform advertising, technology-enabled product claims, and health/support-oriented consumer goods.

The pole therefore reflects a recent commercial register in which advertising language is highly **transactional, technical, institutional, and service-oriented**, often specifying who qualifies, what system is being used, what technology supports the product, and where further information can be obtained.

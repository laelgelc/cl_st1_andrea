## Interpretation of Factor 3 negative pole

Factor 3 negative appears to encode a discourse of **everyday domestic consumption, convenience, and sensory household routines**, especially around food, drink, cleaning, and family/home use. The pole is characterised by vocabulary connected with ordinary domestic scenes: meals, kitchens, laundry, household tools, children/family roles, and immediate product usefulness.

Although the decade means are all positive overall, the **lowest mean scores** occur in the **2000s** and **1990s**, with the **2020s, 1980s, and 2010s** also relatively low compared with the strongly positive 1950s and 1960s. Thus, this negative pole is most associated with the **later commercial decades**, especially the **2000s**, which also dominate the example excerpts.

### Main lexical pattern

The negative loadings cluster into several related semantic fields.

#### 1. Home, household work, and practical convenience

Several high-loading words point to domestic labour and product practicality:

- **mother** -0.83  
- **handy** -0.74  
- **sink** -0.62  
- **serve** -0.57  
- **paper** -0.47  
- **wash** -0.43  
- **detergent** -0.42  
- **suds** -0.40  
- **electric** -0.38  
- **ready** -0.37  
- **built-in** -0.36  
- **refill** -0.33  
- **press** -0.31  

This vocabulary suggests commercials concerned with the management of domestic life: washing, cleaning, serving, preparing, pressing, refilling, and making things “handy” or “ready.” The presence of **mother** as the strongest loading word also gives the pole a domestic-family orientation, although the examples supplied are not strongly maternal. In the full subcorpus, however, this loading indicates that texts scoring on this pole often invoke the household caregiver or family manager.

#### 2. Mealtimes, breakfast, taste, and eating/drinking

Another major lexical cluster concerns everyday food consumption:

- **breakfast** -0.60  
- **drink** -0.59  
- **dinner** -0.46  
- **rich** -0.39  
- **sweet** -0.35  
- **eat** -0.33  
- **mmm** -0.68  
- **golden** -0.57  
- **white** -0.47  

These words point to commercials that stage consumption through familiar eating occasions and sensory pleasure: breakfast foods, drinks, sweet taste, richness, and the voiced expression **“mmm.”** This is not abstract branding language but concrete, embodied product use: tasting, drinking, eating, serving, and enjoying.

The examples strongly support this. In the Honeycomb excerpt, the loading words **“mmm”** and **“breakfast”** occur in a playful cereal-commercial scene:

> “Mmm, that high-sweet crunch can’t get past me… Honeycomb cereal is part of this good breakfast.”

Similarly, the Pop-Tarts excerpt repeats **“breakfast”** several times:

> “We’re the breakfast crew and we’ve got our own style of breakfast… Part of this complete breakfast… breakfast should be loud.”

The Baby Bottle Pop example uses **“drink”** in a candy/drink hybrid promotion:

> “There’s a great tasting baby bottle pop that you can drink…”

These excerpts show the pole’s strong connection to **youth-oriented, sensory, snack/breakfast advertising**, especially in the 2000s examples.

#### 3. Family, children, pets, and ordinary social life

A smaller but still coherent cluster evokes family and everyday social participants:

- **mother** -0.83  
- **girl** -0.31  
- **dog** -0.35  
- **everybody** -0.31  

These words suggest a discourse situated in ordinary family or community life rather than elite, technical, or corporate settings. The Pontiac excerpt illustrates the broad populist address through **“everybody”**:

> “Everybody everywhere… Everybody everywhere… It’s getting around fast.”

Although this particular example is an automobile ad rather than a food or cleaning ad, its repeated “everybody everywhere” fits the pole’s tendency toward accessible, everyday, inclusive address.

### Evidence from the examples

The most informative examples are the first five, which contain actual f3 negative loading words. They point to three prominent commercial styles:

1. **Breakfast/cereal and snack advertising**  
   - Honeycomb: **mmm**, **breakfast**  
   - Pop-Tarts: **breakfast**  
   - Baby Bottle Pop: **drink**

2. **Household cleaning/convenience advertising**  
   - Wiss tablets: **handy**  
   - stains, powder, tablets, grass stains

3. **Broad, popular mass-address advertising**  
   - Pontiac Vibe: **everybody**

The first and fourth excerpts are especially important because they show the verbal style associated with the pole: energetic, playful, youth-oriented, and rooted in routine consumption moments such as breakfast. The cleaning-product excerpt supports the domestic-convenience side of the loading list, particularly through **handy**, stains, powder, and tablets.

Some later excerpts contain no listed f3 negative loading words and are therefore less useful for interpreting this pole. They should be treated cautiously. The interpretation should rely more heavily on the loading structure plus the examples that actually instantiate the loading vocabulary.

### Decades driving the pole

For a negative pole, the relevant decades are those with the **lowest mean Factor 3 scores**. These are:

1. **2000s**: 0.225  
2. **1990s**: 0.240  
3. **2020s**: 0.291  
4. **1980s**: 0.311  
5. **2010s**: 0.340  

The **2000s** appear to be the strongest driver among the supplied examples, since all example excerpts come from that decade and the most negative-scoring examples are 2000s commercials. This suggests that, in the verbal subcorpus, the 2000s contain a noticeable concentration of commercials using this discourse of playful everyday consumption, breakfast/snack products, practical household convenience, and broad popular address.

Diachronically, the factor scores suggest that this pole is **least associated with the 1950s and 1960s**, which have much higher positive means on Factor 3, and **more associated with later decades**, especially around the **1990s–2000s**. The pattern may indicate a shift away from the earlier Factor 3 profile and toward commercials organised around fast, sensory, everyday product-use scenarios: breakfast foods, candy drinks, cleaning tablets, convenient household solutions, and inclusive mass-market slogans.

## Possible labels for Factor 3 negative

### 1. **Domestic Convenience and Everyday Consumption**

This is the strongest general label. It captures both major lexical fields: household practicality and routine consumption. Words such as **mother, handy, sink, serve, wash, detergent, suds, ready, refill**, and **press** indicate domestic convenience, while **breakfast, drink, dinner, eat, sweet, rich**, and **mmm** indicate everyday food and sensory consumption.

### 2. **Household Routines and Mealtime Product Use**

This label foregrounds the routinised settings encoded by the pole: breakfast, dinner, washing, serving, drinking, eating, and cleaning. It is especially appropriate because the examples repeatedly stage products within ordinary routines: cereal as part of breakfast, Pop-Tarts as breakfast, candy as a drinkable treat, and detergent tablets as a practical stain solution.

### 3. **Family-Home Consumption Discourse**

This label highlights the family/domestic orientation suggested by **mother, girl, dog, everybody**, together with household and food vocabulary. It is useful if the aim is to emphasise the social setting of the pole: products are presented as part of ordinary family or home life.

### 4. **Sensory Domestic Product Promotion**

This label gives more weight to the affective and sensory words: **mmm, sweet, rich, soft, golden, bright, wonderful**. It fits the food examples especially well, where taste, crunch, sweetness, and enjoyment are central. However, it is slightly narrower than the full loading list because it underplays cleaning and household convenience vocabulary.

### Recommended label

The most balanced label is:

## **Domestic Convenience and Everyday Consumption**

This label best accounts for the full set of negative loadings and the example excerpts. The pole brings together household management, convenient product use, family/home settings, breakfast and snack routines, taste pleasure, and accessible mass-market address. It appears to be driven especially by later-decade commercials, above all the **2000s**, where the examples show playful food, drink, and household-product advertising built around ordinary acts of eating, drinking, cleaning, and serving.

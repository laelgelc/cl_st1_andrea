## Factor f4 (negative pole): interpretation

This pole appears to encode a **mid-century domestic product-demonstration discourse**, centred on **sensory product qualities, ingredient claims, food preparation, household care, and convenience for the family home**. It is driven especially by the **1950s**, whose mean score is the lowest by a clear margin, and all the supplied high-scoring negative-pole excerpts come from 1950s commercials.

### Possible labels for this pole

1. **“Domestic convenience and sensory product demonstration”**  
2. **“Freshness, flavour, and homemaking discourse”**  
3. **“Ingredient-based family household advertising”**  
4. **“Mid-century food-and-home product efficacy discourse”**  
5. **“Convenient homemaking through natural/fresh product qualities”**

The strongest overall label would be:

> **Domestic convenience and sensory product demonstration**

This label captures both the lexical loadings and the examples: commercials repeatedly present products as easy to use, suitable for family meals or household routines, and superior because of their flavour, texture, freshness, ingredients, or practical efficacy.

---

## Evidence from the factor loadings

The loading words cluster strongly around several related semantic fields.

### 1. Sensory food qualities and appetitive description

High-loading items include:

- **creamy**
- **flavor**
- **sweet**
- **fresh**
- **rich**
- **golden**
- **soft**
- **mmm**
- **terrific**

These words point to a discourse of **taste, texture, freshness, and pleasurable consumption**. Products are not described abstractly but through sensory appeal: they are creamy, rich, sweet, fresh, golden, and delicious. This is especially visible in food advertising, but some of the same vocabulary extends to personal-care products, as in “creamy” soap lather or a “fresh” complexion.

### 2. Ingredients, naturalness, and product composition

Another strong cluster concerns what products are made of:

- **blend**
- **natural**
- **ingredient**
- **dissolve**
- **press**
- **ordinary**
- **product**

These items suggest a style of advertising that explains or demonstrates the product’s composition. The commercial voice often tells the viewer why the product is superior: it contains better ingredients, blends more smoothly, dissolves faster, uses fresh or natural components, or avoids “ordinary” substitutes.

This is a highly **expository, demonstrative advertising style**. The product is sold through explanation: its ingredients, preparation, texture, and performance are made verbally explicit.

### 3. Domestic food preparation and meal routines

The pole is also strongly associated with meal-making and kitchen routines:

- **quick**
- **dinner**
- **cake**
- **prepare**
- **serve**
- **eat**
- **minute**
- **breakfast**
- **drink**
- **dish**

These terms place the discourse in the domestic kitchen. The advertised products help the household prepare breakfast, dinner, desserts, cakes, drinks, or dishes. The recurring emphasis on **quick**, **minute**, **prepare**, and **serve** highlights convenience: products save time, reduce labour, and make domestic routines easier.

### 4. Family, children, mothers, and household responsibility

The loadings also include:

- **child**
- **mother**
- **family-related domestic references indirectly through dinner, breakfast, serve, eat**

This supports the interpretation of the pole as tied to **family-oriented homemaking**. The implied addressee is often the person responsible for feeding or caring for the family, especially the mother or wife in 1950s advertising ideology.

### 5. Household and personal-care extension

Although food dominates, the pole is not only about food. Loadings such as:

- **soap**
- **gentle**
- **prevent**
- **last**
- **month**
- **soft**

show that the same discourse extends to household and personal-care products. These products are also presented through ingredients, texture, performance, freshness, gentleness, and lasting results.

So the pole is broader than “food advertising” alone. It is better understood as a **domestic product discourse**, covering food, kitchen products, toiletries, and beauty/care items, all framed through sensory quality and practical household benefit.

## Evidence from the example excerpts

The examples strongly reinforce the loading-based interpretation.

### Mayonnaise: ingredients, freshness, creaminess, serving suggestions

The Best Foods mayonnaise excerpt contains many central loading words: **creamy, blend, ingredient, break, flavor, press, fresh, wonder**. It explains the product’s superiority through its ingredients:

> “Whole eggs freshly broken from the shell, fresh pressed salad oil, choice vinegar and spices…”

It then gives a practical serving suggestion:

> “Blend two parts Best Foods to one part milk and a little lemon juice. Serve hot and creamy…”

This is a clear instance of **ingredient-based sensory demonstration** combined with **domestic recipe instruction**.

### Coffee creamer: natural dairy, blending, dissolving

The Preem excerpt also directly reflects the loadings: **creamy, blend, natural, flavor, sweet, fresh, dissolve**. The commercial contrasts the product with artificial alternatives and stresses naturalness:

> “the only instant coffee creamer that’s a natural dairy product”

It also highlights ease of use:

> “blends in coffee”  
> “dissolves instantly”

This combines **natural ingredient claims**, **sensory creaminess**, and **convenience**.

### Instant pudding: busy-day convenience and family dessert

The Jell-O Instant Pudding excerpt includes **creamy, flavor, quick, dinner, minute, terrific**. It dramatizes the busy homemaker’s day and offers the product as a solution:

> “the terrific busy day dessert you can make at the very last minute”

The discourse here is strongly about **time-saving domestic convenience**, especially in relation to family meals.

### Pillsbury rolls: easy preparation, freshness, sweetness, richness

The Pillsbury excerpt contains **quick, cake, sweet, fresh, rich**. It promotes a breakfast treat that appears homemade but requires little labour:

> “your family will think you spent all day in the kitchen”  
> “Pillsbury does all the hard work”

This is a classic example of the pole’s discourse: convenience products allow the homemaker to produce family pleasure with minimal effort, while still preserving the appearance of care and abundance.

### Swanson TV dinners: convenience for wives and husbands

The Swanson excerpt includes **break, dinner, sweet, fresh, minute**. It explicitly frames the product around household scheduling and marital domesticity:

> “my wife never panics”  
> “she can have a swell dinner ready in just 25 minutes”

This excerpt makes especially clear the gendered domestic logic underlying the pole: convenience foods are advertised as tools that help the wife manage family expectations and unpredictable male schedules.

### Yuban coffee: richness, ageing, blending, freshness

The Yuban excerpt has **blend, natural, flavor, fresh, rich**. It focuses less on quick preparation and more on quality, natural maturation, and flavour:

> “fresh roasted at peak flavor”  
> “this natural maturing brings out all their richness flavor and aroma”

This supports the sensory-quality side of the factor.

### Toothpaste, soap, sugar, and home permanent: household/personal-care extension

The non-food examples show that the pole generalises beyond food.

Listerine toothpaste uses **ingredient, flavor, fresh, child** and combines child appeal, special ingredients, freshness, and prevention:

> “Children love the fresh, lively flavor…”  
> “special ingredient…”  
> “cut down tooth decay…”

Kame soap uses **creamy, cake, fresh, wonder**:

> “Kame’s lather is so gentle and creamy”  
> “your first cake will bring a clearer, fresher complexion”

The home permanent excerpt uses **natural, ingredient, quick, month**:

> “new penetrating ingredient”  
> “lasting and much more natural wave pattern”  
> “for at least six months”

These examples confirm that the pole is not simply about eating. It is about **domestic product efficacy**, expressed through freshness, naturalness, texture, ingredients, quickness, and visible results.

For a negative pole, the lowest mean scores are the relevant ones. The 1950s stand out clearly as the main decade associated with this discourse. The supplied high-scoring negative-pole texts are also all from the 1950s, which confirms that this pole is strongly anchored in early television-commercial language.

The standard deviation for the 1950s is also much larger than for later decades, suggesting that the 1950s contain particularly extreme examples of this style. Later decades are much closer to zero, indicating that the discourse becomes less central or less distinctive over time.

## Diachronic tendency

Diachronically, this pole suggests a discourse that is especially characteristic of **early television advertising**, particularly the 1950s. The commercials at this pole rely on:

- explicit verbal product demonstration;
- detailed ingredient claims;
- sensory adjectives;
- domestic meal preparation;
- convenience for the homemaker;
- family-oriented benefits;
- practical instructions such as blending, dissolving, serving, preparing, heating, or applying.

The later decades do not score as strongly on this pole, which suggests a decline in this specific style of spoken commercial language. The pattern points to a movement away from the long, explanatory, kitchen- and household-centred product pitch that was prominent in early TV commercials.

---

## Summary interpretation

Factor f4 negative represents a discourse of **domestic convenience, sensory product quality, and ingredient-based demonstration**, especially in 1950s television commercials. It is characterised by words of taste and texture such as **creamy, flavor, sweet, fresh, rich**, by ingredient and composition terms such as **blend, natural, ingredient, dissolve**, and by domestic preparation vocabulary such as **quick, dinner, cake, prepare, serve, minute, breakfast**.

The examples show commercials selling mayonnaise, creamer, pudding, rolls, TV dinners, coffee, toothpaste, soap, sugar, and hair products through claims about freshness, natural ingredients, ease of use, family usefulness, and pleasing results. The pole is therefore best labelled:

> **Domestic convenience and sensory product demonstration**

with alternative labels such as:

> **Freshness, flavour, and homemaking discourse**  
> **Ingredient-based family household advertising**  
> **Mid-century food-and-home product efficacy discourse**

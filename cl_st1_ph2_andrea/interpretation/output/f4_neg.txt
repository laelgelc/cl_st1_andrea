## Factor f4 (negative pole): interpretation

This pole appears to encode a **domestic product-demonstration discourse centred on food quality, sensory appeal, and convenient household preparation**, especially characteristic of the **1950s television-commercial verbal style**.

The strongest decade association is clearly the **1950s**: its mean score is the lowest by a substantial margin, at **-0.505**, while all other decades are much closer to zero. The high-scoring examples are also entirely from the 1950s. This suggests that the pole is driven primarily by early television advertising discourse, particularly commercials that verbally demonstrate household products through ingredients, preparation steps, sensory qualities, and family-serving benefits.

### Core lexical pattern

The loading words cluster around several related semantic fields:

1. **Sensory food qualities**
   - *creamy, flavor, golden, sweet, rich, fresh, natural*
   - These words construct products as appetising, wholesome, and pleasurable.

2. **Ingredients and preparation**
   - *blend, ingredient, prepare, dissolve, press, break, serve, dish*
   - These suggest recipe-like explanation and practical household demonstration.

3. **Convenience and speed**
   - *quick, minute, last, breakfast, dinner*
   - The discourse repeatedly frames products as solutions for busy domestic routines.

4. **Domestic/family orientation**
   - *mother, child, dinner, breakfast, food*
   - The imagined consumer is often the homemaker preparing food or caring for family members.

5. **Household-product extension**
   - *soap, gentle, product, prevent, stick*
   - Although food dominates, the pole also extends to household and personal-care products that use similar sensory and domestic vocabularies: freshness, creaminess, gentleness, protection, ease of use.

### Evidence from the examples

The excerpts strongly illustrate this pattern.

In the Best Foods mayonnaise commercial, the discourse is built around **ingredients, freshness, and recipe use**: “whole eggs freshly broken,” “fresh pressed salad oil,” “blend two parts Best Foods,” and “serve hot and creamy.” The product is not merely named; it is explained as an ingredient in domestic food preparation.

The Preem coffee creamer commercial similarly combines **naturalness, dairy purity, sensory richness, and dissolving/blending action**: “natural dairy product,” “fresh sweet cream,” “blends in coffee,” “dissolves instantly,” “golden goodness.” This is highly consistent with the major loadings *creamy, blend, natural, flavor, golden, sweet, fresh,* and *dissolve*.

The Jell-O Instant Pudding and Swanson TV Dinner examples show the convenience side of the pole. They repeatedly foreground the pressure of household time management: “busy day,” “dinner time,” “last minute,” “quick and easy,” “ready in just 25 minutes,” “heat and serve.” These examples link speed and convenience to the homemaker’s ability to provide acceptable family food.

The Pillsbury rolls example adds the semi-recipe demonstration style: “crumble it over the bottom of a cake pan,” “moisten,” “place them in the pan,” “bake them.” It also uses the sensory vocabulary of sweetness, richness, freshness, and family pleasing.

The pole is not limited to food commercials. The Listerine toothpaste and Kame soap examples show how the same discourse extends into personal care: “fresh, lively flavor,” “special ingredient,” “clean and fresh,” “gentle and creamy,” “fresh complexion.” Even non-food products are described through a vocabulary of freshness, creaminess, ingredients, and domestic care.

Saran Wrap is especially revealing because it is a household utility product, yet the language still centres on food preservation and kitchen practice: “press it into place,” “keeps flavor locked in,” “keeps food fresh,” “prepare party snacks,” “bowl or dish.” This confirms that the pole captures a broader **kitchen-and-household management discourse**, not only food taste advertising.

## Decade pattern and diachronic tendency

The pole is most strongly associated with the **1950s**, which has by far the lowest mean score. The other decades hover near zero, with mild negative tendencies in the **1980s, 2000s, 2010s, and 1970s**, but none approach the 1950s. This suggests that the discourse was especially prominent in early television advertising, where commercials often gave detailed verbal demonstrations of products, ingredients, preparation methods, and domestic benefits.

Diachronically, the pattern points to a decline in this highly explicit, homemaker-oriented, recipe-demonstration style after the 1950s. Later decades may retain fragments of the discourse, especially in food, household, or family-care advertising, but the pole is clearly anchored in the early era of television commercials.

## Possible labels for f4 negative

### 1. **Domestic Convenience and Sensory Food Preparation**
This is probably the strongest label. It captures the combination of convenience words such as *quick, minute, prepare, serve,* and *dinner* with sensory food descriptors such as *creamy, flavor, golden, sweet, rich,* and *fresh*.

### 2. **Postwar Homemaker Product-Demonstration Discourse**
This label foregrounds the historical and social character of the pole. The examples are overwhelmingly 1950s commercials addressed to domestic household management: feeding the family, pleasing husbands and children, preparing dinner, keeping food fresh, and using products efficiently.

### 3. **Freshness, Ingredients, and Kitchen-Use Advertising**
This label is more lexical and descriptive. It reflects the prominence of *fresh, natural, ingredient, blend, dissolve, press, prepare,* and *serve*. It also allows for the inclusion of non-food household products such as Saran Wrap, toothpaste, and soap, which borrow the same freshness/ingredient vocabulary.

### 4. **Recipe-Style Household Product Promotion**
This label highlights the procedural quality of many examples: “blend two parts,” “add to cold milk,” “heat and serve,” “press it into place,” “crumble,” “moisten,” “place,” and “bake.” The commercials often sound like miniature recipes or household demonstrations.

## Suggested final dimension label

**Domestic Convenience and Sensory Food-Preparation Discourse**

This label best balances the factor loadings and the excerpts. The pole is defined by a dense cluster of words related to taste, texture, freshness, ingredients, cooking, serving, and quick preparation, and it is strongly driven by 1950s commercials that present products as practical aids for domestic food preparation and family care.

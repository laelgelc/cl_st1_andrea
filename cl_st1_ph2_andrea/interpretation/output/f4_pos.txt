## Factor f4 (positive): interpretation as a discourse dimension

### Suggested main label  
**“1960s-style demonstrative product-feature and value-selling discourse”**

### Possible alternative labels  
1. **“Bright/instant consumer-technology demonstration discourse”**  
2. **“Product demonstration, visual quality, and purchase-value pitch”**  
3. **“New consumer goods: features, colour, speed, cost, and dealer appeal”**  
4. **“Technical-feature advertising with visual enhancement and affordability claims”**

---

## Interpretation

The positive pole of Factor 4 appears to encode a discourse of **product demonstration and practical selling**, especially associated with **1960s television commercials**. The discourse foregrounds visible product performance, technical features, colour/brightness effects, speed or immediacy, price/cost, and purchase channels such as dealers, orders, gifts, or visits.

The strongest lexical loadings include:

- **Visual and photographic vocabulary:** *bright, camera, flash, picture, Kodak, black, blue, white, screen, television*
- **Product-feature and technical vocabulary:** *automatic, combination, electric, safe, fit, handy, built-in, control, complete, detail*
- **Speed/immediacy vocabulary:** *second*
- **Commercial transaction and value vocabulary:** *cost, sell, dealer, buy, order, pay, price, visit, gift, holiday, send*
- **Consumer-benefit vocabulary:** *wonderful, plenty, big, want, tomorrow*

Together, these suggest commercials that do not simply evoke lifestyle or emotion, but **show or describe what the product does**, why it is improved or modern, how quickly or conveniently it works, and why it is worth buying.

---

## Evidence from the factor loadings

The highest-loading words are particularly revealing:

- **bright (.91), camera (.87), flash (.85), picture (.70), Kodak (.66)** strongly point to **visual media, photography, image quality, and optical technology**.
- **black (.64), blue (.54), white (.48)** suggest a concern with **colour, contrast, visual vividness, and visible results**. This is reinforced by the detergent example, where the product makes clothes “bright,” “whiter whites,” “bluer blues,” and “blacker blacks.”
- **cost (.73), sell (.56), dealer (.47), buy (.45), order (.38), pay (.35), price (.35)** indicate a strongly transactional advertising style: the commercial moves from product description to affordability and purchase instruction.
- **automatic (.48), electric (.45), built-in (.30), control (.32), complete (.33), handy (.33)** point to a discourse of **modern convenience and technical innovation**.
- **second (.53)** captures claims of speed and instantaneity, especially in camera, calculator, and product-demonstration contexts.

This pole is therefore not only a “photography” factor, although photography is prominent. More broadly, it represents a **demonstrative, feature-based, visibly verifiable sales discourse**: the product is made attractive through claims about how it looks, performs, operates, costs, and can be obtained.

---

## Evidence from the excerpts

The excerpts strongly support this interpretation.

### 1. Detergent as visible colour/brightness performance  
The Bold detergent commercial uses several loading words: *bright, black, blue, second*. Its central claim is visual and demonstrable:

> “Now you can get clothes better than clean and white. Now you can get clothes bright.”  
> “For whiter whites, bluer blues… even blacker blacks.”

Here, the product benefit is encoded through **visible colour enhancement**. The discourse is comparative and product-performance oriented: not merely “clean,” but “bright.”

### 2. Cameras, flash, and instant pictures  
Several high-scoring examples are camera commercials, especially Polaroid, Sylvania, and Kodak. These use loading words such as *camera, flash, picture, Kodak, blue, black, second*.

The Polaroid example says:

> “Great new camera. Gives you big, big black and white pictures in seconds.”

The Sylvania example says:

> “I just took four flash pictures without changing bulbs.”  
> “Blue Dot Flash Cube.”  
> “When the dot is blue, the bulb is good.”

These examples show the pole’s emphasis on **new visual technology**, **ease of use**, **instant results**, and **technical novelty made accessible to ordinary consumers**.

### 3. Affordability and value claims  
Other excerpts broaden the pole beyond photography. The Jeep commercial uses *cost, sell, want* and stresses practical value:

> “Jeep vehicles are low in cost, cost less to operate, too.”  
> “A two-year-old Jeep Universal sells for up to 90% of its original factory list price.”

The Dodge commercial similarly uses *cost* and *want*:

> “If you don’t want to pay that big car toll…”  
> “luxury without sending you overboard on price.”

These examples show that the pole also includes **economic justification**: low cost, resale value, price, and rational purchase appeal.

### 4. Dealer, gift, and purchase-route discourse  
The Kodak movie camera example combines sentiment with practical product selling:

> “A Kodak Instamatic movie camera will make a perfect gift.”  
> “A whole roll will cost you less than a dozen snapshots.”  
> “At your Kodak dealers.”

This combines several elements central to the pole: **camera technology, family memory, gift suitability, cost reassurance, and dealer availability**.

### 5. Modern office calculation and speed  
The Olivetti Underwood calculator example uses *cost* and *second*:

> “Just a second.”  
> “It used to take me hours… now in seconds.”  
> “complete accuracy.”

This fits the wider discourse of **modern technical efficiency**: products save time, reduce errors, and improve control.

The positive mean is not extremely large, but the **1960s are clearly the highest-scoring decade**, and all ten supplied high-scoring excerpts come from the 1960s. This strongly suggests that the pole is driven by a subset of 1960s commercials, especially those advertising cameras, detergents, cars, calculators, carpets, toys, and other consumer goods through concrete product claims.

The 1990s show only a very small positive mean, while the 1970s and 2020s are essentially neutral. The 1950s have the lowest mean, though with a large standard deviation, suggesting variability rather than a stable association with this pole.

---

## Diachronic tendency

Diachronically, the pole appears to capture a **mid-century, especially 1960s, advertising register** rather than a steady long-term trend. It is strongest in the decade where television advertising often foregrounded:

- new consumer technologies,
- household modernization,
- visible product demonstrations,
- colour and brightness effects,
- “instant” or rapid performance,
- price and cost reassurance,
- dealer-based purchase instructions,
- gift and holiday consumption.

The discourse is therefore characteristic of an era in which commercials often explained and demonstrated products quite explicitly, presenting them as **modern, practical, affordable, technically improved, and visually impressive**.

---

## Overall interpretation

Factor f4 positive can be interpreted as a discourse of **demonstrative consumer-product modernity**. It combines the language of visual proof — *bright, picture, flash, black, blue, white* — with the language of technical convenience — *automatic, built-in, control, handy, complete* — and the language of concrete selling — *cost, price, buy, dealer, order, pay, gift*.

A concise label for this pole would be:

> **“Product demonstration and value-selling discourse of visual/technical consumer goods.”**

A more historically specific label would be:

> **“1960s demonstrative consumer-modernity advertising: bright visuals, instant technology, and affordable purchase.”**

## Factor f5 (negative pole): interpretation as a discourse dimension

The negative pole of Factor 5 appears to encode a discourse of **practical product performance, demonstrable usefulness, and consumer problem-solving**, especially characteristic of **1960s television-commercial speech**. High-scoring texts at this pole present products as things that *work*, *hold*, *stick or do not stick*, *run*, *control*, *prepare*, *complete*, *cost*, *pay for themselves*, or make everyday activities easier and more enjoyable. The pole is strongly associated with commercials that verbally demonstrate product functions, emphasise material or technical properties, and frame consumption through concrete domestic, automotive, food, grooming, and household-use scenarios.

### Suggested labels for this pole

Possible labels include:

1. **Demonstrated Product Performance and Practical Utility**
2. **Functional Consumer Problem-Solving**
3. **Material Performance, Convenience, and Everyday Use**
4. **1960s Hard-Sell Demonstration Discourse**
5. **Concrete Product Efficacy and Value Claims**

The strongest overall label would be:

> **Demonstrated Product Performance and Practical Utility**

This label captures both the lexical pattern and the example texts: the commercials repeatedly describe products through what they physically or practically do, how they solve a problem, and why they are efficient, reliable, convenient, or good value.

Diachronically, this suggests that the dimension captures a style that is particularly associated with mid-century product-demonstration advertising, while remaining available in later advertising when commercials foreground practical functionality, product testing, household convenience, or concrete value claims.

## Evidence from the factor loadings

The loading words point to several overlapping semantic fields.

### 1. Product action, operation, and performance

High-loading words include:

- **run**
- **control**
- **act**
- **build**
- **hold**
- **stick**
- **complete**
- **ready**
- **happen**

These words suggest commercials concerned with how products behave or perform. They are not primarily abstract branding terms; they are practical, action-oriented words. Products are described as things that *run*, *hold*, *control*, *act*, *stick*, become *ready*, or produce *complete* results.

This is especially compatible with product-demonstration discourse, where the verbal script explains what a product does under test-like or everyday conditions.

### 2. Physical/material properties and demonstrations

The words:

- **weight**
- **stick**
- **hold**
- **rubber**
- **screen**
- **picture**
- **detail**
- **weather**
- **tablet**

point toward concrete, material, or technological properties. These are the kinds of lexical items likely to appear in commercials showing tests, comparisons, close-ups, mechanisms, or physical proof: how much something weighs, whether it sticks, whether it holds, how a screen or picture looks, how a product behaves in weather, and so on.

This supports an interpretation of the pole as involving **material evidence and functional proof**.

### 3. Domestic consumption, food, and preparation

Several loadings relate to eating and household food practices:

- **eat**
- **food**
- **dinner**
- **prepare**
- **sweet**

These suggest a domestic consumer context: cookware, wraps, snack foods, food storage, dinner preparation, and everyday eating. The examples strongly support this, with Teflon cookware, Baggies plastic bags, Kaiser foil, and General Mills snacks.

The pole is therefore not simply technological. It also includes **domestic practicality**: keeping food fresh, preventing sticking, making meals easier, or providing snacks.

### 4. Cost, purchase, and consumer value

The loadings also include:

- **pay**
- **cost**
- **buy**
- **send**

These terms point to explicit consumer-economic discourse: price, value, purchase, guarantees, money back, and direct response. In the examples, the Dodge commercial foregrounds not wanting to “pay that big car toll,” while the antifreeze commercial includes a money-back guarantee. The calculator example discusses cost calculations and efficiency.

This supports the label **practical utility and consumer value**, rather than simply “product demonstration.”

### 5. Positive evaluative promotion

The words:

- **great**
- **amazing**
- **big**
- **enjoy**
- **hope**

show that the pole also contains familiar promotional evaluation. However, these evaluative words occur alongside concrete functional lexis. The pole is not just about praise; it is praise attached to claims of performance, convenience, readiness, strength, freshness, accuracy, or value.

## Evidence from the example excerpts

The examples illustrate the loading pattern very clearly. They are mostly 1960s commercials in which the spoken text explains a product’s practical benefit through demonstration, comparison, or performance claim.

Loading words include **run, eat, food, stick, great**.

This is a strong example of the pole’s practical problem-solution orientation. Teflon solves the problem of food sticking and cleanup; Xerex solves the problem of radiator leaks. The discourse is materially concrete and functionally demonstrative.

### Groom and Clean hair product

Loading words include **enjoy, ready, hold, great**.

Here the product is presented through bodily/grooming performance: hair *holds*, looks natural, and avoids grease. The commercial also uses youthful 1960s promotional language — “wild new experience,” “make the scene,” “join the swing” — but the core claims are still about product function.

### Baggies plastic bags

The Baggies excerpt includes:

Loading words include **weight, food, let, hold**.

This is almost a textbook case of product-demonstration discourse. The ad uses a physical test involving weight to prove that Baggies are stronger and preserve freshness. The lexical items **weight**, **food**, and **hold** directly encode the demonstration.

### Dodge Polara

This example shows the consumer-value side of the pole. The car is sold through a combination of affordability, size/luxury, performance, and readiness. The discourse is promotional, but the evaluation is anchored in cost and practical value.

Again, the ad uses an explicit test. The product’s value is demonstrated through a physical contrast: ordinary spray sticks; Hidden Magic holds without stickiness. This strongly supports a label involving **demonstrated performance**.

Here the product is a technical convenience item. It is sold through readiness, ease of use, and the ability to capture pictures without changing bulbs. This aligns with the pole’s focus on practical product functionality.

## Discourse encoded by the pole

Taken together, the loadings and examples suggest that this pole encodes a discourse in which commercials present consumer products as **practical technologies of everyday improvement**. The product is usually framed as a solution to a concrete problem:

- food sticks → use Teflon or foil;
- food loses freshness → use Baggies;
- hair will not hold naturally → use Groom and Clean or Hidden Magic;
- radiator leaks happen → use antifreeze;
- calculations take too long → use a calculator;
- changing flash bulbs is inconvenient → use flash cubes;
- records can be damaged → use Zenith’s tonearm;
- big-car luxury costs too much → buy Dodge.

The verbal style is explanatory, demonstrative, and benefit-driven. It often relies on claims of visible proof, physical testing, mechanical superiority, freshness, strength, accuracy, readiness, or economy.

This pole therefore appears to capture a **commercial discourse of practical efficacy**: products are worth buying because they perform better, solve problems, save time, reduce cost, improve everyday routines, or provide technically demonstrable advantages.

---

## Diachronic interpretation

The dominance of the **1960s** is important. The pole seems closely associated with a mid-century advertising style in which commercials often worked by:

- naming a problem;
- showing or describing a product demonstration;
- explaining the product’s mechanism or special feature;
- making a direct performance claim;
- closing with an imperative, guarantee, or value proposition.

The 1960s examples include cookware, food storage, hair products, snack foods, cars, cameras, calculators, foil, and stereos. This range suggests that the pole is not product-category specific. Rather, it captures a broader 1960s commercial register: energetic, direct, concrete, and demonstrative.

The later negative means in the 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s suggest that this discourse does not disappear. It may reappear whenever modern commercials return to functional claims, food preparation, technical features, or problem-solution demonstrations. However, the much stronger 1960s mean indicates that this discourse is especially characteristic of that decade in the sampled verbal subcorpus.

---

## Summary interpretation

**Factor f5 negative pole** represents a discourse of **demonstrated product performance, practical utility, and concrete consumer problem-solving**, driven especially by **1960s commercials**. The loading words foreground product action, material performance, food and domestic use, cost/value, readiness, and positive promotional evaluation. The examples show commercials explaining how products hold, prevent sticking, keep food fresh, calculate accurately, save money, take pictures, improve grooming, or deliver superior sound. The pole is therefore best understood as a mid-century-style advertising discourse of **functional proof and everyday usefulness**.

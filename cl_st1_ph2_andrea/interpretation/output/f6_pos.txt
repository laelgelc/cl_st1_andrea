## Factor f6 positive pole: interpretation

The positive pole of Factor 6 appears to encode a discourse of **gentle domestic and bodily cleanliness**, centred on products that clean, soften, whiten, beautify, or care for bodies, skin, clothes, and household surfaces. The lexical pattern combines three closely related semantic fields:

1. **Beauty, femininity, and personal-care aesthetics**  
   High-loading words such as *lovely, beauty, delicate, woman, lady, soft, beautiful, girl, young, creamy* point to a discourse of feminine-coded attractiveness, softness, and bodily care.

2. **Laundry, washing, soap, and household cleaning**  
   Words such as *soap, wash, detergent, clothes, suds, machine, white, bright* strongly associate the pole with cleaning products, especially laundry detergents, soaps, bleaches, and sanitizers.

3. **Gentleness, softness, and protective care**  
   Loadings such as *gentle, soft, kind, delicate* suggest that cleaning is not framed simply as removal of dirt, but as careful, reassuring, skin- or fabric-friendly treatment.

Together, these loadings suggest a commercial register in which cleanliness is aestheticised and domesticated: products promise not only to clean, but to make skin softer, clothes whiter, colours brighter, bodies fresher, and women or families feel cared for.

## Evidence from the excerpts

The high-scoring examples are overwhelmingly from the **1980s**, which is also the only decade with a positive mean score. The excerpts strongly illustrate the laundry, soap, skin-care, and domestic-cleaning side of the loading set.

Several examples focus on laundry and whiteness:

- Lysol Laundry Sanitizer: “your wash so white, bright, fresh-smelling”
- Tide with bleach: “whitens better than any other detergent”
- Clorox 2: “keeps white, bright” and “helps clothes look newer longer”
- Dermasafe: “hypoallergenic laundry detergent” that “cleans your clothes” and “loves your skin”

These examples activate loading words such as *wash, detergent, white, clothes,* and *bright*. The discourse here is about visible cleanliness, hygienic assurance, and fabric care.

Other examples focus on soap, freshness, and bodily care:

- Irish Spring: “green and white stripes,” “deodorant soap,” “fresh and clean”
- Dove: “regular soap,” “softer, smoother skin,” “my skin feels soft”
- Hair-care example: “clean beauty”

These excerpts activate *soap, beauty, woman, soft,* and *green*. The emphasis is not merely functional cleansing but sensory and aesthetic transformation: freshness, smoothness, softness, and beauty.

The Huggies example extends the same semantic field into infant care, with “soft, gentle elastic,” showing that the pole also includes protective care discourse, not only beauty or laundry.

## Decades driving the pole

The mean decade scores show that this pole is most associated with the **1980s**:

| Decade | Mean f6 |
|---|---:|
| 1980s | 0.029 |
| 1950s | -0.049 |
| 1970s | -0.165 |
| 1990s | -0.160 |
| 2020s | -0.165 |
| 2000s | -0.216 |
| 2010s | -0.272 |
| 1960s | -0.447 |

Although the 1980s mean is only slightly positive, it is the highest decade score, and all ten illustrative high-scoring texts come from the 1980s. This suggests that the pole is driven especially by 1980s commercials for detergents, soaps, bleaches, deodorant soaps, skin-care products, diapers, and household cleaners.

The 1950s have a near-neutral mean but a large standard deviation, which may indicate that some 1950s texts also contain this kind of domestic-beauty-cleanliness vocabulary, though not consistently across the decade. The later decades, especially the 2000s and 2010s, score lower on average, suggesting that this particular lexical style becomes less central in the selected verbal-commercial corpus after its 1980s concentration.

## Diachronic tendency

Diachronically, this does not look like a steadily increasing or decreasing dimension. Instead, it appears to be a **localized 1980s peak** in a lexical-commercial style built around laundry, soap, whiteness, softness, and feminine/domestic care. The pole captures a mode of advertising language where cleaning products and personal-care products are promoted through sensory reassurance: *white, bright, fresh, soft, gentle, beautiful, lovely*.

## Possible labels for f6 positive pole

### 1. **Gentle Domestic Cleanliness and Beauty**
This is probably the best overall label. It captures both major parts of the loading set: domestic cleaning/laundry vocabulary and personal-care/beauty vocabulary. It also reflects the repeated emphasis on *gentle, soft, delicate,* and *lovely*.

### 2. **Soft, White, and Beautiful Cleanliness**
This label foregrounds the recurring evaluative imagery in the examples: clothes become *white* and *bright*, skin becomes *soft* and *smooth*, and bodies become *fresh* and *clean*. It is more evocative, though slightly narrower than the first label.

### 3. **Feminised Laundry, Soap, and Care Discourse**
This label highlights the gendered aspect of the loadings: *woman, lady, girl, beauty, lovely, delicate*. It is appropriate because the pole combines domestic-product language with feminine-coded beauty and care language. However, it may understate the broader household-cleaning and infant-care examples.

### 4. **Aestheticised Household and Body Care**
This label captures the way the pole treats cleaning as an aesthetic and sensory improvement: whiter clothes, brighter colours, softer skin, fresher scent, cleaner beauty. It is analytically broad and useful, though less directly tied to the highly loaded words *soap, detergent,* and *wash*.

### 5. **Laundry/Soap Cleanliness with Gentle Care**
This is the most literal label. It fits the examples very well, especially the 1980s texts, but it gives less prominence to the beauty/femininity loadings such as *lovely, beauty, woman, lady,* and *delicate*.

## Recommended label

**Gentle Domestic Cleanliness and Beauty**

This label best represents the positive pole because it integrates the main lexical clusters: cleaning products, laundry and soap, whiteness and brightness, softness and gentleness, and feminine-coded beauty/care language. The pole is most clearly driven by 1980s commercials in which household and personal-care products promise hygienic, aesthetic, and emotionally reassuring forms of cleanliness.

## Interpretation of Factor f7 negative pole

Factor f7 negative appears to encode a discourse of **everyday care, household/personal wellbeing, and reassuring product-enabled maintenance**. The pole is associated with language that presents products as helping consumers manage ordinary life: feeding, preparing, staying safe, caring for children or pets, enjoying food, preserving appearance, avoiding loss, and maintaining connection or comfort.

This interpretation is supported by both the lexical loadings and the high-scoring negative-pole examples, although the examples show only a limited part of the full lexical pattern.

## Decades driving this pole

Because this is the negative pole, the relevant decades are those with the **lowest mean scores** on Factor 7:

- **2020s:** 0.068  
- **2010s:** 0.097  
- **1990s:** 0.120  
- **2000s:** 0.127  

All decade means are positive, so this pole is not strongly decade-separated in absolute terms. However, the **lowest scores occur in the most recent decades**, especially the **2020s**, and all the supplied illustrative excerpts are from 2020s commercials. This suggests a mild diachronic tendency for this discourse to become more characteristic of recent advertising, particularly in selected 2020s texts.

The 1950s and 1960s have the highest positive means and therefore do not drive this negative pole.

## Lexical basis of the pole

The loading words cluster around several related semantic fields.

### 1. Care, safety, protection, and continuity

Important loading items include:

- **safe**
- **stay**
- **lose**
- **child**
- **young**
- **dog**

These suggest a discourse concerned with keeping people, pets, or valued things secure, protected, and present. The verbs and nouns point to avoiding risk or loss, maintaining safety, and caring for vulnerable dependents such as children, young people, or animals.

This is strongly reflected in the 2020s examples:

- Ring helps families “find lost dogs.”
- Spruce is a “pet-friendly” weed killer.
- Hyundai helps make accidents “stay little.”
- Microsoft Viva helps workers “stay connected.”

Although the examples mostly activate only **dog** and **stay**, they illustrate the broader loading pattern of safety, maintenance, protection, and care.

### 2. Domestic consumption, food, and routine

Another group of loading words relates to food and household routines:

- **food**
- **dish**
- **breakfast**
- **prepare**
- **plenty**
- **rich**
- **weight**

These items suggest domestic and bodily routines: preparing food, eating breakfast, managing diet or weight, and presenting food as abundant, satisfying, or pleasurable. This points to a discourse of everyday practical consumption rather than large-scale aspiration or abstract branding.

The Taco Bell example partly reflects this through food-related language: “flavor,” “cheesy,” “crunch,” and “Loaded Nacho Taco,” even though the only loading word present is **stay**. The loading set suggests that across the full subcorpus, this pole includes a wider food/domestic advertising register than the small sample of excerpts alone shows.

### 3. Positive affect and product enjoyment

Several loadings are strongly evaluative:

- **favorite**
- **special**
- **wonderful**
- **enjoy**
- **lovely**
- **pleasure**
- **rich**

These words frame products through affective satisfaction, warmth, and sensory or emotional appeal. The discourse is not only practical but also reassuringly positive: the product makes everyday life more enjoyable, pleasant, special, or comfortable.

This positive-affective element appears indirectly in the examples:

- Taco Bell offers “flavor,” “possibilities,” and “value beyond belief.”
- Garnier promises “ultra glassy sleek.”
- Hyundai links safety to the consumer being “irreplaceable.”
- The Red Bull dog commercial uses playful humour around a “crazy dog.”

The loading words show that, in the full subcorpus, this pole is associated with a warmly evaluative commercial style.

### 4. Product offers and consumer-facing promotion

The pole also includes explicitly commercial terms:

- **product**
- **offer**
- **hurry**
- **favorite**
- **special**

These suggest a promotional discourse oriented toward concrete products, special offers, and consumer invitation. This is not a purely narrative or institutional mode; it remains recognisably advertising-oriented, with products positioned as immediate solutions to everyday needs.

## Interpretation of the example excerpts

The 2020s examples show several realizations of the same broad discourse.

### Microsoft Viva: staying connected

The Microsoft Viva excerpt uses **stay** in the phrase “stay connected as a team.” Here the product is framed as a way to preserve connection, knowledge-sharing, and continuity under changed working conditions. This extends the pole beyond household care into workplace wellbeing and social/organizational maintenance.

### Dog and pet-care examples

Several excerpts use **dog**:

- Red Bull’s comic “crazy dog” scenario.
- Spruce’s “pet-friendly” weed killer.
- Ring’s lost-dog search technology.

These examples foreground pets as family members, vulnerable companions, or sources of humour. This aligns closely with the loading cluster around **dog**, **safe**, **lose**, **child**, and **young**, suggesting care for dependents and the protection of valued domestic life.

### Taco Bell, Garnier, Hyundai: staying within, staying up, staying little

The repeated loading word **stay** is used flexibly:

- “Why stay inside the lines…”  
- “sleek that stays up to five washes”
- accidents that “stay little”

Across these examples, **stay** contributes to meanings of persistence, maintenance, restraint, and protection. Products help effects last, keep risks small, or encourage consumers to move beyond ordinary limits in a playful way.

## Possible labels for f7 negative

### 1. **Everyday Care and Reassurance Discourse**

This is probably the strongest label. It captures the recurring emphasis on safety, pets, children, food, routine, and positive reassurance. Products are presented as helping consumers care for themselves, their families, their pets, or their daily environments.

### 2. **Domestic Wellbeing and Product Comfort Discourse**

This label foregrounds the domestic and lifestyle vocabulary: **food**, **breakfast**, **dish**, **prepare**, **child**, **dog**, **safe**, **enjoy**, **pleasure**, **lovely**, and **wonderful**. It is suitable if the pole is understood as centred on home, family, consumption, and emotional comfort.

### 3. **Everyday Protection, Maintenance, and Enjoyment**

This label captures the combination of practical and affective meanings: products help things **stay** safe, connected, sleek, small, or under control, while also offering pleasure, abundance, favourite tastes, or special experiences.

### 4. **Family/Pet Safety and Everyday Lifestyle Solutions**

This label is more specific to the supplied 2020s excerpts, especially those involving dogs, pet safety, lost pets, and accident prevention. However, it may underrepresent the broader loading set, especially the food, pleasure, and promotional vocabulary.

## Recommended label

**Everyday Care and Reassurance Discourse**

This label best balances the full loading pattern with the examples. The pole brings together language of safety, staying, avoiding loss, caring for children or pets, preparing and enjoying food, and presenting products as pleasant, special, reliable solutions. Diachronically, the discourse appears most associated with the **2020s**, and to a lesser extent the **2010s/2000s/1990s**, suggesting that recent television commercials in this corpus more often use verbal strategies centred on care, connection, safety, and everyday reassurance.

## Factor f7 positive pole: interpretation as a discourse dimension

The positive pole of Factor 7 appears to encode a discourse of **tangible product demonstration, component-based explanation, and practical consumer instruction**, especially characteristic of early television advertising. It is strongly driven by the **1950s**, with the **1960s** also scoring relatively highly, and then shows a steady decline across later decades.

### Suggested labels for this pole

Possible labels include:

1. **“Product-Mechanism Demonstration”**  
2. **“Material Product Features and Functional Explanation”**  
3. **“Component-Based Utility Advertising”**  
4. **“Practical Demonstration of Tangible Goods”**  
5. **“Mid-Century Product Instruction and Feature Display”**

Of these, the most suitable overall label is probably:

> **Product-Mechanism Demonstration**

This label captures both the lexical loadings and the high-scoring examples: commercials at this pole foreground physical products, their parts, their mechanisms, their materials, and the practical advantages those features provide.

---

## Lexical evidence

The loading words cluster around several related semantic fields.

### 1. Physical product parts and mechanisms

High-loading items such as:

- **point**
- **tube**
- **refill**
- **part**
- **ingredient**
- **fit**
- **dissolve**

suggest discourse focused on the internal or visible components of products. These are not abstract brand-image words; they refer to concrete features that can be shown, handled, replaced, fitted, filled, or explained.

This is especially clear in the writing-instrument vocabulary:

- **point**
- **paper**
- **write**
- **answer**
- **touch**
- **refill**

These words strongly indicate commercials for pens and writing products, but the broader pattern extends beyond pens to picture tubes, home permanents, radios, cars, dehumidifiers, paper cups, and beauty products.

### 2. Demonstration and explanation

Words such as:

- **mean**
- **seem**
- **happen**
- **prevent**
- **course**
- **answer**
- **hear**

point to an explanatory, demonstrative style. These commercials often tell the viewer what a feature means, what happens when the product is used, what it prevents, or what sensory evidence proves its quality.

The discourse is instructional and evidential: the product’s value is explained through visible or audible proof.

### 3. Consumer choice and practical evaluation

Words such as:

- **choose**
- **ordinary**
- **exclusive**
- **famous**
- **beautiful**
- **quick**
- **tomorrow**
- **month**
- **hour**

show a typical advertising frame: the consumer is invited to choose a product because it has exclusive, famous, beautiful, quick, or long-lasting properties. However, these promotional adjectives are embedded in practical explanations rather than purely emotional or lifestyle appeals.

The pole therefore reflects a discourse in which persuasion is achieved by **showing how the product works** and **naming the functional advantage of its design**.

---

## Evidence from the examples

The high-scoring examples strongly support this interpretation. Notably, all ten provided high-scoring excerpts are from the **1950s**, which matches the decade scores.

### Writing instruments as a central subtheme

Several of the highest-scoring texts are for pens. These examples contain many of the strongest loading words.

In the Schaefer pen commercial, the product is presented through writing, paper, handwriting, the pen point, and the filling tube:

- **point**
- **paper**
- **answer**
- **write**
- **touch**
- **choose**
- **tube**
- **course**

The discourse combines emotional appeal — handwriting as personality — with highly specific product mechanics: the custom-fitted point, the snorkel filling system, and the tube that touches the ink.

Similarly, the Parker 51 commercial focuses on the technical treatment of the pen point:

> “exclusive new electro-polished point”  
> “dissolves all the microscopic burrs and ridges found on ordinary points”  
> “the smoothest you ever touch to paper”

This is a classic example of the pole: a physical component is named, a technical process is described, and the consumer benefit is demonstrated through tactile experience.

The Paper Mate Capri commercial also fits the pole closely:

- **point**
- **paper**
- **write**
- **refill**

Its focus is the built-in spare refill, the two points, the ink supply, and the practical problem of running out of ink. Again, persuasion is based on explaining a product mechanism.

### Broader material-product demonstrations

The pole is not limited to pens. Other examples show the same discourse pattern across product categories.

The Richard Hudnut home permanent commercial uses:

- **ordinary**
- **prevent**
- **exclusive**
- **hour**
- **happen**

It contrasts an ordinary home permanent with a new improved product and explains how an exclusive ingredient prevents weak surface waves. The commercial is structured around a visible demonstration: “this is what can happen” with an ordinary product, followed by what happens with the improved version.

The Westinghouse dehumidifier commercial explains the product’s function step by step:

> humid air goes in, dry air comes out, moisture condenses into water

The loading words include:

- **part**
- **mean**
- **hour**
- **happen**

This is highly consistent with a product-mechanism discourse: the viewer is taught how the appliance works and what practical problem it solves.

The RCA picture tube commercial also exemplifies this pole. It uses **mean** and **tube**, and explains screen brightness, sharpness, electron guns, particles, quality control, and fit with television sets. The value of the product is established through technical manufacturing claims and component performance.

The Ford example is slightly different but still fits the factor through sensory demonstration. The commercial asks the viewer to evaluate quality by what they can **hear**:

> “you hear a good solid sound”  
> “what you hear and don’t hear is the sound of a Ford”

This broadens the pole from visual/tactile demonstration to sensory proof more generally.

## Decade pattern and diachronic tendency

The mean decade scores are highest in the earliest decades:

| Decade | Mean f7 |
|---|---:|
| 1950s | 0.592 |
| 1960s | 0.495 |
| 1970s | 0.214 |
| 1980s | 0.146 |
| 1990s | 0.120 |
| 2000s | 0.127 |
| 2010s | 0.097 |
| 2020s | 0.068 |

The pole is therefore most strongly associated with the **1950s and 1960s**, especially the 1950s. The high standard deviation in the 1950s also suggests that some commercials from that decade score extremely highly, which is consistent with the supplied examples.

Diachronically, the pattern suggests a gradual weakening of this discourse style over time. Early television commercials often relied on announcer-led demonstrations, product handling, direct explanations, and technical claims about product construction. The positive pole of Factor 7 captures that older advertising mode: the commercial as a small product demonstration or instructional display.

Later decades still have positive mean scores, so the discourse does not disappear entirely. However, its prominence declines, suggesting that this kind of explicit, material, feature-by-feature explanation becomes less central in the verbal style of television commercials after the 1960s.

---

## Summary interpretation

Factor 7 positive marks commercials that foreground **physical products as engineered objects**. These texts explain parts, materials, mechanisms, refills, tubes, points, ingredients, and sensory evidence. They often use a demonstrative style: showing what happens, explaining what a feature means, contrasting ordinary and improved versions, and instructing the consumer what to choose or try.

A concise label for this pole would be:

> **Product-Mechanism Demonstration**

Alternative labels such as **“Material Product Features and Functional Explanation”** or **“Mid-Century Product Instruction and Feature Display”** would also be appropriate, especially given the strong association with 1950s and 1960s television-commercial discourse.

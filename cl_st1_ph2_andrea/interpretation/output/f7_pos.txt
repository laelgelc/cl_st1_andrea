## Factor f7 (positive pole): interpretation

The positive pole of Factor 7 appears to encode a **mid-century discourse of tangible product demonstration, mechanical explanation, and practical product improvement**. It is strongest in the **1950s** and **1960s**, with a clear decline across later decades.

### Suggested labels

1. **“Demonstrated Product Mechanics and Practical Utility”**  
2. **“Material Product Demonstration and Functional Improvement”**  
3. **“Mid-century Hands-on Product Explanation”**  
4. **“Parts, Refills, Ingredients, and Proof-of-Function Discourse”**  
5. **“Concrete Product Features and Demonstrable Benefits”**

The best overall label would be:

> **Demonstrated Product Mechanics and Practical Utility**

This label captures both the lexical loadings and the high-scoring examples: commercials at this pole explain how a product works, name its physical parts, demonstrate its improvement over an “ordinary” alternative, and connect material design to everyday practical benefit.

The pole is clearly driven by the earliest decades:

| 1960s | **0.495** |

| 1980s | 0.146 |

| 2000s | 0.127 |
| 2010s | 0.087 |

The **1950s and 1960s score much higher** than all later decades. The 1950s also have a large standard deviation, suggesting that this pole is not simply “all 1950s advertising”, but is especially associated with a subset of highly scoring early commercials, particularly those built around explicit demonstrations of pens, appliances, cosmetics, radios, home products, and other tangible goods.

Diachronically, the pole represents a style that is **strongly early-television-commercial in character**: explanatory, demonstrative, object-focused, and materially specific. From the 1970s onward the mean scores fall substantially, suggesting that this kind of verbal product explanation becomes less central in the selected commercial subcorpus over time.

## Evidence from the factor loadings

The loading words cluster into several related semantic fields.

### 1. Writing, surface contact, and material artefacts

The highest-loading items include:

- **point** 1.04  
- **paper** 1.01  
- **answer** .88  
- **write** .83  
- **touch** .78  
- **refill** .70  
- **tube** .62  

These words strongly suggest commercials that describe **physical tools and their material operation**, especially writing instruments. The prominence of **point**, **paper**, **write**, **touch**, and **refill** is highly consistent with the high-scoring pen commercials in the excerpts. However, the pattern is broader than pens alone: the vocabulary points to concrete products with parts, surfaces, contact points, refills, and replaceable or functional components.

### 2. Product parts, mechanisms, and technical explanation

Other loading words include:

- **part** .78  
- **prevent** .69  
- **ingredient** .46  
- **dissolve** .40  
- **stop** .37  
- **fit** .47  
- **hour** .48  
- **quick** .33  
- **happen** .42  

These words encode a discourse of **mechanism and causality**: what part of the product does the work, what ingredient causes the effect, what problem is prevented, what happens when the product is used, how quickly it works, and how long it takes. This supports an interpretation centred on **explanatory product-function talk**.

### 3. Demonstrative contrast and product distinctiveness

The pole also includes:

- **ordinary** .76  
- **exclusive** .67  
- **famous** .56  
- **beautiful** .42  
- **choose** .72  

These words are typical of commercials that contrast a new or improved product with an “ordinary” alternative. The product is presented as **exclusive**, **famous**, **beautiful**, or specially chosen because of some material advantage.

### 4. Presenter-style explanation and audience guidance

Words such as:

- **mean** .69  
- **course** .59  
- **folk** .66  
- **hear** .56  
- **seem** .72  
- **tomorrow** .37  
- **something** .32  

suggest a spoken, host-like advertising style: the presenter explains what a feature “means”, invites the audience to see or hear proof, reassures them with conversational markers such as “of course”, and closes with practical calls to action such as “try it tomorrow” or “call tomorrow”.

Together, the loadings indicate a pole concerned with **concrete product attributes, functional explanation, and demonstrable practical benefit**, delivered in a spoken explanatory sales style.

## Evidence from the example excerpts

The excerpts reinforce the same interpretation.

### Pen and writing-instrument commercials

Several of the highest-scoring examples are pen advertisements.

In the Schaefer pen commercial, the loading words include:

- **point**
- **paper**
- **answer**
- **write**
- **part**
- **touch**
- **choose**
- **seem**
- **mean**
- **tube**
- **course**

The advertisement combines emotional discourse about handwriting and personal expression with detailed product mechanics:

> “every word you write with your Schaefer white dot pen lets the you come through”  
> “part of the secret’s in the custom fitted Schaefer point”  
> “only the filling tube touches the ink”

This is a very clear instance of the pole: the product is sold through a combination of **material design**, **personal utility**, and **technical explanation**.

The Parker 51 example similarly foregrounds material improvement:

> “exclusive new electro-polished point”  
> “dissolves all the microscopic burrs and ridges found on ordinary points”  
> “the smoothest you ever touch to paper”

Here, the commercial describes a product part, explains the manufacturing process, contrasts it with “ordinary” points, and translates the mechanism into a sensory benefit.

The Paper Mate Capri commercial also fits the pole strongly:

> “built-in spare refill”  
> “two points, two ink supplies”  
> “ready to write again with a completely new point”

This is not merely a brand-image appeal; it is an explanation of **parts, refills, functional backup, and practical preparedness**.

### Refillable and component-based consumer goods

The Revlon Futurama lipstick case excerpt contains:

- **part**
- **refill**
- **mean**
- **folk**
- **course**

The emphasis is on a permanent case and replaceable refill:

> “all you need ever buy again is a Revlon refill”  
> “That means a saving of 35 cents”

This extends the pole beyond writing instruments. The same discourse applies to cosmetics: the product is explained through **components, replacement, economy, and practical value**.

The Dixie cup excerpt also uses **paper**, **refill**, and **mean**:

> “Dixie dispenser and several refill boxes”  
> “Dixie cups means there’s less of breaking glasses”  
> “more sanitary”

Again, a concrete household product is justified through practicality, hygiene, convenience, and replenishable parts.

### Technical demonstrations and causal explanations

The Richard Hudnut home permanent commercial includes:

- **ordinary**
- **prevent**
- **exclusive**
- **hour**
- **happen**

It contrasts the improved product with an ordinary one:

> “exclusive Richard Hudnut penetrating ingredient”  
> “prevents weak surface waves”  
> “what can happen with an ordinary home permanent”  
> “just one hour”

The discourse is explicitly causal: an ingredient penetrates hair, prevents a problem, and produces a measurable effect in a specified time.

The Westinghouse dehumidifier example uses:

- **part**
- **mean**
- **hour**
- **happen**

It explains the process of removing moisture:

> “what happens to the moisture?”  
> “it condenses into water and drops into this container”  
> “take three gallons of water out of the air in 24 hours”  
> “use it to remove the dampness from any part of your house”

This is classic demonstration copy: problem, mechanism, observable result, household application.

The RCA Victor radio commercial similarly demonstrates durability and sound quality:

> “non-breakable impact case”  
> “tube might jar loose”  
> “means no chipping, no cracking, no breaking”  
> “hear that tone”

The Ford commercial uses **hear** and **course** to make sound into proof of construction quality:

> “you hear a good solid sound”  
> “what you hear and don’t hear is the sound of a Ford”

The product’s material quality is made perceptible through sensory demonstration.

## Discourse encoded at this pole

This positive pole represents commercials that are verbally organised around:

- **physical products as material objects**
- **named product parts and components**
- **mechanisms of operation**
- **refills, tubes, points, ingredients, cases, and fitted parts**
- **demonstrations of what happens when the product is used**
- **contrasts between improved and ordinary versions**
- **claims of prevention, smoothness, durability, speed, sanitation, economy, or reliability**
- **spoken presenter explanation**
- **sensory proof through touching, hearing, seeing, or observing results**

The pole therefore captures a mode of advertising in which the commercial’s persuasive force comes from explaining and demonstrating **how the product works** and why its physical design matters.

---

## Diachronic interpretation

Because the highest scores occur in the **1950s and 1960s**, this pole appears to reflect a strongly **early television advertising style**. These commercials often resemble live product demonstrations or presenter-led sales pitches. They devote substantial verbal space to explaining a product’s features, naming its parts, and showing how those parts solve practical household or personal problems.

The lower scores from the 1970s through the 2020s suggest that this style becomes less prominent over time in the selected verbal subcorpus. Later commercials may still contain product information, but this specific cluster of vocabulary — **point, paper, write, refill, tube, ordinary, exclusive, prevent, ingredient, happen, hour, hear** — is most characteristic of the early decades.

---

## Concise interpretation

Factor f7 positive can be interpreted as:

> **A discourse of mid-century product demonstration, in which commercials explain tangible goods through their parts, materials, mechanisms, refills, ingredients, and observable practical benefits.**

The pole is especially driven by **1950s and 1960s commercials**, with high-scoring examples from pens, cosmetics, home permanents, appliances, radios, cups, and cars. The language is concrete, explanatory, and demonstrative, presenting products as improved material technologies that can be touched, heard, refilled, fitted, or shown to prevent everyday problems.

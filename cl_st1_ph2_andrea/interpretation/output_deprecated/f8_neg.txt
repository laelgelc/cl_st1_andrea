## Interpretation of Factor f8 (negative pole)

### Most plausible discourse dimension label  
**Product-detail, proof, and transaction discourse**

Alternative labels:

1. **Practical consumer-information and sales-offer discourse**  
2. **Feature-proof-and-order advertising discourse**  
3. **Product mechanics, performance, and purchase-terms discourse**  
4. **Concrete product-demonstration and deal-making discourse**

The strongest overall label is **Product-detail, proof, and transaction discourse**, because this pole combines lexical items connected with product facts, functional performance, practical demonstrations, consumer technologies, household goods, vehicles, and explicit buying or ordering language.

Thus, this pole appears to capture a discourse that becomes relatively more salient by the 1990s and then weakens somewhat after that.

## Lexical evidence

The negative pole contains several semantic clusters.

### 1. Product facts, proof, and evaluation

Highly loaded words include:

- **fact**
- **quality**
- **answer**
- **fast**
- **fit**
- **current**

These words point to an advertising style concerned with giving reasons, evidence, specifications, and practical reassurance. The discourse is not simply emotional branding; it often foregrounds what the product does, how well it works, or why it is the rational choice.

This is illustrated by the Oldsmobile excerpt:

> “The results of an independent 100,000-mile test in which the Oldsmobile Achieva incurred lower total costs than either Honda Accord or Toyota Camry.”

Even though the only loading word in that excerpt is **something**, the passage strongly exemplifies the broader factor logic: test results, comparison, cost evidence, and consumer choice.

The DuPont example also fits this proof-oriented discourse:

> “DuPont announced that its energy unit, Conoco, would pioneer the use of new double-hold oil tankers in order to safeguard the environment.”

Here the loading word is **order**, but the discourse is institutional, factual, and justificatory: a company presents a technical action as evidence of responsibility.

---

### 2. Product mechanisms, machines, and functional processes

Important loadings include:

- **machine**
- **television**
- **screen**
- **stereo**
- **video**
- **current**
- **dish**
- **kitchen**

These words suggest concrete product environments and mechanisms: appliances, electronics, screens, media devices, household spaces, and technical components. The pole is therefore associated with commercials that talk about products as functioning objects within everyday consumer life.

The Nice and Soft tissue commercial is a clear example:

> “This is our fluffing machine. The printing press. Our paper softener. The perfume department.”

The loading word **machine** is central here. The ad takes the audience into a production process and explains how the product is made, or more precisely, what unnecessary features are not added. This is a classic product-mechanics discourse: the ad creates trust by showing the material process behind the commodity.

The Subaru excerpt similarly lists product features:

> “power windows for your fingers, AM-FM stereo cassette for your ears, comfortable seats for your bum, anti-lock brakes for your feet…”

The loading word is **something**, but the surrounding language is strongly feature-based. The product is broken down into functions, parts, and bodily benefits.

---

### 3. Cleaning, household, and problem-removal discourse

The loading list includes:

- **suds**
- **dirty**
- **detergent**
- **kitchen**
- **rid**
- **dish**

This cluster points to a household-cleaning and problem-solution register. Although the provided examples do not strongly illustrate the detergent/cleaning subcluster, the loadings show that across the full verbal subcorpus this pole includes commercials organised around dirt, cleaning, removal, kitchen work, and household product efficacy.

This matters because the factor loadings represent the whole analysed subcorpus, not only the small set of excerpts. The examples foreground 1990s cars, cards, games, tissue, and direct-response products, but the lexical structure of the pole is broader and includes domestic product demonstration.

---

### 4. Buying, ordering, leasing, and deal-making

Another strong cluster includes:

- **order**
- **deal**
- **sell**
- **lease**
- **month**
- **hold**

This gives the pole a transactional character. The discourse is not only about what products are; it is also about how to acquire them, what they cost, and what kind of offer is available.

The Instant White excerpt is the clearest example:

> “Call now… That’s both sizes for only $19.95… So order now.”

The loading word **order** directly marks the commercial as direct-response sales discourse. It moves from problem identification — whiter teeth — to demonstration — “you can really see the difference” — to proof — “proven both safe and effective” — to transaction — “Call now” and “order now.”

The Sega price-comparison example also participates in deal discourse:

> “Right now, it costs up to $10 more to play certain games on Super Nintendo than to play the same games on Sega Genesis.”

Although the loading word is **mind**, the excerpt is structured around price comparison and value.

---

### 5. Mental framing and conversational pitch

Several loaded words are not product nouns but interactional or cognitive items:

- **something**
- **mind**
- **hear**
- **answer**
- **okay**

These words suggest a colloquial, mentally engaging, sometimes playful advertising voice. The examples show this clearly.

In the Little Caesars commercial:

> “The mind reacts to two pizzas for $5.99 with shock, temporary delusion, confusion, and finally, acceptance.”

The loading word **mind** is not incidental: the product offer is framed as a psychological reaction. The ad turns a price promotion into a comic mental event.

In the Pitfall game commercial:

> “Pitfall, the mind adventure for Sega Genesis and Super Nintendo Entertainment Systems…”

Again, **mind** is used to position the product as cognitively stimulating.

In the Sega example:

> “Which is fine, if you don’t mind being sucked dry.”

Here **mind** appears in a sarcastic, competitive sales pitch. The discourse is practical — price comparison — but delivered through youthful, confrontational humour.

The gaming excerpt with **okay** also shows a casual, aggressive 1990s tone:

> “Okay, we’ve got good news and bad news. The good news is, these games kick serious butt. The bad news is, you’re the butt.”

This suggests that the 1990s contribution to the pole is not merely informational. It is often a compressed, witty, colloquial form of product-and-deal discourse.

## Role of the examples

The examples are all from the **1990s**, and most contain only one loading word each: **mind**, **something**, **order**, **machine**, or **okay**. On their own, the examples might suggest a 1990s style of quick, ironic, conversational pitching. However, the full loading list broadens the interpretation.

Taken together, the examples show commercials that foreground:

- access and utility: MasterCard cash access;
- product features: Subaru Legacy;
- institutional action and technical responsibility: DuPont/Conoco;
- game experience and price competition: Pitfall, Sega;
- psychological response to a deal: Little Caesars;
- test-based product comparison: Oldsmobile;
- production-process explanation: Nice and Soft;
- direct-response ordering: Instant White.

These are diverse product categories, but they share a practical orientation: the viewer is told what the product does, why it works, what makes it better, how it is made, how much it costs, or how to get it.

---

## Proposed interpretation

Factor f8 negative represents a discourse of **practical product specification, evidence, and consumer transaction**. It is associated with commercials that emphasise product facts, mechanisms, features, demonstrations, tests, household or technical utility, buying terms, deals, leasing, selling, and ordering. The discourse often presents the product as a concrete solution to a practical problem or as a rationally defensible purchase.

The pole is especially linked to the **1990s** in the supplied data. In that decade, this discourse often appears in a compact, self-conscious, conversational style, using words such as **mind**, **something**, **okay**, and **order** to frame product information through humour, direct address, or call-to-action sales language.

A concise label would therefore be:

> **Product-detail, proof, and transaction discourse**

Other acceptable labels would be:

> **Practical consumer-information and sales-offer discourse**  
> **Feature-proof-and-order advertising discourse**  
> **Concrete product-demonstration and deal-making discourse**

## Factor f1 (negative): interpretation as a discourse dimension

This pole appears to encode a **mid-century visual discourse of glamorous product display, feminine grooming, foamy cleanliness, and scenic leisure**. It is most strongly associated with the **1950s**, with some continuation into the **1960s**, and it becomes progressively less characteristic across later decades.

Because this is the **negative pole**, the relevant decade scores are the **lowest mean scores**. The lowest means are:

- **1950s:** 0.81  
- **1960s:** 1.05  
- then a gradual rise through the 1970s–2020s, reaching **8.93 in the 2020s**

The diachronic pattern therefore suggests that this pole is driven especially by **early television-commercial visual styles**, particularly the 1950s examples.

---

## Main discourses encoded at this pole

### 1. Feminine elegance, fashion display, and grooming

The strongest loading item is **strapless** (-.86), followed by terms such as **bodice**, **pearl**, **tailored**, **vanity**, **groom**, **gown**, **neckline**, **high-waisted**, **ruffle**, **wide-brimmed**, **hairstyle**, and **well-dressed**. These point to a visual discourse centred on women’s appearance, clothing construction, beauty routines, and polished self-presentation.

This is strongly confirmed in the examples:

- swimsuit modelling with **strapless** suits, structured **bodices**, **high-waisted** garments, and resort fashion;
- women at **vanities**, applying powder, cleansing the face, or displaying hair products;
- elegant presenters wearing **pearls**, formal dresses, shawls, or tailored coats;
- Cinderella transformed into a woman in a **strapless** gown after the cleaning product works its magic.

This pole therefore captures a visual world where products are embedded in ideals of feminine poise, refinement, bodily presentation, and domestic or beauty-related self-care.

---

### 2. Foamy cleanliness, bathing, polishing, and product transformation

A second major lexical cluster concerns cleansing and visible product efficacy: **suds**, **foamy**, **lather**, **deodorant**, **powder**, **porcelain**, **wax**, **gleam**, **shimmer**, **glitter**, and **star-shaped**. These words suggest commercials where cleanliness is made visually dramatic through bubbles, foam, sparkle, shine, and transformation.

The examples give this cluster substantial support:

- Bab-O turns Cinderella’s grimy sink into a shining clean surface through **suds**, **foam**, **glitter**, and a **magical** fairy-tale intervention.
- Ajax uses animated elves, cleanser powder, **foamy** scrubbing, and star-like sparkles on bathroom surfaces.
- Dial soap shows bathing, **lather**, **suds**, deodorant soap, and a clean beauty routine.
- Cosmetics and hair products are shown through wiping, grooming, setting, and restoring appearance.

The discourse is not merely “cleaning” in a functional sense. It is **cleanliness as spectacle**: dirt, sagging hair, body odour, dull surfaces, or domestic grime are visually overcome by foam, sparkle, laboratory-like graphics, or magical intervention.

---

### 3. Scenic leisure, travel, water, and resort imagery

Another prominent lexical group involves water, travel, and leisure settings: **shoreline**, **pond**, **sailboat**, **mast**, **reed**, **sail**, **ocean**, **vessel**, **poolside**, **fin**, and related period mobility terms such as **two-tone**, **whitewall**, **streamlined**, and **exhaust**.

This cluster is also visible in the examples:

- swimsuit commercials unfold around poolside terraces, cabanas, seawalls, water, and distant **shorelines**;
- Chevrolet and Hertz commercials present scenic travel through mountains, coastlines, bridges, beaches, and open roads;
- boating and fishing imagery appears with water spray, boats, fins, and shorelines;
- Greyhound and car commercials present travel as comfortable, scenic, and aspirational.

This suggests a discourse of **mid-century leisure mobility**: products and services are visually associated with beaches, resort spaces, road trips, boats, convertibles, and picturesque landscapes.

---

### 4. Theatrical, magical, and ornamental commercial staging

The loadings also include words such as **magical**, **carriage**, **kiss**, **glitter**, **scallop**, **gleam**, **shimmer**, **star-shaped**, **handwritten**, **gramophone**, **phonograph**, and **RCA**. These point toward older commercial aesthetics involving theatrical sets, fantasy scenarios, musical performance, formal props, and decorative display.

The excerpts illustrate this clearly:

- the Bab-O commercial uses a Cinderella fantasy, fairy queen, sparkling can, horse-drawn **carriage**, prince, and **kiss**;
- the Chevrolet commercial features a stage-like singer in a formal **bodice** performing before scenic car imagery;
- product displays often use glowing rings, abstract patterns, oversized packages, mascots, or animated signs;
- beauty and grooming products are staged in dressing-room or vanity settings.

This pole therefore includes a discourse of **the product as a theatrical object**: the commercial visual field is decorative, performative, and often fantastical.

## Proposed labels for f1 negative

### Best label  
**“Mid-century glamour, grooming, and foamy cleanliness spectacle”**

This label captures the strongest recurring elements: early-period visual style, women’s fashion and grooming, soap/cleanser foam, sparkle, and staged transformation.

### Alternative labels

1. **“Vintage feminine elegance and product-transformation display”**  
   Emphasises the women’s fashion, vanity, pearls, bodices, grooming, and magical or demonstrative product effects.

2. **“1950s glamour-cleanliness and leisure display”**  
   Highlights the decade that drives the pole while also including cleaning, beauty, resort, travel, and scenic mobility imagery.

3. **“Ornamental mid-century advertising spectacle”**  
   Focuses on the broader visual style: theatrical staging, sparkle, fantasy, formal dress, scenic travel, and product displays.

4. **“Foam, fashion, and scenic leisure in early television commercials”**  
   More descriptive and corpus-specific, foregrounding the three major lexical fields: cleansing foam, fashion/grooming, and water/travel leisure.

---

## Summary interpretation

Factor f1 negative is best understood as a historically early commercial-visual discourse, driven especially by the **1950s** and secondarily by the **1960s**. It combines **feminine glamour**, **beauty and grooming routines**, **foamy domestic or bodily cleanliness**, **sparkling product transformation**, and **scenic leisure or travel imagery**. The pole reflects a mid-century television-advertising visual world of polished women, shiny surfaces, theatrical product demonstrations, resort settings, cars, boats, beaches, and magical or decorative effects.

## Interpretation of Factor f1 (negative pole)

This pole appears to encode a discourse of **mid-century, product-centred, monochrome television advertising**, especially commercials organised around **static product displays, brand end cards, domestic appliances, packaged goods, and formal advertising claims**. It is most strongly associated with the **1950s**, with some weaker association with other earlier decades, and it becomes much less characteristic of the post-2000 visual-commercial subcorpus.

### Possible labels for f1 negative

1. **“Monochrome Product Display and Brand Sign-off”**  
2. **“Mid-Century Static Advertising Display”**  
3. **“Archival Domestic Product Demonstration”**  
4. **“Print-like Packaged-Goods Commercial Visuality”**  
5. **“Early Television Product-and-Logo Presentation”**

The strongest label is probably:

> **Monochrome Product Display and Brand Sign-off**

because the pole combines black-and-white/archival visual features with repeated product packaging, brand logos, end cards, domestic appliances, and explicit advertising display formats.

The **1950s are clearly the strongest decade for this pole**, and all ten illustrative excerpts come from 1950s commercials. The 1970s and 1980s also score relatively low, suggesting that some of the pole’s visual vocabulary may continue in later traditional advertising forms, but the main discourse is driven by early television-commercial aesthetics.

Diachronically, there is a clear shift away from this pole after the 1990s. The 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s have much higher scores on Factor 1 and therefore are much less associated with this negative pole. This suggests that the pole captures a historically earlier visual-commercial mode: static, object-focused, monochrome or archival, and organized around brand presentation rather than dynamic lifestyle or cinematic visual storytelling.

## Lexical evidence from the loadings

The loading words strongly support an interpretation centred on **older television technology, monochrome visual style, domestic consumer goods, and explicit advertising display**.

### 1. Early television, radio, and RCA/Victor branding

Highly negative loadings include:

- **rca**
- **victor**
- **gramophone**
- **master**
- **listen**
- **record**
- **radio**
- **tv**
- **broadcast**

These words point directly to early broadcast media and legacy home-entertainment brands. The repeated RCA Victor vocabulary is especially important: *RCA*, *Victor*, *gramophone*, and *master* evoke the iconic “His Master’s Voice” end-card imagery. This is not simply a generic consumer-product discourse; it is specifically tied to early television’s relationship with older audio-visual technologies such as radio, records, gramophones, and television sets.

### 2. Monochrome and archival visual texture

Other loadings point to the visual materiality of old television footage:

- **monochrome**
- **photographic**
- **grain**
- **underexpose**
- **shadowy**
- **bluish**
- **picture**
- **closing**

These terms suggest descriptions of degraded, black-and-white, high-contrast, or archival footage. The examples repeatedly mention black-and-white presentation, dark backgrounds, faint image noise, scan-line interference, and static closing cards. The pole therefore captures not only what is advertised, but also the visual texture of the advertisements as historical broadcast artefacts.

### 3. Product packaging, size, and physical object display

A major lexical cluster concerns objects as physical products:

- **size**
- **boxy**
- **thick**
- **stick**
- **nozzle**
- **pouch**
- **substance**
- **mixture**
- **application**
- **physical**
- **limited**

These words suggest close visual attention to product form, packaging, containers, applicators, and material properties. The excerpts include boxed soap, a vitamin package, a pen, a cigarette pack, a dishwashing-liquid bottle, a small appliance, and electronic devices. The visual discourse is therefore highly object-centred: commercials show products as things to be inspected, displayed, held, placed on counters, or presented in end cards.

### 4. Domestic consumer goods and household settings

The loadings also include domestic and household vocabulary:

- **appliance**
- **domestic**
- **living**
- **sofa**
- **armchair**
- **décor**
- **blanket**
- **sock**
- **soap**
- **deodorant**
- **tooth**
- **hotel**

These words point toward household consumption and everyday domestic environments. In the examples, products are placed in kitchens, living rooms, desks, and home-electronics settings. The domestic world is represented through appliances, furniture, counters, shelves, packages, and household-use scenes.

### 5. Advertising claims and display conventions

Finally, the pole includes overtly promotional vocabulary:

- **advertising**
- **claim**
- **attention**
- **represent**
- **identification**
- **closing**
- **limited**
- **participate**

These terms match the examples’ emphasis on end cards, slogans, promotional signage, product claims, and text-heavy layouts. The visual style often resembles print advertising transferred to television: a product is centered, surrounded by large text, slogans, or offer information.

## Evidence from the example excerpts

The examples give a consistent picture of the pole as one of **static, product-centred, black-and-white commercial presentation**.

### RCA Victor and early home electronics

Several excerpts are dominated by RCA Victor imagery.

In **f1_neg_001**, the commercial ends with a static RCA Victor card:

- loading words: **victor**, **master**
- visual features: black-and-white studio setup, television set, cabinet-style home electronic unit, RCA Victor end card, dog listening to a gramophone, “His Master’s Voice”

This example strongly illustrates the media-technology cluster in the loadings: **RCA**, **Victor**, **gramophone**, **master**, **listen**, **record**, **radio**, and **tv**.

Similarly, **f1_neg_005** ends with an RCA Victor promotional card:

- loading words: **victor**, **master**
- visual features: “FIRST IN BLACK AND WHITE TELEVISION,” “FIRST IN COMPATIBLE COLOR TELEVISION,” RCA logo, dog listening to gramophone, “HIS MASTER’S VOICE”

These examples show that the pole is partly driven by early TV’s self-promotional or technology-brand discourse, where the commercial’s final visual authority comes from a formal corporate logo and slogan.

### Static product packaging and promotional text

Other examples involve product packaging and large advertising text.

In **f1_neg_004**, the RYBUTOL commercial uses a static advertising display:

- loading words: **size**, **beginning**, **advertising**
- visual features: boxed product, “HIGH POTENCY,” “FREE!”, “$1.98 bottle,” “WITH EVERY PURCHASE OF 100 SIZE RYBUTOL,” point-of-sale style arrangement, large overlapping promotional text

This closely matches the loading vocabulary around **advertising**, **claim**, **size**, **limited**, and product display. The visual discourse is not cinematic or narrative; it is a graphic sales display.

In **f1_neg_003**, the Dial soap commercial ends with a boxed product in a spotlight-like frame:

- loading words: **deodorant**, **size**, **infer**, **uncluttered**
- visual features: black-and-white abstract background, boxed soap, “DEODORANT BATH & BEAUTY SOAP,” dark uncluttered display, centered product

This example illustrates the pole’s focus on packaged goods, physical product identity, and clean final product presentation.

In **f1_neg_010**, the Joy commercial ends with a bottle and text graphic:

- loading word: **advertising**
- visual features: dishwashing close-up followed by final product-and-text card, bottle labeled “Joy,” large stylized words around the product

Again, the product is visually anchored by a final advertising card.

### Product use followed by end-card closure

Several examples have a simple structure: opening product image or demonstration, followed by a final logo, slogan, or product display.

In **f1_neg_006**, a man stands before a Kellogg’s sign, then the commercial moves to a retail display and a closing Dixie Cup title card:

- loading words: **beginning**, **infer**
- visual features: presenter, product shelves, “Dixie Cup,” “America’s No. 1 Paper Cup”

In **f1_neg_007**, a pen is shown first as an object and then in use:

- loading word: **beginning**
- visual features: dark product close-up, hand writing with pen, product-use demonstration

In **f1_neg_009**, a cigarette pack appears in a composed final product card:

- loading words: **beginning**, **represent**
- visual features: “BELAIR” package, seascape background, “Breathe Easy,” “Smoke Clean”

Across these excerpts, the commercials often rely on **sampled beginning and final frames**, and the word **beginning** itself loads on the pole. This may reflect a descriptive pattern in the visual subcorpus, but it also corresponds to a commercial form in which the opening and closing images are especially salient: product introduction, demonstration, and final branded sign-off.

## Discourse encoded by the pole

This pole encodes a discourse in which the television commercial is treated as a **visual product presentation**. The product, package, appliance, logo, or claim is the main object of attention. The visual world is often sparse, staged, monochrome, and display-oriented.

The pole’s discourse includes several overlapping elements:

### 1. Product as physical object

Products are described as visible, tangible things: boxes, bottles, appliances, pens, soap packages, cigarette packs, cup stacks, and electronic devices. The visual emphasis falls on their **shape, size, packaging, label, position, and display**.

### 2. Advertising as signage

The commercials frequently include large written text, slogans, claims, prices, free offers, and brand names. The visual mode resembles print advertising or point-of-sale display: text and product image coexist within a static frame.

### 3. Domestic modernity

Many loading words point toward household goods and domestic environments: appliances, soap, deodorant, furniture, living rooms, kitchen counters, and home electronics. The discourse is one of mid-century consumer modernity: products are shown as useful, modern, and appropriate for the home.

### 4. Broadcast-era authority

The RCA Victor examples show a discourse of technological authority and brand prestige. The brand end card, gramophone image, and “His Master’s Voice” slogan give the commercials a formal, corporate, broadcast-era identity.

### 5. Archival monochrome aesthetics

The pole is also marked by black-and-white imagery, dark backgrounds, grain, underexposure, and static end cards. The visual descriptions repeatedly foreground the material condition of old television footage.

---

## Summary interpretation

Factor f1 negative is best interpreted as a discourse of:

> **Monochrome Product Display and Brand Sign-off**

It is driven especially by the **1950s**, with some broader association with earlier or traditional advertising modes. The lexical loadings foreground RCA Victor, gramophones, radio/TV, monochrome imagery, domestic appliances, packaged goods, advertising claims, and static displays. The examples show black-and-white commercials built around products, packages, household goods, end cards, slogans, logos, and formal brand presentation.

Diachronically, the pole appears to represent an older visual-commercial style that is strongest in the 1950s and declines markedly in the post-2000 decades.

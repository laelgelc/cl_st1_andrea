## Interpretation of Factor f2 (negative pole)

The negative pole of Factor 2 appears to encode a discourse of **staged product demonstration, domestic problem-solving, and explicit visual merchandising**. It is especially associated with commercials whose visual descriptions focus on products being handled, displayed, labelled, demonstrated, arranged on shelves or counters, and connected to practical household or consumer needs.

### Possible labels for this pole

1. **“Staged Product Demonstration and Visual Merchandising”**  
2. **“Domestic Problem-Solution Product Display”**  
3. **“Presenter-Led Demonstrative Selling”**  
4. **“Instructional Household Consumer Display”**  
5. **“Product Proof through Props, Captions, and Package Close-Ups”**

The strongest overall label would be:

> **Staged Product Demonstration and Visual Merchandising**

because it captures both the lexical loadings and the example texts: objects, packages, captions, shelves, domestic interiors, demonstrations, product assortments, and direct visual evidence of usefulness.

---

## Decades driving the pole

For a negative pole, the lowest mean Factor 2 scores are most relevant. The lowest decade score is:

- **1950s: 4.12**

The next-lowest scores are:

- **1970s: 4.96**
- **1980s: 5.02**
- **2010s: 5.77**

The examples are overwhelmingly from the **1950s**, and the 1950s have the lowest mean score, so this pole is primarily driven by **1950s visual-commercial style**. The 1970s and 1980s may retain some related visual-discourse features, but the pole is most clearly anchored in early television advertising.

Diachronically, the pattern suggests that this discourse is not simply “old advertising” in a linear sense, because the 1960s do not score especially low. Rather, the pole seems to capture a particular visual format that is especially prominent in the 1950s: direct product demonstration, package display, household-use scenarios, on-screen claims, and presenter-style visual explanation.

These words point to commercials where the viewer is shown products, labels, offers, categories, captions, shelves, selections, or sales information. This fits a visual discourse of explicit selling, where the commercial does not merely create mood but shows the product as an object to be identified, selected, bought, used, or compared.

The examples repeatedly involve product arrays: RCA batteries in different sizes, Campbell’s soup cans in a line, Stopette bottles in a display, Dixie cup refill boxes, Bulova watches in cases, and Deep Magic bottles in a special-price pack.

This is highly visible in the examples: kitchens with open shelving, windowsills, refrigerators, cabinets, sinks, dining tables, high-backed chairs, bathroom porcelain, dressing tables, and living-room furniture.

## Evidence from the examples

The example excerpts strongly support the interpretation of this pole as demonstrative, domestic, and product-centred.

### Gold Medal flour: product-result demonstration

The Gold Medal example includes a domestic kitchen, a baking result, on-screen lettering, and a final package-and-cake display. Loading words such as **shelving** and **high-backed** appear, but the larger discourse is visual proof: the flour produces a successful cake, and the woman is presented as a “Gold Medal Baking Expert.”

This is classic product-demonstration advertising: domestic setting, product package, result display, and evaluative visual claim.

### RCA batteries: technical handling and retail assortment

The RCA example is almost entirely built around object manipulation and product display. Hands open a radio, install a battery, arrange wires, and then show an **assortment** of battery packages. The loading word **assortment** appears directly in the excerpt.

This illustrates the technical-demonstrative side of the pole: the product is shown as a practical component with different sizes, packaging, and use contexts.

### Dixie Cups: sanitary household convenience with captions

The Dixie example includes a kitchen setting, a refrigerator, a cup dispenser, product close-ups, shelf displays, and bold captions: “NO GLASSES TO WASH,” “NO BROKEN GLASSES,” “MORE SANITARY.” The loading word **caption** appears directly.

This is a clear case of explicit visual merchandising: the product benefit is not implied but written onto the screen and demonstrated through household use.

### Campbell’s soup: feeding, family, cans, and product lineup

The Campbell’s example contains loading words **windowsill**, **bacon**, and **feed**. It shows a mother solving the problem of feeding children, followed by soup close-ups, eating scenes, and a final shelf display of Campbell’s cans.

This example shows the domestic problem-solution discourse especially well: household pressure, product idea, children fed, product range displayed.

### Stopette: object demonstration, bathroom setting, and package display

The Stopette example includes loading words **porcelain** and **high-backed**. It demonstrates the spray mechanism, shows the bottle dropped into a porcelain sink, presents package displays, and uses handwritten-style on-screen wording such as “Poof!”

The commercial uses visual proof: the bottle sprays, does not break, is clean to handle, and is available in displays. Again, the pole is product-centred and demonstrative.

### Beauty and grooming examples: vanity, product close-ups, price displays

The Bulova, Lady Schick, Gossamer, Deep Magic, and Serutan examples have few or no listed loading words, but they strongly align with the discourse suggested by the loadings. They feature:

- presenters,
- close-ups of packages,
- product names,
- price cards,
- grooming demonstrations,
- product cases,
- model names,
- special offers,
- final display shots.

These examples show that the pole is not limited to food or household cleaning. It also covers beauty, health, jewellery, and personal-care advertising when the visual style is demonstrative and display-oriented.

## Discourse dimension represented by the pole

This pole represents a visual-commercial discourse in which the advertisement functions as a **showroom, demonstration counter, kitchen lesson, bathroom test, or retail shelf**. The product is repeatedly materialised as a visible object: a can, bottle, package, battery, watch, shaver, cup, dispenser, or carton. The viewer is invited to inspect it, see it used, read its claims, and observe its practical result.

The pole is therefore associated with:

- direct product presentation;
- package and label visibility;
- close-ups of branded objects;
- staged domestic interiors;
- practical household or grooming problems;
- on-screen captions and sales claims;
- demonstrations by presenters or hands;
- product assortments and shelf displays;
- visual proof of function, convenience, or reliability.

The 1950s examples make this especially clear. They often move through a sequence such as:

1. introduce domestic or consumer situation;
2. show product or package;
3. demonstrate use;
4. add caption, price, or claim;
5. end on a product lineup or package close-up.

This makes the pole a discourse of **explicit consumer instruction and visual proof**.

---

## Recommended label

The best concise label is:

> **Staged Product Demonstration and Visual Merchandising**

Alternative labels, depending on analytical emphasis, include:

- **Domestic Product Demonstration**
- **Presenter-Led Consumer Instruction**
- **Household Problem-Solution Display**
- **Product Close-Up and Package-Display Advertising**
- **Early Television Demonstrative Selling**

The main label is preferable because it accommodates both major components of the pole: the **demonstration of product use** and the **visual merchandising of products as purchasable objects**.

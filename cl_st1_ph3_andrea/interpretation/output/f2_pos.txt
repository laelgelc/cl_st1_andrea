## Interpretation of Factor f2 positive pole

Factor f2 positive appears to encode a visual discourse of **vehicular mobility, road/off-road performance, and outdoor terrain traversal**. High-scoring texts at this pole foreground vehicles, wheels, tyres, steering, bodywork, roads, rough ground, weather, and landscape. The pole is especially driven by **1960s commercials**, which have by far the highest mean score, suggesting a strong mid-century visual style centred on cars, trucks, tyres, outdoor movement, and demonstrations of mechanical capability.

### Possible labels for this pole

1. **“Outdoor Vehicular Performance and Terrain Mobility”**  
2. **“Road, Wheel, and Off-Road Demonstration Discourse”**  
3. **“Automotive Action in Rugged Landscapes”**  
4. **“Traction, Travel, and Vehicle Capability”**  
5. **“Mid-Century Motoring Adventure Visuality”**

The strongest label is probably:

> **Outdoor Vehicular Performance and Terrain Mobility**

because it captures both the dominant lexical pattern and the recurring visual scenarios in the examples: vehicles moving through paved roads, mud, snow, dust, mountains, rural paths, race courses, bridges, and rough outdoor landscapes.

## Evidence from the high-scoring examples

The examples strongly reinforce the lexical interpretation.

### Firestone tyre montage: wheels, surfaces, and traction

The highest-scoring example is a Firestone commercial. It is almost a concentrated version of the pole. It includes:

- fire trucks on streets,
- boots on pavement,
- tractors in rough soil,
- cars beside water,
- industrial trucks on sandy ground,
- bicycles and wagons,
- aircraft landing gear,
- lawn mowers,
- bulldozers,
- vehicles in mud,
- tyres throwing rain spray and dust.

The loading words in this example include **paved, rural, bodywork, dusty, rough, boot, drive, pavement, muddy, haze, whitewall, ground, underside, truck, grass, vehicle, path, outdoor, bicycle, heavy, pass, push, roll**.

This excerpt shows the pole’s core discourse very clearly: **the visual demonstration of wheels and tyres as enabling mobility across work, leisure, road, rain, mud, dust, grass, pavement, and industrial terrain**. The product is not simply displayed; it is proved through repeated scenes of movement and surface contact.

- dashboards,
- badges,

The “Noah’s Ark” station wagon example adds a utility dimension: the wagon is shown in a rocky outdoor setting, loading animals into its rear cargo space. The ranger/bear example shows a Rambler Rebel descending dusty mountain roads and later crossing a paved highway bridge. This is an especially strong instance of the proposed label “Outdoor Vehicular Performance and Terrain Mobility”: the commercial’s visuals are structured around showing what the vehicle can do in difficult outdoor conditions.

### Snow tyre example: traction as visual proof

The Gulf snow tyre example gives the same discourse a winter variant. A sedan is stuck in deep snow; a new tyre is installed; the tyre bites into the snow, throws it aside, and enables the car to move forward. Loading words include **steering, drive, whitewall, ground, throw, snow, truck, sedan, snowy, winter, path, heavy, pass, push, roll**.

This example demonstrates that the pole is not limited to off-road adventure. It also covers **product demonstrations of mechanical reliability, grip, safety, and traction**, especially when those qualities are visualised through movement across difficult surfaces.

### Racing and beach examples: broader kinetic outdoor action

Not every example is a conventional car advertisement. The Camel racing commercial uses the race track, sports cars, steering wheels, bodywork, pavement, course, grass, rush, trail, and rolling movement. It contributes a related discourse of **speed, masculine leisure, competition, and outdoor kinetic performance**.

The beach-ball boys’ clothing commercial is less automotive, but it shares several pole features: **sun, sky, shadow, silhouette, pace, pass, push, roll, balance, broad, gather**. It suggests that the factor can also capture outdoor action scenes where bodies or objects move dynamically through sunlit space. However, the overall lexical loadings and the majority of examples show that this broader kinetic outdoor imagery is dominated by vehicular and road/off-road contexts.

## Decade pattern and diachronic tendency

The decade means are:

- 1950s: 4.12  
- 1960s: 7.65  
- 1970s: 4.96  
- 1980s: 5.02  
- 1990s: 6.67  
- 2000s: 6.91  
- 2010s: 5.77  
- 2020s: 6.43

The **1960s score highest by a clear margin**, and all of the supplied high-scoring examples come from the 1960s. This strongly suggests that the pole is driven by a 1960s commercial visual idiom in which cars, tyres, fuel, trucks, racing, roads, and outdoor capability were central advertising resources.

The 1960s examples often combine:

- black-and-white or high-contrast imagery,
- cars in rural or rugged environments,
- close-ups of chrome, badges, steering wheels, tyres, and grilles,
- demonstrations of traction, cargo space, speed, or handling,
- masculine or adventurous scenarios: explorers, rangers, racing drivers, off-roaders, mountain roads,
- landscape as proof of product strength.

However, the factor is not exclusively 1960s. The next strongest decades are the **2000s, 1990s, and 2020s**, which suggests that this discourse recurs later, probably through SUV, truck, outdoor-lifestyle, safety, weather, and mobility advertising. The presence of loading words such as **suv, pickup, trailer, alloy, sporty, urban, safety, fine-print, disclaimer, model**, and **environment** points to later automotive-advertising conventions as well. Thus the pole seems to capture a recurring visual-commercial repertoire, but one especially concentrated in the 1960s.

Diachronically, the pattern suggests:

1. **A strong 1960s peak**, associated with mid-century automobile culture and visual demonstrations of vehicle capability.  
2. **A moderate decline in the 1970s and 1980s**, where this style is less dominant in the balanced sample.  
3. **A revival or persistence from the 1990s onward**, likely connected with SUV/truck advertising, outdoor lifestyle branding, road-safety imagery, and cinematic vehicle shots.  
4. **Continued relevance in the 2020s**, though not as dominant as in the 1960s.

## Proposed interpretation

This positive pole represents a discourse of **vehicles as agents of movement, control, traction, and capability in outdoor space**. Commercial visuals at this pole turn roads, snow, mud, dust, mountains, fields, tracks, and rural landscapes into testing grounds. The advertised product is often validated by showing wheels turning, tyres gripping, cars passing, trucks hauling, vehicles climbing, steering wheels controlled, and bodywork moving through visually challenging environments.

The pole is therefore best understood as:

> **Outdoor Vehicular Performance and Terrain Mobility**

It encodes a commercial visual style in which **mobility is made persuasive through action**: cars, trucks, tyres, and related products are shown moving through demanding landscapes, with dust, snow, mud, glare, silhouettes, and rough ground serving as visual evidence of strength, reliability, speed, and practical usefulness.

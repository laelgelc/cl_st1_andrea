## Interpretation of Factor f3 negative pole

The negative pole of Factor 3 appears to encode a **recent/contemporary visual-commercial discourse of bright, colour-coded lifestyle demonstration**, centred on ordinary people, styled bodies, domestic or everyday settings, product problem-solving, and polished brand graphics.

Although the mean decade scores are all positive, the **lowest means** occur in the most recent decades:

- **2010s: 0.019** — lowest and represented by all high-scoring negative excerpts  
- **2020s: 0.117**
- **2000s: 0.262**
- **1990s: 0.864**

This suggests that the pole is diachronically associated with **late 20th- and especially 21st-century visual advertising styles**, with the 2010s particularly prominent in the examples. The steady decline from the 1950s to the 2010s indicates that this discourse becomes increasingly characteristic of later decades.

---

## Main discourse encoded at this pole

### A bright, contemporary lifestyle-and-demonstration advertising discourse

The strongest loading word is **bright** (-.48), and the pole is saturated with other light, colour, and surface terms:

- **sunlit, sunny, golden, orange, blue, teal, mint-green, crystal, icy, cold**
- also material or product-surface terms such as **stainless-steel**, **drip**, **tomato**, **tongue**

These loadings point to a visual style organised around **high-key lighting, clean surfaces, saturated brand colours, and visually legible product environments**. The excerpts strongly support this. Many open in or repeatedly return to bright, polished settings:

- a **sunny suburban driveway** and **bright office lobby** in the State Farm example;
- a **bright white kitchen**, bedrooms, garage, and poolside settings in the Noom testimonial;
- a **bright white ballet studio** and golden theatrical lighting in the Under Armour/Misty Copeland ad;
- a **bright gym**, **bright bathroom**, **sunlit kitchen**, **bright classroom**, and glowing urban energy graphics in the other examples.

The colour terms are not incidental. **Orange** appears repeatedly in relation to brand palettes, logos, graphic screens, spills, or visual accents: FedEx purple/orange, AT&T orange text, Kayak orange blocks, Mr. Clean’s orange mess, ExxonMobil’s orange/pink light trails. **Mint-green**, **teal**, **blue**, and **golden** similarly index a contemporary visual vocabulary of clean design, app-like graphics, wellness branding, transit-energy visuals, and aspirational lighting.

A suitable label for this pole would therefore be:

> **“Bright contemporary lifestyle demonstration”**

---

## Embodied lifestyle display

A second major component of the pole is its attention to **people as styled, visible bodies**. The loadings include many words referring to appearance, clothing, hair, and physicality:

- **bun, ponytail, tousle**
- **short-sleeve, long-sleeve, pant, leggings, strapless, button-up**
- **beard, goatee**
- **athletic, workout, graceful, powerful**
- **tongue**

The examples strongly reinforce this embodied orientation. They describe bodies in detail: clothing, hair, posture, build, facial expression, and movement. For example:

- the Noom ad introduces multiple named users in specific outfits and domestic spaces;
- the Misty Copeland ad focuses intensely on the dancer’s **bun**, muscles, pointe shoes, legs, shoulders, and controlled movement;
- the Planet Fitness ad contrasts a hyper-muscular gym body with a regular employee;
- the Bounty ad features an **athletic** Olympic hockey player moving through a home;
- the Mr. Clean ad foregrounds the white-clad masculine cleaning body and the woman’s flirtatious reactions;
- the Listerine-style bathroom ad centres the mouth, teeth, and **tongue** through both live action and animation.

This suggests that the pole is not simply about setting or colour. It is also about **commercial bodies as lifestyle evidence**: the body demonstrates health, fitness, beauty, cleanliness, domestic competence, aspiration, or comic excess.

A possible alternative label is therefore:

> **“Colourful embodied lifestyle display”**

This label captures the convergence of colour-rich visual design with detailed bodily styling and action.

---

## Product demonstration and problem-solution staging

The pole also carries a discourse of **practical demonstration**, often in everyday domestic or semi-public settings. Several loadings point toward concrete demonstrations, surfaces, messes, examinations, and product-relevant objects:

- **drip, stainless-steel, tongue, tomato, examination, seatbelt**
- **matter-of-fact, dramatization**
- **pro, directed**

The examples repeatedly show products or services solving visible problems:

- State Farm alternates between a happy car-loan scene and a stripped car problem;
- Noom presents testimonial users in everyday spaces;
- Bounty demonstrates absorbency through a golden liquid spill;
- Mr. Clean removes an orange mess from a glass cooktop, cleans shower glass, and mops floors;
- the mouthwash ad animates liquid washing plaque and germs from teeth;
- FedEx demonstrates delivery management through a smartphone screen;
- AT&T uses children in a classroom to dramatize doing two things at once;
- ExxonMobil visualises natural gas and energy networks as glowing coloured transit lines.

The loadings **matter-of-fact**, **dramatization**, **amuse**, **expressive**, **disbelief**, and **anxious** suggest that these demonstrations are often staged as brief emotional or comic scenarios rather than purely neutral displays. The State Farm excerpt includes excited **disbelief** and frustration; the Planet Fitness ad uses comic discomfort; the Mr. Clean ad turns cleaning into fantasy and flirtation; the classroom ad uses children’s exaggerated performance; the FedEx ad uses a matter-of-fact app demonstration embedded in a doorstep encounter.

Thus another possible label would be:

> **“Everyday problem-solution demonstration”**

This foregrounds the pole’s orientation toward visible, practical, product-relevant action.

---

## Diachronic interpretation

The decade scores suggest a clear diachronic tendency. The pole is most associated with the **recent decades**, especially the **2010s**, with the 2020s and 2000s also relatively close to it. The examples are all from the 2010s, which indicates that this decade strongly exemplifies the pole in the sampled high-scoring texts.

The pattern suggests the emergence or intensification of a contemporary advertising visual style characterised by:

- bright, clean, high-definition environments;
- named ordinary users, athletes, or relatable household figures;
- lifestyle testimony and everyday domestic realism;
- comic or aspirational micro-narratives;
- colourful brand-coded graphics and end screens;
- visible product demonstration through mess, movement, hygiene, fitness, transport, or app use.

The pole therefore appears to capture a **modern commercial visual grammar**: polished, bright, colourful, body-centred, and demonstration-oriented.

---

## Proposed labels

### Preferred label

**Bright contemporary lifestyle demonstration**

This is the strongest label because it accounts for both the loadings and the examples: the repeated lexical emphasis on **bright**, colour terms, clothing/body description, and practical demonstration corresponds closely to the 2010s examples of domestic, fitness, hygiene, testimonial, service, and brand-graphic advertising.

### Other possible labels

1. **High-key branded everyday problem-solving**  
   Emphasises the bright lighting, ordinary settings, and visible product/service solutions.

2. **Colour-coded embodied lifestyle advertising**  
   Highlights the importance of colour, clothing, bodies, hair, fitness, performance, and expressive people.

3. **Contemporary testimonial-and-demonstration visuals**  
   Captures the Noom-style user testimonial, FedEx app demonstration, cleaning demonstrations, hygiene animation, and practical product staging.

4. **Bright domestic and lifestyle product display**  
   Useful if one wants to foreground the many homes, kitchens, bathrooms, bedrooms, classrooms, gyms, driveways, and offices in the examples.

Overall, Factor f3 negative is best interpreted as a discourse of **bright, contemporary, colour-saturated lifestyle advertising in which styled bodies and everyday scenes demonstrate products, services, or brand values through clean visual problem-solving and short affective vignettes**.

## Factor f4 (negative): interpretation as a discourse dimension

The negative pole of Factor 4 appears to encode a discourse of **vehicle-centred, engineered-product display**, especially the visual rhetoric of automobile advertising: cars are shown as designed machines, demonstrated through exterior styling, interior features, road performance, technical components, pricing information, and branded model identity.

Although some example texts include non-automotive demonstrations, the lexical loadings are overwhelmingly dominated by automotive, mechanical, road-infrastructure, and commercial-specification vocabulary. The pole is therefore best interpreted as a **car/transport technology display discourse**, with a broader secondary emphasis on functional product demonstration.

---

## Possible labels for f4 negative

### 1. **Automotive Specification and Road-Test Display**

This is probably the strongest label.

The loading words point very directly to the visual grammar of car commercials:

- vehicle types and brands: **ford, automobile, coupe, four-door, sedan, cab, motorcycle, trailer**
- car parts and design features: **passenger-side, gearshift, bodywork, alloy, hub, headrest, whitewall, dash, streamlined**
- driving environments: **highway, paved, two-lane, guardrail, rural, vegetation**
- performance/operation lexis: **operate, machinery, mechanism, sporty, sturdy**
- commercial/legal terms: **warranty, lease, tax, cash, bank, due, secure**
- display/advertising elements: **billboard, program, original**

The examples strongly reinforce this interpretation. The highest negative-pole excerpts are mostly 1970s automobile commercials:

- **Ford Fairmont Futura** shown in stylised futuristic landscapes, with fuel-economy text and a final profile shot of the **coupe**.
- **Mercury Monarch** shown as a **four-door** sedan, with chrome grille, whitewall tires, smooth-road demonstration, price text, taxes, and Ford/Lincoln-Mercury branding.
- **Mazda coupe/sedan** shown on mountain and coastal roads, with MPG figures, dashboard, stereo controls, folded cargo space, and a manual **gearshift**.
- **Mustang II Cobra II** shown on dirt and paved roads, with performance-feature text: spoiler, stabilizer bars, shocks, rack-and-pinion steering, steel-belted radials, and “4-on-the-floor.”
- **Toyota sports coupe** shown in rural and residential movement, with dashboard, **gearshift**, **headrest**, trunk, and brand display.
- **Monte Carlo** shown in a futuristic showroom/studio, with emphasis on upholstery, dashboard, grille, headrests, chrome trim, and model identity.

This label captures both the lexical loadings and the dominant imagery in the examples: the car is not merely present; it is visually analysed as a technical object and as a purchasable model with specifications.

---

### 2. **Engineered Mobility and Mechanical Product Demonstration**

This broader label is also plausible, especially because the examples include a few non-car product demonstrations.

Beyond automobiles, several excerpts show products through their mechanical or functional action:

- the Firestone “Snow Biters” animation visualises tire tread as teeth gripping snow;
- the Big Wheel Rally Cycle commercial focuses on wheels, racing-style movement, cones, timer, and slalom handling;
- the Liquid-plumr commercial visualises liquid travelling through standing water and pipes to reach a clog;
- the Milk-Bone commercial uses an animated mouth diagram to show teeth being cleaned.

These texts have few or no direct f4 negative loading words, so they should not dominate the interpretation. However, they suggest that this pole may also attract commercials that present products as **working mechanisms**: tires grip, drains clear, teeth are cleaned, wheels turn, gearshifts move, bodies handle curves.

The shared discourse is therefore one of **demonstrable functionality**. Products are shown through physical operation, mechanical action, and problem-solving performance.

Still, because the factor loadings are so strongly automotive, this broader label should be treated as a secondary interpretation rather than the main one.

---

### 3. **Showroom, Styling, and Purchase-Feature Automobility**

A more commercially specific label would emphasise how these visual descriptions combine style, technical features, and sales conditions.

The loadings include not only vehicle and mechanical vocabulary but also purchasing and ownership terms:

- **warranty**
- **lease**
- **tax**
- **cash**
- **bank**
- **due**
- **secure**

The examples likewise include visible sales information:

- fuel-economy figures: “33 HWY MPG,” “26 CITY MPG,” “25/34”
- model and price displays: “UNDER $4800,” “Sedan $5495,” “Coupe $5795”
- feature lists: spoilers, shocks, rack-and-pinion steering, radials, “4-on-the-floor”
- brand and division displays: Ford, Mercury Monarch, Toyota, Monte Carlo

This suggests a discourse in which the visual field functions almost like a showroom or brochure: the viewer is invited to inspect the vehicle’s body, interior, performance features, price, and ownership terms.

This label is useful if the emphasis is on the commercial-sales side of the pole rather than only the road-test side.

## Decades driving this pole

For the negative pole, the relevant decades are those with the **lowest mean Factor 4 scores**:

| Decade | Mean fac4 |
|---|---:|
| 1970s | 4.95 |
| 1990s | 4.97 |
| 1960s | 5.46 |
| 2000s | 5.60 |
| 1980s | 5.76 |
| 1950s | 6.06 |
| 2010s | 7.47 |
| 2020s | 7.97 |

The **1970s** are especially prominent in the supplied examples: nearly all of the illustrative high negative-pole texts come from 1970. This suggests that, in the visual subcorpus, the 1970s strongly instantiate this pole through car commercials built around model display, road handling, fuel economy, interior appointments, and feature lists.

The decade means also indicate that the **1990s** score almost as low as the 1970s, so the pole is not exclusively a 1970s phenomenon. The 1990s likely also contribute substantially to this automotive/technical-product discourse across the full subcorpus, even though the provided examples are mainly 1970s. The presence of loading terms such as **touchscreen, keypad, mouse, cassette, program** may reflect later forms of product technology and interface display, possibly contributing to the 1990s/2000s portion of the pole.

Diachronically, the pattern suggests that this discourse is **less characteristic of the most recent decades**, since the 2010s and 2020s have the highest mean scores and therefore are furthest from the negative pole. In this dataset, the negative pole seems most associated with mid-to-late twentieth-century and transitional commercial visuality, especially the car-centred visual style of the 1970s and, to a lesser extent, the 1990s.

## Overall interpretation

Factor 4 negative can be interpreted as a discourse of:

> **automotive and engineered-product display, where commercials visually foreground vehicles, machinery, technical features, road performance, model identity, and purchase specifications.**

The central semantic domain is **automobility**: cars are shown in profile, on highways, in rural or futuristic landscapes, from interior and exterior angles, with attention to gearshifts, headrests, bodywork, tires, grilles, fuel economy, price, and brand identity. A secondary but related tendency involves **functional product demonstration**, where products are shown as mechanisms that act on the world: gripping snow, clearing drains, cleaning teeth, or navigating a course.

Best concise label:

> **Automotive Specification and Road-Test Display**

Alternative labels:

> **Engineered Mobility and Mechanical Product Demonstration**  
> **Showroom, Styling, and Purchase-Feature Automobility**  
> **Car-Centred Technical Product Display**

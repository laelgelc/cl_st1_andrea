## Interpretation of Factor f4 (positive pole)

Factor f4 positive is best interpreted as a discourse of **curated lifestyle space**: commercials visually organise products and services inside richly furnished, comfortable, colourful, often domestic or leisure-oriented environments. The pole is driven by vocabulary of **home interiors, soft furnishings, gardens, pets, resort/leisure spaces, casual clothing, accessories, and bright colour palettes**. High-scoring texts tend to present the advertised brand not simply as an object, but as part of an attractive lived environment: a cozy home, pet-friendly room, sunny backyard, resort pool, stylish entryway, workout room, club patio, or app-enabled lifestyle scene.

### Suggested labels

Possible labels for this pole include:

1. **“Curated Domestic and Leisure Lifestyle Spaces”**  
2. **“Cozy Home, Garden, Pet, and Lifestyle Staging”**  
3. **“Comfortable, Colourful, Inhabited Lifestyle Environments”**  
4. **“Aspirational Everyday Living: Home, Pets, Leisure, and Décor”**

The strongest label is probably:

> **Curated Domestic and Leisure Lifestyle Spaces**

This captures both the dominant domestic vocabulary and the broader extension into resorts, patios, clubs, pools, gardens, and app-mediated lifestyle settings.

## Justification from the factor loadings

The loadings are heavily concentrated in words that describe **furnished, decorated, inhabited spaces**. The most prominent items include:

- **Interior/domestic space:** *lounge, rug, furniture, blanket, sofa, living, furnishing, carpet, couch, domestic, laundry, bedding, home, room, chair, table, curtain, drape, entryway*
- **Comfort and décor:** *cozy, decorative, candle, sheer, airy, built-in, tidy, lived-in, neutral, linen*
- **Garden/outdoor residential space:** *garden, backyard, potted, flower, patio, leafy, hedge, fence, grass, suburban*
- **Pets and family domesticity:** *dog, pet, paw, treat, tongue*
- **Leisure/resort imagery:** *swimming, pool, poolside, lounge, sunny, palm, hotel, ocean, shoreline*
- **Colour and visual styling:** *turquoise, teal, lavender, cream, lime-green, blue-and-white, peach, greenish, colorful*
- **Casual embodied lifestyle:** *garment, bracelet, necklace, ponytail, sunglasses, skirt, t-shirt, blouse, denim, sweater, sleeve, sleeveless, sneakers*

The semantic centre of the pole is therefore not a single product category. Instead, it is a visual-commercial discourse in which brands are embedded in **styled environments of comfort, care, leisure, and everyday aspiration**. The loading set strongly suggests that high-scoring texts contain many references to **props, furniture, plants, pets, rooms, textiles, apparel, colours, and relaxed bodily presence**.

There is also a smaller but important contemporary layer in words such as *app* and *smartphone*. These suggest that this lifestyle staging can be connected to digital convenience: booking hotels, ordering pet supplies, remote work, home fitness, or streaming. However, the digital element is not the core of the pole; it is integrated into the broader lifestyle environment.

## Justification from the examples

The high-scoring examples strongly support this interpretation. Although all ten excerpts shown are from the 2020s, they illustrate the pole’s main lexical clusters very clearly.

### 1. Resort and leisure lifestyle staging

The Orbitz example is highly loaded with words such as *lounge, blanket, cozy, furnishing, swimming, potted, sunny, turquoise, pool, hotel, room, sunglasses, palm,* and *table*. Visually, it moves through beaches, ski lodges, hotel bedrooms, tropical resorts, soaking tubs, poolside lounge chairs, and golf courses. The commercial presents travel as a sequence of carefully styled leisure environments: turquoise pools, sunny terraces, lounge chairs, hotel rooms, ocean views, and bright branded app interfaces.

This shows the pole’s leisure side: not only “home,” but also **aspirational spaces of relaxation and consumption**.

### 2. Pet-centred domestic convenience

The PetSmart/DoorDash example is loaded with *rug, furniture, living, cozy, dog, pet, potted, ball, home, teal, chair, decorative, little, cream, room, leafy,* and *colorful*. It constructs a playful miniature neighbourhood and then shifts into a real warm living room with a dog, pet bed, rug, chair, plants, side table, and delivery bag.

The Spruce weed-killer example similarly centres on a dog in a living room and backyard, with loading words such as *garden, sofa, living, backyard, dog, pet, potted, flower, sunny, domestic, home, patio, paw,* and *table*. Here, the product is framed through the discourse of **pet-safe, home-and-garden care**.

The Febreze example also uses *rug, garden, sofa, living, cozy, furnishing, dog, pet, potted, flower, entryway, home, curtain, room,* and *leafy*. The home is visually staged as a soft, comfortable, plant-filled domestic environment transformed by freshness.

Together these examples show a strong discourse of **the home as a cared-for, pet-friendly, emotionally warm space**.

### 3. Home as multifunctional lifestyle hub

Several high-scoring 2020s examples present the home as a place where work, entertainment, health, family, and consumption overlap.

The Microsoft example includes *living, dog, pet, sunny, domestic, home, room, table,* and *colorful*. It shows people working from dining rooms, kitchens, living rooms, boats, yards, and family spaces. The home becomes both workplace and family environment.

The Mirror home-fitness example includes *lounge, sofa, living, cozy, built-in, home, chair, curtain, room,* and *leafy*. Workout technology is integrated into stylish living rooms, bright plant-filled rooms, attic-like home spaces, and family interiors.

The Disney+/Hulu example is anchored in a bright living room with *sofa, living, domestic, airy, home, room, leafy, table,* and *neutral*. Streaming entertainment is mediated through a host seated in a curated home interior, while the montage moves through other domestic and lifestyle scenes.

These examples indicate a contemporary commercial discourse in which the home is not merely a background but a **central lifestyle platform**: for work, streaming, family, exercise, pets, cleaning, décor, and digital services.

### 4. Décor, colour, and renovation aesthetics

The BEHR paint example is especially relevant to the décor vocabulary: *sofa, entryway, home, sheer, cloth, teal, decorative, curtain, room, leafy, table,* and *bag*. The commercial foregrounds walls, paint, entryways, curtains, plants, tables, drop cloths, ladders, and carefully styled rooms. It directly literalises the pole’s concern with **home improvement and visual styling**.

The bathroom/toilet-health example also scores high because, despite its unusual subject matter, it is visually organised through rooms and décor: *rug, cozy, garment, potted, flower, turquoise, domestic, home, bedding, teal, candle, curtain, room,* and *table*. It moves across multiple bathroom settings—old-fashioned, modern, luxurious, public, bedroom-adjacent—again using rich spatial staging.

### 5. Casual bodies, clothes, and accessories

The loading list also contains many clothing and appearance words: *garment, bracelet, necklace, ponytail, sunglasses, skirt, t-shirt, curly, blouse, denim, sleeve, sleeveless*. These appear in examples such as Orbitz, Michelob ULTRA, Microsoft, Disney+, and BEHR. They suggest that the pole is not only about spaces but also about **casual lifestyle personae**: people are visually situated through relaxed clothes, accessories, hairstyles, and recreational postures.

The Michelob ULTRA example, for instance, is less domestic than some others, but it still fits the pole through *lounge, sunny, ball, ponytail, patio, sunglasses, skirt, teal, curly, cream, bag, sleeveless,* and *sleeve*. It constructs a leisure-club world of athletic clothing, sunny courts, patio bars, racquet bags, and relaxed social competition.

## Decades driving the pole

The decade means show that the positive pole is strongest in the most recent decades:

| Decade | Mean f4 |
|---|---:|
| 2020s | 7.97 |
| 2010s | 7.47 |
| 1950s | 6.06 |
| 1980s | 5.76 |
| 2000s | 5.60 |
| 1960s | 5.46 |
| 1990s | 4.97 |
| 1970s | 4.95 |

The **2020s** are the clearest driver, followed by the **2010s**. This fits the examples, all of which are from the 2020s and strongly feature domestic interiors, pets, app-based convenience, home fitness, streaming, remote work, delivery, home improvement, and lifestyle leisure.

The relatively high score for the **1950s** is also noteworthy. It suggests that earlier television-commercial visuals may also have relied on domestic rooms, furnishings, family homes, and household products. However, the highest contemporary scores imply that this discourse has intensified or broadened in recent decades. In the 2010s and 2020s, the domestic setting appears to expand into a more general **lifestyle ecosystem**: home plus pets, apps, wellness, décor, delivery, streaming, work, gardens, and leisure travel.

The lower means in the **1970s** and **1990s** suggest that this particular configuration of cozy domesticity, colourful lifestyle décor, pet/home care, and leisure staging is less central in those decades than in the 2010s–2020s.

## Diachronic tendency

Diachronically, the pole suggests a movement toward **highly staged lifestyle environments**, especially in recent advertising. Contemporary commercials increasingly show products as part of an integrated lifestyle scene rather than as isolated goods. The home becomes a visually rich hub of consumption, care, comfort, identity, and technology. Pets, plants, soft furnishings, pastel or teal colour palettes, casual clothing, and app interfaces all contribute to a discourse of **managed comfort and aspirational everyday living**.

The 2020s examples especially suggest a post-2010s advertising style in which almost any product or service—travel booking, pet delivery, weed killer, air freshener, beer, software, fitness technology, streaming, paint—can be promoted through attractive scenes of **domestic comfort, leisure, personal wellbeing, and visually curated lifestyle**.

---

## Recommended interpretation

**Factor f4 positive encodes a discourse of “Curated Domestic and Leisure Lifestyle Spaces.”**  

At this pole, commercials visually sell products through cozy rooms, sofas, rugs, gardens, pets, potted plants, bright colours, casual clothing, sunny patios, pools, resort spaces, and app-enabled convenience. The discourse is especially characteristic of the 2010s and 2020s, where advertising frequently embeds brands in aspirational but familiar environments of home, care, relaxation, wellness, entertainment, and lifestyle consumption.

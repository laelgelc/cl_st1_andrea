## Factor f5 (negative pole): interpretation

### Recommended label  
**“Situated suburban-automotive consumer practicality”**

### Other possible labels  
- **“Home, driveway, and vehicle-based product worlds”**  
- **“Practical ownership in domestic, garden, retail, and car settings”**  
- **“1970s suburban/material consumer realism”**  
- **“Tidy property, mobility, and household-use discourse”**

## Interpretation

The negative pole of Factor 5 appears to encode a discourse of **products embedded in concrete, everyday consumer environments**, especially **houses, driveways, gardens, garages, cars, kitchens, retail displays, and semi-rural leisure spaces**. The pole is strongly associated with a visual world of **suburban property, vehicle ownership, domestic maintenance, and practical consumer use**.

This is not simply a “home” factor or simply an “automobile” factor. The loading vocabulary suggests a broader discourse in which advertised goods are placed within recognizable sites of ownership and use: the **suburban house**, the **garage**, the **front door**, the **driveway**, the **car interior**, the **garden**, the **farm/rural road**, the **department-store holiday display**, or the **kitchen cleanup area**. The commercials at this pole present commodities as part of orderly, functional, respectable everyday life.

## Lexical evidence

Several lexical clusters define this pole.

### 1. Suburban and domestic property settings  
Key loadings include:

- **garage**
- **suburban**
- **house**
- **entryway**
- **entry**
- **door**
- **hedge**
- **garden**
- **backyard**
- **leafy**
- **poolside**
- **potted**
- **tidy**
- **linen**
- **stainless-steel**

These words point to **material domestic space**: houses, thresholds, lawns, gardens, clean interiors, and household objects. The emphasis is visual and spatial. Products are not abstractly presented; they are placed in a lived environment.

### 2. Automotive ownership and vehicle display  
A second strong cluster is automotive:

- **pickup**
- **cab**
- **passenger-side**
- **four-door**
- **sedan**
- **convertible**
- **Ford**
- **chrome**
- **vinyl**
- **trailer**
- **transportation**
- **garage**
- **door**

This cluster suggests commercials that visually dwell on **vehicle bodies, interiors, trim, doors, seating, dashboards, wheels, and exterior styling**. The automotive discourse is not only about movement; it is about ownership, inspection, display, and practical transport.

### 3. Retail, payment, and promotional purchase discourse  
Another prominent cluster concerns buying conditions:

- **lease**
- **due**
- **month**
- **tax**
- **cash**
- **pay**
- **total**
- **year**
- **promotional**
- **employee**
- **department**

These words indicate that the pole also includes a **commercial transaction frame**: price, payment, lease terms, store departments, promotional offers, and employee-mediated retail spaces. This is especially compatible with automobile advertising, appliance/cookware retailing, and household-product demonstrations.

### 4. Rural, outdoor, and leisure practicality  
A smaller but meaningful cluster evokes rural or outdoor settings:

- **farm**
- **rural**
- **saddle**
- **sturdy**
- **trailer**
- **high-visibility**

These words extend the pole beyond suburbia into **practical outdoor mobility and leisure**: riding, farming, rural roads, utility vehicles, and durable equipment.

### 5. Respectable social presentation  
Person-description terms such as:

- **clean-cut**
- **well-dressed**
- **tailored**
- **short-sleeve**
- **attentive**

suggest that people in these commercials are often visually coded as **respectable, practical, competent, and socially orderly**. They are shown using, demonstrating, comparing, buying, or presenting products in controlled everyday settings.

## Evidence from the excerpts

The examples strongly support this reading, even though each individual excerpt contains only a few loading words. Together, they show the pole’s characteristic visual world.

### Suburban house, family, yard, and product integration  
In **f5_neg_001** from the 1970s, the loading words are **suburban**, **house**, and **door**. The commercial is set around a large suburban house, driveway, doorway, lawn, and leafy yard. Pepsi is integrated into a family gathering around a newborn. The product is not presented in isolation; it becomes part of **family celebration, home space, and intergenerational domestic sociability**.

This is a clear example of the pole’s domestic-property discourse: the house, car, lawn, doorway, family, picnic table, and beverage all form one coherent consumer scene.

### Car display against house, hedge, driveway, and interior detail  
In **f5_neg_002**, the loading words are **hedge**, **house**, and **door**. The Audi sedan is shown in front of a brick house, hedges, and a driveway. The visual description lingers on the car door, tan interior, dashboard, gear selector, chrome trim, seating, and road performance.

This excerpt links the domestic-property setting with the automotive cluster in the loadings. The car is framed as an object of **domestic affluence, careful inspection, and practical driving pleasure**.

### Automotive demonstration, price, chrome, and respectable presenter  
In **f5_neg_010**, the loading words are **clean-cut** and **door**. The Mercury Monarch commercial includes a suited presenter, a four-door sedan, chrome grille, vinyl roof, price text, taxes, and an “actual demonstration.” This aligns closely with the loading set: **four-door**, **sedan**, **chrome**, **vinyl**, **tax**, **cash/pay-type purchase vocabulary**, **clean-cut**, and **door**.

The discourse here is one of **credible ownership and value**: the car is demonstrated, inspected, priced, and visually stabilized as a respectable purchase.

### Household cleaning and domestic demonstration  
In **f5_neg_005** and **f5_neg_009**, the settings are kitchens and cleaning areas. Josephine demonstrates Comet at the sink; the Mr. Muscle commercial shows oven-cleaning demonstrations, product labels, stains, sponges, gloves, and household work.

These excerpts support the pole’s practical domestic-use side. The commercials present products through **demonstration, comparison, cleaning efficacy, and household competence**. The loading word **attentive** in f5_neg_005 is particularly apt: viewers are shown people watching, comparing, and learning how the product works.

### Garden, hedge, lawn tractor, and domestic maintenance  
In **f5_neg_007**, the loading word is **hedge**. The commercial features a riding lawn tractor moving between neatly clipped hedges and across a lawn. This is almost a textbook instance of the pole’s **tidy property maintenance** discourse: outdoor domestic space, machinery, grass, hedges, and practical control of the home landscape.

### Department-store holiday display and polished household goods  
In **f5_neg_008**, the loading word is **stainless-steel**. The commercial presents cookware arranged with evergreen branches, pine cones, Christmas decorations, and smiling department-store figures. This connects the pole’s household-object vocabulary with **retail display** and **holiday purchase discourse**: polished goods, department-store setting, seasonal promotion, and respectable consumer abundance.

### Rural mobility, utility, and outdoor communication  
In **f5_neg_004**, the repeated vehicle-door imagery, Jeep, CB radio, rural road, bridge, fisherman, and outdoor setting activate the pole’s rural/transport side. Even though only **door** appears as a loading word in the excerpt, the wider vocabulary of the factor — **pickup**, **cab**, **trailer**, **rural**, **farm**, **transportation**, **sturdy** — fits this kind of product world.

### Equestrian leisure and lifestyle equipment  
In **f5_neg_003** and **f5_neg_006**, the loading word **saddle** links the pole to equestrian imagery. These commercials use horse riding, saddles, riding boots, reins, and leisure settings to present wine or a watchband. This expands the pole from purely domestic practicality into **lifestyle ownership and aspirational outdoor leisure**, but still through concrete material settings and equipment.

## Decade pattern

For a negative pole, the relevant decades are those with the **lowest mean Factor 5 scores**. The lowest mean is:

- **1970s: 1.398**

The next lowest are:

- **1950s: 1.602**
- **2020s: 1.631**
- **1960s: 1.660**

The 1970s clearly appear to drive this pole most strongly. All ten high-scoring negative-pole excerpts provided are from the **1970s**, and they repeatedly show the same visual logic: domestic houses, cars, kitchens, hedges, cookware, household demonstrations, outdoor leisure, and practical consumer environments.

The diachronic tendency is therefore not a simple linear rise or decline. Instead, this pole seems especially characteristic of the **1970s visual-commercial style** in the sample, with some weaker affinity to earlier decades and a possible limited reappearance in the 2020s. The 1990s, by contrast, have the highest mean Factor 5 score and therefore do not drive this negative pole.

Because all decade means are positive, the decade pattern should be treated as **relative rather than absolute**: the 1970s are not necessarily the only decade containing this discourse, but they are the decade in which this pole is most strongly represented relative to the others.

## Overall discourse dimension

This pole can be interpreted as a discourse of **practical consumer goods situated in recognizable spaces of ownership and use**. Its visual world is populated by:

- suburban houses and doorways;
- driveways, garages, cars, and vehicle interiors;
- kitchens, ovens, sinks, cookware, and cleaning demonstrations;
- gardens, hedges, lawns, and backyard gatherings;
- retail displays, promotions, and payment/pricing cues;
- rural roads, utility vehicles, saddles, and outdoor leisure.

A concise label for the pole would therefore be:

> **“Situated suburban-automotive consumer practicality”**

This label captures the central combination of **place-based domesticity**, **vehicle/property ownership**, **practical product demonstration**, and **1970s-style material consumer realism** that is visible in both the factor loadings and the example excerpts.

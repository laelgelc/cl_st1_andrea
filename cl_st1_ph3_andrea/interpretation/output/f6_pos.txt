## Factor f6 (positive): interpretation as a discourse dimension

### Suggested primary label  
**“Domestic tabletop demonstration and serving discourse”**

### Other possible labels  
1. **“Packaged food preparation, serving, and product-display discourse”**  
2. **“Mid-century kitchen-table consumption discourse”**  
3. **“Demonstrated domestic use: meals, utensils, packages, and serving scenes”**  
4. **“Tableware-and-package centred household consumption discourse”**

---

## Interpretation

The positive pole of Factor 6 is strongly associated with visual descriptions of commercials that centre on **domestic food preparation, meal serving, table settings, eating, and product display**. The pole is lexically dominated by concrete objects from the kitchen, dining table, and packaged-goods display: **utensil, spoon, fork, plate, dish, saucer, cup, pitcher, pot, platter, dinner, milk, drink, spoonful, mound, pastry, frosted, tomato, shredded, crumb**, and related preparation terms such as **serve, taste, preparation, remove, measure, pinch, drip, take, pick**.  

A second important strand is the recurrent presence of **packages and labels**: **carton, package, packaging, packet, wording, picture, illustration, readable, copyright, starburst**. This indicates not only scenes of food and household use, but also commercials that visually stage the product as a labelled object to be shown, read, handled, opened, poured from, or placed beside the finished result.

Together, these loadings suggest a discourse of **demonstrated consumption**: products are not merely advertised abstractly but are visually embedded in practical household routines — preparing, serving, eating, washing, arranging, comparing, displaying, and reading labels or offer materials.

---

## Evidence from the factor loadings

The highest-loading words point very clearly to **meal service and tabletop material culture**:

- **utensil, spoon, fork, plate, dish, saucer, cup, pitcher, platter, pot**
- **serve, taste, spoonful, dinner, drinking, drink**
- **mound, pastry, frosted, crumb, tomato, shredded, fruit, foamy**

These terms evoke close visual attention to food surfaces, table settings, prepared dishes, and eating actions. The pole is therefore highly object-centred and tactile: food is shown as something scooped, poured, sampled, placed, arranged, and served.

A second cluster concerns **preparation and demonstration**:

- **preparation, remove, measure, pinch, drip, cookware, vessel, shallow, counter, tabletop**

These suggest procedural visuals: hands opening packages, measuring detergent or ingredients, placing food into pans, pouring drinks, or transferring items from carton to oven, table, or serving dish.

A third cluster concerns **packaged-product display and readable commercial copy**:

- **carton, package, packaging, packet, wording, picture, illustration, readable, printed, starburst, copyright**

This indicates that high-scoring texts often include explicit shots of boxes, cans, coupons, labels, signs, and on-screen wording. The visual discourse is therefore not only culinary or domestic, but also strongly tied to the **material presentation of branded goods**.

Finally, some clothing and domestic-person words — **collar, short-sleeved, coat, moustache, eyeglass, turtleneck, torso** — point to staged presenters, homemakers, children, clerks, hosts, or family members appearing in controlled domestic or service settings. These are not the semantic core of the factor, but they support the mid-century staged-commercial feel of the pole.

---

## Evidence from the high-scoring examples

The examples are overwhelmingly from the **1950s**, and they repeatedly show the same visual pattern: domestic or service-space scenes organised around food, tableware, serving, product packages, and readable labels.

### 1. Food, tableware, and family serving scenes

Several examples are classic kitchen or dining-table commercials.

In the Campbell’s soup example, the mother feeds children at a kitchen table with **bowls, spoons, plates, cups of milk**, and a visible soup can. Loading words such as **spoon, serve, taste, spoonful, dish, milk, pot, plate, cup, package, counter, readable** are directly realised in the visual description. The commercial repeatedly shows children eating, lifting spoons, tasting soup, and the final product lineup of Campbell’s cans.

The Aunt Jemima example similarly centres on breakfast preparation and family eating: a woman pours batter, uses a **spoon**, cooks on a griddle, serves pancakes at a table with **plates, cups, pitcher, fork**, and ends with a product package display. This strongly exemplifies the pole’s combination of **preparation + serving + branded package**.

The Good Measure diet dinner example shows a place setting with **utensils, plate, glass**, then a fuller stew dish, then labelled cans. The commercial visually converts the product into a plated dinner and then back into a package lineup. This movement between **served food** and **readable product label** is central to the pole.

### 2. Packaged convenience foods and “heat/serve” demonstrations

The Swanson TV dinner excerpt is especially representative. It contains many high-loading items: **utensil, spoon, serve, dish, fork, mound, dinner, carton, plate, package, picture, tabletop, readable**. The commercial shows the man removing a frozen dinner carton, the tray being placed in the oven, the phrase **“HEAT and SERVE”**, and then the prepared meal arranged with utensils around it. This is almost a prototypical instance of the factor: packaged product, procedural preparation, table setting, visible food compartments, and label display.

The Pillsbury caramel nut rolls excerpt also fits closely. It shows a breakfast-table arrangement with **plate, cup, saucer, spoon, fork**, then a preparation sequence involving a **shallow** pan, measuring cup, dough pieces, spooned liquid, and finally a product package graphic. The loading words **spoon, serve, dish, fork, saucer, measure, shallow, plate, pinch, cup, drink, package, piece, picture, tabletop** are all anchored in the visual content.

### 3. Product display, coupons, readable labels, and offer material

The Baker’s cocoa example foregrounds package display and readable promotional text. The product package is surrounded by a **cup on a saucer, frosted cake, dessert dish, plate of chocolate pieces**, then shown in a store display with a coupon and guarantee text. Here the factor’s packaging/readability vocabulary — **wording, package, picture, readable** — is especially important.

The Calometric example similarly uses the table as a display surface. It shows a meal-plan arrangement with **dinner, plates, cups, saucers, pitcher or pot**, followed by a product box, bottle, business-reply envelope, and point-of-sale sign. This demonstrates that the factor is not limited to food enjoyment; it also includes diet products and household-commercial demonstrations where the product is materially displayed, labelled, and explained.

### 4. Service counters and hospitality settings

The Red Cap Ale example broadens the discourse from home kitchen to domestic hospitality. It shows a formal buffet table with **platters, dishes, cups, pitchers, glassware**, bottles on ice, a pouring demonstration, and a final product display with bottle and foamy mug. The soda-fountain Vanquish example similarly includes a counter, drinks, saucers, a spoonful from a sundae, product packaging, and written copy. These examples show that the factor includes not only home kitchens but also staged service spaces where products are served, sampled, poured, or displayed.

### 5. Household cleaning through food/table mess and product demonstration

The Oxydol detergent example is slightly less food-product-centred, but it still contributes strongly because its visual structure begins with a messy party table: **plates, cups, utensils, pitcher, platter, crumbs**, spilled drink, and children’s soiled clothes. It then moves into a domestic laundry demonstration with measuring cup, detergent package, readable label, and clean garments. This shows that the factor captures a broader **domestic demonstration format**, not simply food advertising. The shared visual grammar is still practical and object-centred: mess, product, measuring, package, before/after result.

## Decades driving the pole

The decade scores show that this pole is driven most strongly by the **1950s** and **1960s**:

| Decade | Mean f6 score |
|---|---:|
| 1950s | 5.47 |
| 1960s | 4.76 |
| 1980s | 3.90 |
| 1970s | 3.30 |
| 2000s | 2.07 |
| 2020s | 1.92 |
| 1990s | 1.89 |
| 2010s | 1.62 |

The **1950s** are the clearest driver, and all ten provided high-scoring examples come from that decade. The **1960s** also score strongly, suggesting that this visual-commercial style remained highly characteristic into the following decade. The **1970s** and especially the **1980s** still show moderate-to-high association with the pole, implying continued use of food preparation, product demonstration, and tabletop product-display conventions.

From the **1990s onward**, the mean scores are much lower, though still positive. This suggests a diachronic weakening of this visual discourse in the selected commercial visual subcorpus. The pole appears most typical of earlier television advertising, especially mid-century commercials that relied on clear domestic staging, demonstrators, close-ups of products, visible labels, and practical demonstrations of use.

---

## Diachronic tendency

The positive pole appears to encode a historically prominent style of television-commercial visuality: **the mid-century demonstrational household commercial**. In the 1950s and 1960s, commercials often visually organise persuasion around the product’s practical use. A typical sequence is:

1. show a domestic problem or occasion;
2. introduce a packaged product;
3. demonstrate preparation, serving, measuring, pouring, or use;
4. show the resulting food, clean laundry, drink, or household benefit;
5. end with a package, label, coupon, or offer displayed clearly to the viewer.

The high early-decade scores suggest that this factor captures a conventional advertising mode in which the viewer is invited to inspect the product almost physically: its box, can, carton, label, texture, serving suggestion, and practical use are all made visually explicit. Later decades appear less strongly tied to this pole, indicating that this dense concentration of tableware, food preparation, package display, and demonstrational household staging becomes less central over time.

---

## Summary

Factor f6 positive is best interpreted as a discourse of **domestic tabletop demonstration and serving**. It is characterised by:

- kitchen and dining-table objects: **spoons, forks, plates, cups, saucers, pitchers, dishes, platters**;
- food preparation and serving actions: **serve, taste, measure, remove, take, pick, pinch**;
- visible prepared foods and textures: **mounds, spoonfuls, frosted items, crumbs, drinks, milk, dinner dishes**;
- product packaging and commercial copy: **cartons, packages, labels, illustrations, wording, readable text**;
- staged domestic or service environments: kitchens, breakfast tables, buffet tables, soda counters, laundry counters, and product displays.

The pole is driven chiefly by the **1950s and 1960s**, with a continued but weaker presence in the 1970s and 1980s, and much lower association from the 1990s onward. A concise label for the pole would therefore be:

> **“Domestic tabletop demonstration and packaged-product serving discourse.”**

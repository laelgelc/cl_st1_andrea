## Factor f7 (negative pole): interpretation

This negative pole appears to encode a **contemporary, action-oriented visual discourse of bodies, mobility, product testing, technology, and regulated claims**, especially characteristic of the **2010s** and, more broadly, the **2000s–2020s**.

Although the mean decade scores are not negative overall, the **lowest decade means** occur in:

- **2010s:** 0.087 — strongest decadeal association with this pole  
- **2020s:** 0.408  
- **2000s:** 1.165  

This suggests that the pole is driven primarily by **recent commercial visual styles**, with the clearest concentration in the **2010s**. The examples are all from the 2010s, reinforcing that this decade provides especially strong high-scoring instances of the pole.

---

## Core discourse encoded at this pole

### 1. Kinetic bodily activity and performance

A major lexical cluster concerns **physical exertion, exercise, athletic movement, and embodied action**:

- **exercise**
- **strain**
- **gym**
- **athletic**
- **physical**
- **bench**
- **stationary**
- **bike**
- **swimming**
- **pool**
- **poolside**
- **flip**
- **rush**
- **intense**
- **focused**

These words point to commercials whose visuals foreground **active bodies**, often in stylised or highly mediated settings: gyms, rooftops, pools, sports-like spaces, or physically demanding situations.

This is strongly illustrated by the examples:

- The **Planet Fitness** commercial opens in a gym with exercise machines, gym users, and a muscular man moving through the space.
- The **Just For Men** commercial shows young men performing rooftop jumps, flips, and parkour-like movements.
- The **Lexus** commercial combines athletic imagery with high-speed car performance, beginning with athletes and then transferring the performance motif to the car.
- The **Kerydin** commercial includes beaches, bare feet, poolside lounging, garden work, and exercise mats, linking the product to everyday embodied activity.

The pole therefore includes a discourse of **dynamic bodies under exertion**, not necessarily formal sport only, but a broader visual vocabulary of movement, fitness, strain, speed, and physical display.

---

### 2. Mobility, vehicles, driving, and performance machines

Another strong cluster relates to cars, driving, transport, and vehicle offers:

- **auto**
- **seatbelt**
- **traffic**
- **lease**
- **month**
- **professional**
- **warning**
- **safety**
- **refer**
- **receive**
- **apply**

The examples show that this pole is also associated with **automotive and mobility advertising**, particularly contemporary car commercials that combine rapid motion, cinematic camera work, legal disclaimers, and sales/lease information.

Examples include:

- The **Lexus IS** commercial, with night driving, track imagery, professional-driver warnings, horsepower displays, and lease-offer screens.
- The **Alfa Romeo** commercial, with high-speed scenic driving, road shots, motion blur, professional-driver disclaimer text, and close-ups of wheels and exhaust.
- The **Chevrolet “Real people. Not actors.”** commercial, set in a large vehicle display hall, with people examining different Chevrolet models.
- The **Kia Soul** hamster commercial, which mixes urban mobility, stylised character performance, vehicle display, safety references, and lease pricing.

This suggests a discourse of **commercialised mobility and machine performance**, where cars are not just products but visualised through speed, control, technological polish, and regulated sales language.

---

### 3. Product demonstrations, tests, and visual proof

Several loading words point toward **demonstration-like visual structures**:

- **application**
- **user**
- **apply**
- **receive**
- **target**
- **relief**
- **medical**
- **warning**
- **safety**
- **refer**

These suggest commercials that visually stage a product’s use, effect, comparison, or eligibility conditions. This is especially clear in the examples involving product trials, demonstrations, or healthcare/legal framing.

The **Dawn** example is a strong case: it shows a controlled comparison setup with bottles, sponges, water tanks, tracks, splashes, bubbles, and ripples. The visual description repeatedly presents the product as being tested under semi-laboratory conditions.

The **Kerydin** example uses a medical-advertising structure: everyday activities involving bare feet are followed by white-background product frames, toenail close-ups, FDA-related text, and fine-print safety information.

The **Capital One Venture** example also uses a quasi-demonstrative structure: airport counters, forms, laptops, reward-mile claims, boarding passes, and card close-ups visually support the product’s financial/travel proposition.

Thus, the pole includes a discourse of **visual evidence and procedural demonstration**: products are shown being tested, applied, compared, used, qualified, or explained.

---

### 4. Water, surfaces, motion effects, and sensory spectacle

A notable subset of loadings concerns water, floating, rippling, and visually textured movement:

- **ripple**
- **float**
- **deep**
- **swimming**
- **pool**
- **poolside**
- **air**
- **hot**
- **icy**

These terms point to a visual discourse of **sensory spectacle**, where movement through air, water, heat, cold, or reflective surfaces becomes central to the commercial’s appeal.

This is strongly supported by the examples:

- The **Dawn** commercial uses splashing sponges, underwater shots, bubbles, ripples, and floating objects.
- The **Levi’s** commercial shifts from intimate urban close-ups to a blue-toned underwater scene where a couple embraces and kisses amid turbulent rippling water.
- The **Kerydin** commercial uses beaches, pools, feet in sand, and overhead poolside/beach compositions.
- The car commercials use smoke, mist, glare, lens flare, reflections, and motion blur as part of their performance aesthetic.

This pole is therefore not only about action but about **visually intensified action**: splashes, flips, speed, ripples, glare, smoke, and cinematic movement.

---

### 5. Contemporary technology, interfaces, and user-oriented settings

Several loadings suggest modern digital and consumer-technology contexts:

- **user**
- **application**
- **headphones**
- **headset**
- **lowercase**
- **refer**
- **receive**

These terms indicate a more recent commercial environment in which products are embedded in **apps, screens, headsets, laptops, digital text, and user interfaces**.

The examples support this:

- The **Oculus Quest** opening screen shows a headset and controllers, with brand and rating information.
- The **Capital One** example includes a laptop screen, headphones, airport digital interaction, and card/boarding-pass close-ups.
- The **Chevrolet** example includes a dashboard screen with app-like icons.
- The **Planet Fitness** example ends with a modern minimalist title card using lowercase text: “not his planet.”

The pole therefore includes a discourse of **contemporary user experience**, where commercials visually incorporate screens, interfaces, devices, and user-centred environments.

## Possible labels for f7 negative pole

### Label 1: **Contemporary Kinetic Lifestyle and Product-Performance Discourse**

This is probably the most comprehensive label. It captures the pole’s association with:

- athletic bodies and exercise;
- fast movement and visual energy;
- cars and mobility;
- product demonstrations;
- modern lifestyle settings;
- recent decadeal concentration, especially the 2010s.

It also fits the examples, which repeatedly show bodies, products, and vehicles in motion: gym movement, rooftop flips, driving sequences, water tests, airport mobility, and lifestyle montage.

---

### Label 2: **Action, Mobility, and Demonstration-Based Commercial Visuality**

This label foregrounds three recurrent strands:

1. **Action** — exercise, athleticism, flips, strain, rushing, physicality.  
2. **Mobility** — cars, traffic, seatbelts, leases, airports, travel, roads.  
3. **Demonstration** — applications, warnings, safety text, comparison tests, product-use displays.

It is well suited to the mixture of automotive, healthcare, household-product, and technology commercials in the examples.

---

### Label 3: **2010s Cinematic Performance-and-Compliance Advertising**

This label places more emphasis on the decadeal pattern and on the coexistence of spectacular visuals with legal or regulatory framing.

The loading words **warning**, **professional**, **safety**, **medical**, **lease**, **month**, **apply**, and **refer** suggest that many commercials at this pole include disclaimers, offer details, eligibility conditions, or safety claims. At the same time, words such as **athletic**, **exercise**, **flip**, **ripple**, **overhead**, **intense**, and **cinematic** point to a highly produced visual style.

This label is especially apt for examples such as Lexus, Alfa Romeo, Kerydin, Capital One, Chevrolet, and Kia, where cinematic imagery is combined with fine print, legal language, lease terms, medical text, or product claims.

---

### Label 4: **Embodied Motion, Screens, and Regulated Consumer Experience**

This label highlights the specifically contemporary combination of:

- bodies in motion;
- technologically mediated consumer settings;
- product use and user experience;
- warnings, safety information, and official claims.

It is particularly appropriate if the interpretation wants to stress the connection between **physical lifestyle imagery** and **modern consumer systems**: gyms, apps, headphones, headsets, airport rewards, medical products, automotive leases, and dashboard interfaces.

---

## Recommended label

The strongest overall label for this pole is:

### **Contemporary Kinetic Lifestyle and Product-Performance Discourse**

This label best accommodates both the factor loadings and the examples. The pole is not limited to one product category. It includes fitness, cars, credit cards, clothing, medicine, dish soap, coffee machines, and technology. What unites these commercials is a visual style built around **motion, performance, active bodies, product demonstration, contemporary environments, and regulated consumer claims**.

Diachronically, the pole appears to be driven especially by the **2010s**, with weaker but still relatively associated presence in the **2000s and 2020s**. It therefore seems to mark a more recent television-commercial visual discourse: dynamic, cinematic, mobile, user-oriented, and often accompanied by disclaimers, offer details, or proof-like demonstrations.

## Interpretation of Factor f7 negative pole

Factor f7 negative appears to encode a **contemporary, color-saturated consumer-lifestyle discourse**, centred on visually busy modern commercials that combine branded colour palettes, shopping or gifting situations, domestic and outdoor action, families/children/pets, vehicles, tools, product displays, apps, offer screens, and fine-print promotional framing.

A suitable main label would be:

### **“Contemporary Colour-Saturated Lifestyle Shopping and Product-Use Discourse”**

This pole is especially associated with the **2020s**, which have the lowest mean score on Factor 7 and provide all the high-scoring negative-pole examples. The 2000s, 1990s, and 2010s also have relatively low means compared with earlier decades, suggesting that this discourse becomes increasingly characteristic of later television-commercial visuals.

---

## Evidence from the factor loadings

The loading words point to several overlapping semantic fields.

### 1. Strong colour and brand-palette vocabulary

Many loading words are colour terms or colour combinations:

- **red, blue, green, yellow**
- **navy, olive-green**
- **red-and-yellow, green-and-white**

This suggests commercials whose visual descriptions repeatedly foreground **bright colour schemes**, often linked to product packaging, brand identity, clothing, retail signage, vehicles, holiday decoration, or graphic end cards.

This is strongly confirmed in the examples: Ritz, Danimals, Juicy Drop, Walmart, Toyota, GMC, Bosch, Amazon, and Dell all use highly visible brand or seasonal colour palettes.

### 2. Shopping, retail, offers, and promotional framing

The loadings include:

- **shopping, shopper, department, storefront, awning**
- **holiday, gift**
- **total, entry**
- **warranty, fine-print, legal, copyright**

These words point to commercials organised around **purchase contexts**, retail environments, offer screens, product interfaces, seasonal sales, or legal/disclaimer text. The pole is therefore not simply “domestic” or “family” visual discourse; it is commercialized lifestyle action, with the act of buying, gifting, ordering, or receiving products built into the visual world.

In the excerpts, this appears in the Amazon shopping app, Dell order-management screen, Toyota and GMC holiday event offers, Walmart product-spend fine print, True Value store aisles, and product display end cards.

### 3. Contemporary product categories and mobility

The loading list also contains words such as:

- **pickup, SUV, motorcycle, trailer, garage**
- **mobile, controller, earphone, navigation, update**
- **pro**

These suggest a modern commercial world of vehicles, digital devices, tools, apps, connected consumer technologies, and promotional product categories. Even where the examples are not all digital, they show this contemporary product-use framing: Dell laptops and AI-style creation interfaces, Amazon app shopping, GMC and Toyota vehicles, Bosch tools, True Value hardware projects.

### 4. Family, children, pets, and playful action

Another cluster includes:

- **kid**
- **leash, paw**
- **bound, trot**
- **joyful, impressed, nervous**
- **mud, muddy, pond, garden, farm**
- **ball, fight, shout, pace**

These words indicate commercials with **active bodies and lively scenes**: children playing, pets running, vehicles moving through rough terrain, people reacting emotionally, DIY or outdoor activity, and sometimes comic messiness or physical spectacle.

This is visible in the examples: the puppy bounding through snow in the GMC commercial, the child splashing into a muddy puddle in Danimals, the boy with the Toyota ride-on car, the Juicy Drop kid at the skate park, the Bosch boxing fantasy in a construction space, and family members dancing or reacting joyfully in holiday interiors.

### 5. Domestic, DIY, and everyday material settings

Loadings such as:

- **garage, entryway, staircase, houseplant**
- **ladder, plank, cookware, whisk, tomato**
- **overshirt, knit, beanie, long-sleeve**

suggest lived-in, practical, material environments: homes, kitchens, garages, yards, shops, and workspaces. The pole therefore involves **products embedded in everyday action scenes**, not only abstract product shots.

This is especially clear in the True Value DIY commercial, the Dell kitchen/bakery/home scenes, the Toyota garage/driveway scenes, and the Walmart firehouse kitchen.

## Evidence from the examples

The high-scoring examples are all from the **2020s**, and they illustrate the pole as a modern advertising style built from fast-moving, vivid, product-centred lifestyle vignettes.

### Holiday/gift and family scenes

The Dell, GMC, and Toyota examples are strongly seasonal. They include loading words such as **holiday, shopping, entry, joyful, red, blue, green, yellow, knit, bound, daytime**. These commercials show Christmas trees, snowy landscapes, decorated homes, gift reveals, family reactions, children, pets, vehicles, and promotional offer screens.

This supports a label involving **seasonal consumer lifestyle** and **gift-oriented visual discourse**.

### Retail and shopping environments

The True Value and Amazon examples directly enact shopping:

- True Value: shopper, store associate, red shopping carts, department signs, hardware aisles, DIY purchases.
- Amazon: app interface, “Buy Now” button, delivery box, “Spend less. Smile more.”

These examples activate the loading words **shopping, shopper, department, storefront/entry-related vocabulary, red, blue, yellow** and support the interpretation of this pole as a **shopping/product-acquisition discourse**.

### Bright packaged-product and youth/play scenes

Danimals, Juicy Drop, and Ritz foreground bright packaging, primary colours, children, snacks, graphic transitions, product displays, and playful bodily action. Loading words such as **kid, red-and-yellow, red, blue, green, yellow, mud** appear directly.

These examples show that the pole is not limited to adult shopping or household goods. It also includes **colourful child-oriented product spectacle**, especially where products are shown through energetic, playful, visually graphic scenes.

### Product-use, tools, vehicles, and action spectacle

Bosch, GMC, Toyota, and True Value show the product in use or dramatized through action: trucks in snow, tools in construction spaces, pressure washers, drills, lasers, saws, and vehicles in driveways or garages. Loadings such as **mud, pickup, SUV, garage, ladder, plank, pro, legal, fine-print** fit this broader pattern.

The pole therefore includes **practical consumer-product performance**, often made visually exciting through motion, terrain, mess, or comic fantasy.

## Decade pattern and diachronic tendency

For the negative pole, the relevant decades are those with the **lowest mean Factor 7 scores**:

- **2020s: 0.194**
- **2000s: 0.476**
- **1990s: 0.922**
- **2010s: 1.010**
- **1980s: 1.117**

The strongest driver is clearly the **2020s**, since it has the lowest mean and all the supplied high-scoring negative-pole examples come from that decade. The 2000s, 1990s, 2010s, and 1980s also sit much lower than the 1950s and 1960s, suggesting a broad diachronic movement toward this pole in later advertising visuals.

The trend is not perfectly linear after 1980, but the major diachronic pattern is clear: this pole is associated with **late-modern and especially contemporary commercial visual discourse**. It reflects a newer advertising world of branded colour, shopping interfaces, holiday campaigns, product-use vignettes, lifestyle montage, legal/offer screens, domestic scenes, pets, children, vehicles, and digital or retail environments.

## Possible labels for f7 negative

### 1. **Contemporary Colour-Saturated Lifestyle Shopping Discourse**

This is the strongest label. It captures the colour loadings, the shopping and retail vocabulary, the modern decade pattern, and the examples’ emphasis on colourful branded lifestyles, family scenes, stores, apps, gifts, and product displays.

### 2. **Modern Branded Product-in-Use Vignettes**

This label foregrounds the fact that products are repeatedly shown embedded in action: trucks driving through snow, tools used in DIY and construction, laptops used for ordering and creation, candy consumed by children, coffee unpacked at a firehouse, Amazon packages delivered. It is useful because the pole is not only about shopping, but also about products being enacted in lively scenes.

### 3. **Seasonal Retail, Family, and Everyday Consumer Action**

This label fits the examples especially well, because many high-scoring texts involve holiday events, gift reveals, families, pets, children, shopping, delivery, and domestic spaces. However, it may slightly underrepresent the broader loading evidence for tools, vehicles, mud, legal text, and digital/technical products.

### 4. **Contemporary Promotional Lifestyle Montage**

This broader label captures the rapid movement across scenes, colour palettes, people, products, interfaces, offer screens, and end cards. It is especially apt for the 2020s examples, many of which proceed through montage-like sequences of product use, social reaction, and branded closing graphics.

---

## Recommended label

### **Contemporary Colour-Saturated Lifestyle Shopping and Product-Use Discourse**

This label best balances the lexical loadings and the examples. The pole is driven by recent decades, especially the 2020s, and encodes a visual-commercial style built around bright brand colours, shopping and gifting, family or child-centred lifestyle scenes, pets, vehicles, tools, apps, product displays, and promotional/legal framing.

## Factor f7 positive: interpretation of the pole

The positive pole of Factor 7 appears to encode a strongly **mid-century presentational display discourse** in television-commercial visuals: staged studio address, elegant presenters, formal grooming and fashion, visible product handling, animated or graphic illustration, and prominent on-screen lettering. It is especially characteristic of the **1950s**, with the **1960s** also scoring relatively high, suggesting that this pole captures early television advertising conventions that weaken sharply after the first two decades.

### Possible labels for f7 positive

1. **“Formal Studio Presentation and Product Display”**  
2. **“Mid-Century Demonstrative Elegance”**  
3. **“Staged Presenter-Led Advertising with Scripted Graphics”**  
4. **“1950s Display Aesthetics: Poise, Product Form, and On-Screen Copy”**  
5. **“Theatrical Demonstration and Graphic Product Styling”**

The most suitable overall label is probably:

> **Formal Studio Presentation and Product Display**

This label captures both the interpersonal staging of the commercials — presenters, studio sets, posture, direct address — and the visual treatment of products as carefully displayed objects, often accompanied by lettering, animation, or hand demonstrations.

## Decade pattern

The decade scores show that this pole is overwhelmingly driven by the earliest strata:

| Decade | Mean f7 score |
|---|---:|
| 1950s | **10.16** |
| 1960s | **5.35** |
| 1970s | 1.85 |
| 1980s | 1.12 |
| 1990s | 0.92 |
| 2000s | 0.48 |
| 2010s | 1.01 |
| 2020s | 0.19 |

The **1950s are by far the strongest decade**, and every high-scoring excerpt provided comes from the 1950s. The **1960s also retain a relatively high mean**, though much lower than the 1950s. From the 1970s onward, the scores fall to a low, relatively flat range. This suggests that the pole indexes a visual-commercial style associated with early television advertising: studio-bound, formal, demonstrative, and graphically explicit.

The diachronic tendency is therefore one of **strong early concentration followed by decline**. The pole does not merely identify “old commercials” in a chronological sense; rather, it captures a cluster of visual-discursive practices that were especially common in early television advertising.

## Lexical evidence from the loadings

The loading words form several coherent semantic clusters.

### 1. Formal studio address and presenter-led staging

Highly loaded words include:

- **presenter** (.66)  
- **formal** (.51)  
- **studio** (.51)  
- **stage** (.40)  
- **audience** (.56)  
- **earnest** (.55)  
- **address** (.34)  
- **posture** (.37)  
- **poise** (.66)  
- **pleasant** (.43)  
- **clean-shaven** (.51)

These words point to commercials organised around a visible human presenter or performer, often facing the viewer directly in a controlled studio or stage environment. The discourse is not casual or documentary-like; it is controlled, upright, polished, and explicitly addressed to an audience.

The presence of **poise**, **posture**, **formal**, **earnest**, and **pleasant** suggests a mode of persuasion based on composure and trustworthiness. The presenter is visually disciplined: standing upright, smiling, gesturing deliberately, and introducing or endorsing the product.

### 2. Elegance, grooming, and gendered visual refinement

Another cluster concerns personal appearance and refined styling:

- **neckline** (.77)  
- **graceful** (.69)  
- **curl** (.65)  
- **comb** (.61)  
- **earring** (.49)  
- **hairstyle** (.49)  
- **bodice** (.54)  
- **necklace** (.38)  
- **ruffle** (.42)  
- **sheer** (.30)

This vocabulary evokes carefully arranged bodies, especially women’s bodies, as part of the product display. Hair, dress, jewelry, neckline, curls, and posture are prominent. The commercial visual field is therefore not only about the product but also about an idealised mid-century style of elegance and bodily presentation.

This is visible in the Revlon, Satin-Set, Chevrolet, and Dial examples, where women’s coiffure, jewelry, neckline, dress, and poised gestures are repeatedly described. The product is embedded in a broader visual discourse of refinement, attractiveness, and composed femininity.

### 3. On-screen lettering, script, and graphic copy

A very strong part of the pole concerns visible writing and typographic display:

- **writing** (.71)  
- **script** (.38)  
- **cursive** (.48)  
- **capital** (.46)  
- **underline** (.50)  
- **phrase** (.41)  
- **marker** (.37)  
- **message** (.37)  
- **superimpose** (.55)  
- **emphasis** (.60)

These loadings show that f7 positive is also a discourse of **visual copywriting**: slogans, prices, product names, handwritten phrases, block capitals, cursive brand scripts, and superimposed messages. In early commercials, the screen often functions like a moving print advertisement, with large lettering integrated into the visual composition.

The examples strongly support this. We see:

- “Save 35¢ on every LIPSTICK”
- “BUILT-IN SPARE REFILL”
- “2 POINTS”
- “No need to run out of ink — Ever”
- “BULOVA Miss America”
- “KENTILE EASY CLEAN VINYL”
- “GREASEPROOF”
- “The most AMAZING FOOD WRAP ever developed”
- “Smartest of the Smart Cars”

The loadings and examples together indicate a pole where the visual description repeatedly includes **text as image**.

### 4. Animation, drawing, and stylised graphic effects

The pole also includes visual-graphic terms:

- **animation** (.36)  
- **drawing** (.60)  
- **draw** (.49)  
- **star-like** (.63)

These terms suggest a highly designed visual mode: cartoon mascots, graphic transitions, decorative backgrounds, sparkling highlights, hearts, stars, flowing lines, and animated demonstrations. This is especially clear in the Paper Mate, DeSoto, Muntz TV, and Dial examples, where animation and graphic illustration are central to the commercial’s structure.

This supports interpreting the pole as more than presenter-led realism. It includes a **graphic-theatrical display style**, where products are framed by animated figures, handwritten lines, symbolic shapes, and visual effects.

These loadings point to commercials that show the product as a physical object with parts, surfaces, shapes, and mechanisms. The object is turned, opened, fitted together, pointed at, or shown in close-up. This is evident in the Revlon lipstick refill system, Paper Mate pen refill, Bulova expansion bracelet, Kentile vinyl tiles, and Saran-Wrap demonstrations.

The product is not merely consumed or used in a naturalistic scene. It is **presented, dissected, rotated, and explained visually**.

This reinforces the decade distribution: the pole is not only formally old-fashioned but materially associated with mid-century domestic and consumer design.

## Evidence from the excerpts

The excerpts confirm the interpretation suggested by the loadings.

### Revlon lipstick commercial

The highest-scoring example contains many defining features of the pole: a **formal studio set**, male and female presenters, elegant dress and jewelry, a scripted brand sign, product close-ups, gloved-hand demonstration, and on-screen price lettering. Loading words such as **neckline, flowing, graceful, poise, presenter, formal, studio, earring, hairstyle, inner, outer, capital, script, posture, base, fit, style, backdrop** all appear in the example.

This text exemplifies the pole very clearly: advertising as a staged, elegant demonstration in which product form and social poise are visually intertwined.

### Paper Mate pen commercial

The Paper Mate example foregrounds animation, writing, cursive script, underlining, hearts, and a demonstrative product mechanism. The cartoon pigs riding the pen, the pen writing “NEW,” the refill being removed and reinserted, and the handwritten phrase “No need to run out of ink — Ever” all align with the loading words **animation, drawing, heart, writing, cursive, underline, inner, sleek, rotate, script, phrase**.

This supports the interpretation of the pole as one where graphic writing and animated product explanation are central.

### Chevrolet commercial

The Chevrolet example adds a theatrical performance dimension: a poised female singer on a stage-like set, direct address to the audience, formal styling, and scenic automobile display. Loading words such as **graceful, poise, sweeping, audience, earnest, formal, studio, stage, posture, address, backdrop, profile, convertible** are activated.

Here the product is displayed through performance and scenic aspiration. The car is not only transportation but part of a staged visual spectacle of elegance, travel, and modern desirability.

### Bulova watch commercial

The Bulova example shows a presenter in a staged retail setting, close-up product handling, rotating the watch, demonstrating the expansion bracelet, and overlaying product name and price. This directly reflects **presenter, link, cursive, capital, script, position, posture, form, rotate, profile, attention**.

The discourse is one of controlled product inspection: the commodity is carefully held, turned, displayed, named, and priced.

### Kentile flooring commercial

The Kentile example extends the pole into domestic interiors and practical demonstration. It includes clean staged rooms, bold lettering, installation demonstration, repeated product material labels, and a final script title. The loading words **writing, star-like, telephone, easy, capital, tidy, script, repeat, base, fit, style, attention** reflect this visual mode.

Even though this example is less presenter-centred, it still fits the broader pole because it relies on composed display, visible product surfaces, practical demonstration, and typographic reinforcement.

### DeSoto and Muntz TV commercials

These examples show the animated side of the pole. Both use stylised cartoon figures acting as theatrical presenters, with broad gestures, direct attention to the product, and transitions between animation and live-action product display. Loading words such as **animation, drawing, formal, sweeping, presenter, posture, audience, address, attention, indicate** are central.

These commercials show that the pole includes not only live presenters but also animated presenter-surrogates who perform the same function: directing viewer attention and making the product visually legible.

## Discourse dimension encoded by the pole

Taken together, f7 positive can be interpreted as a dimension of:

> **formal, staged, mid-century product presentation, combining poised human or animated presenters, decorative studio settings, explicit on-screen writing, and careful visual demonstration of product form and function.**

The discourse is highly **presentational**. Products are brought forward, named, pointed to, opened, rotated, fitted together, compared, priced, and framed by lettering or graphics. People in these commercials often serve as presenters, demonstrators, performers, or elegant embodiments of the product’s promise. Visual persuasion depends on controlled display, not on immersive narrative realism.

The pole also carries a clear **aesthetic discourse of refinement**: graceful posture, formal clothing, styled hair, jewelry, clean studio lighting, decorative backgrounds, and polished product surfaces. This is particularly visible in cosmetics, hair products, watches, cars, soaps, and domestic products.

At the same time, the pole includes a **graphic-copy discourse**, where written language is visually central. Cursive scripts, block capitals, underlines, price cards, product names, and superimposed claims are part of the image itself. The commercial visual field often resembles an animated magazine advertisement or showroom display.

## Preferred label

The best concise label is:

> **Formal Studio Presentation and Product Display**

A fuller label could be:

> **Mid-Century Formal Presentation, Graphic Copy, and Demonstrative Product Display**

This fuller version better captures the three main components of the pole:

1. **Formal presentation** — presenters, studio, stage, posture, poise, direct address.  
2. **Graphic copy** — writing, cursive, script, capitals, underlining, superimposed messages.  
3. **Product display/demonstration** — inner/outer parts, base, fit, rotate, pointer, form, style, close-up handling.

The decade scores indicate that this dimension is especially associated with the **1950s**, remains visible in the **1960s**, and then declines substantially across later decades.

## Interpretation of Factor f8 (negative pole)

Factor f8 negative appears to encode a discourse of **product efficacy demonstrated through visible, material proof**. High-scoring texts at this pole foreground products being tested, applied, sprayed, wiped, punctured, frozen, compared, displayed, or otherwise made visually verifiable. The pole is strongly associated with **demonstrational advertising**, especially mid-century commercials that make the product’s action legible through close-ups, staged trials, domestic surfaces, technical setups, containers, sprays, particles, stains, fibers, and packaging.

The clearest decade driver is the **1960s**, which has the lowest mean score for this negative pole and supplies all the illustrative high-scoring excerpts. The 1950s also score relatively low, suggesting continuity with earlier television-commercial demonstration styles, while later decades are less central to this pole.

### Possible labels

1. **“Demonstrated Product Efficacy and Material Proof”**  
2. **“Mid-Century Product Demonstration Discourse”**  
3. **“Visible Performance Testing and Domestic-Technological Proof”**  
4. **“Proof-by-Demonstration Advertising”**  
5. **“Applied Product Action: Sprays, Surfaces, Stains, and Tests”**

The strongest overall label would be:

> **Demonstrated Product Efficacy and Material Proof**

This label captures both the lexical profile and the example texts.

## Justification from the factor loadings

The loadings cluster around several related semantic fields.

### 1. Demonstration, testing, and staged proof

The most central discourse is explicit demonstration. Relevant loadings include:

- **demonstration**
- **dramatization**
- **example**
- **directed**
- **aim**
- **side-by-side**
- **magnify**
- **measure**
- **lab**
- **states**
- **underline**

These words suggest a visual rhetoric in which the commercial does not merely show a product but stages a proof: pointing, aiming, comparing, magnifying, dramatizing, or presenting an example. The commercial visual description becomes organized around observable evidence.

This is strongly reflected in the excerpts: the Faultless starch spray is shown settling on fabric fibers; Jubilee wax is tested with a spill; Zerex is punctured to show anti-leak action; the B.F. Goodrich hose is frozen in ice and then removed still flexible; IBM’s Selectric mechanism is shown in mechanical close-up; Sylvania flashcubes are demonstrated through repeated flashes.

### 2. Product substances, particles, sprays, and surface effects

A second major loading cluster concerns the physical behavior of product substances:

- **powder**
- **fiber**
- **particle**
- **formula**
- **speckle**
- **splatter**
- **aerosol**
- **smear**
- **suds**
- **wax**
- **crystal**
- **mist**
- **drip**
- **remove**
- **squeeze**

These are highly material terms. They point to products acting on surfaces or in the air: sprays, powders, droplets, stains, smears, suds, wax coatings, particles, and fibers. The pole therefore seems especially concerned with **making invisible or small-scale product action visible**.

In the Faultless starch excerpt, the spray is represented as “bright little dots” and later as settling into woven cloth, directly matching **aerosol**, **particle**, and **fiber**. In the Jubilee Kitchen Wax excerpt, liquid is poured onto the counter and behaves differently on the treated side, matching **wax**, **smear**, **protect**, and **demonstration**. In the Raid example, the aerosol cloud and “clean-smelling formula” make the product’s action visible as mist.

### 3. Domestic surfaces, cleaning, laundry, and household materials

Many loadings refer to household interiors and surfaces:

- **living**
- **linen**

- **backyard**

This indicates that much of the demonstration discourse is located in practical domestic environments. Products are shown acting on counters, floors, fabrics, appliances, laundry, carpets, or outdoor household items.

The examples strongly support this. Faultless starch is used at an ironing board and closet; Jubilee is used on kitchen counters and refrigerators; Mr. Clean is shown on floors, laundry, bathtubs, walls, and appliance panels; Raid is sprayed in a home interior; the B.F. Goodrich garden hose is tested for cold-weather flexibility. Even the more technological examples, such as IBM and Sylvania, retain a product-display and use-demonstration structure.

### 4. Strength, protection, resistance, and performance under stress

Another cluster concerns toughness and protective performance:

- **tough**
- **powerful**
- **protect**
- **workout**
- **muscle**
- **forceful**
- **cold**
- **scratch**
- **irregular**

This suggests products being represented as capable, durable, protective, or effective under difficult conditions. The visual discourse is not only “here is the product,” but “here is the product proving itself against a challenge.”

This is especially visible in the hose commercial, where the product is frozen in ice and then shown coiling with ease; in the Zerex commercial, where a can is punctured and the product stops leaks under pressure; and in the cleaning examples, where dirt, smears, speckles, stains, or spills are removed.

### 5. Packaging, containers, and product display

Finally, the loadings include many words linked to physical product forms:

- **packaging**
- **cylindrical**
- **aerosol**
- **coil**
- **vessel**

The commercials at this pole often culminate in product-pack shots: cans, bottles, boxes, coils, flashcube packs, typewriter components, and sale displays. The product package is not backgrounded; it is visually central and often repeatedly shown with readable labels.

This is evident across the excerpts: Faultless spray-on starch can; Jubilee aerosol can and bottle; Mr. Clean bottle, powder box, cleanser can, detergent box, and soap panel; Rexall product packages; B.F. Goodrich hose packaging; Zerex can and jug; Raid aerosol can; Sylvania flashcube packages.

## Justification from the excerpts

The examples are remarkably consistent. They are not primarily mood-based, lifestyle-based, or narrative commercials. Instead, they are built around **showing how something works**.

### Faultless spray-on starch

The commercial demonstrates starch application through an aerosol spray, with visible droplets, star-like particles, and fabric-level imagery. Loading words include **fiber**, **particle**, **aerosol**, **demonstration**, **iron**, and **deliberate**. This is a prototypical case of material proof: the product’s invisible action on cloth is made visible through particles and fibers.

### Jubilee Kitchen Wax

This excerpt stages a before/after or treated/untreated surface demonstration. The product is sprayed and wiped; a spill is poured; liquid beads on the treated “Jubilee side.” Loading words include **protect**, **aerosol**, **smear**, **wax**, and **demonstration**. The commercial uses controlled surface behavior as evidence of product value.

### Mr. Clean

The Mr. Clean commercial shows multiple cleaning trials: floors, laundry, bathtubs, walls, appliances, speckles, and smudges. It also presents competing product categories such as **powder**, cleanser, detergent, and soap. The loading words **powder**, **speckle**, **demonstration**, and **cylindrical** fit the product-comparison and cleaning-proof structure.

### B.F. Goodrich Everflex hose

This is a dramatic stress test. The hose is frozen inside ice, broken out with force, and then shown coiling easily. Loading words include **packaging**, **coil**, **demonstration**, and **cold**. The product’s claim is visually authenticated through an extreme physical trial.

### Zerex anti-leak

The Zerex commercial uses puncture and pressure to dramatize product performance. A pointed tool is aimed at the can; liquid sprays; the leak stops or weakens. Loading words include **aim**, **demonstration**, and **cylindrical**. This is a clear instance of technical proof through staged damage and visible liquid behavior.

### Raid Flying Insect Killer

The Raid example combines animation and live action but still centers on product action: an aerosol can sprays mist, and on-screen text announces a “patented clean-smelling formula.” Loading words include **formula**, **aerosol**, **wax**, and **aim**. The commercial visualizes the product through spray direction, mist, and labeled formula.

### IBM Selectric and Sylvania flashcubes

These examples show that the pole is not limited to cleaning products. IBM demonstrates the typewriter’s mechanical element; Sylvania demonstrates flashcubes and flashbulbs. Both are visually instructional and product-centered, using close-ups, component display, and repeated demonstrations of function. Their loading words, especially **demonstration**, **cylindrical**, and **aim**, fit the broader discourse of product mechanism and performance proof.

## Decade pattern and diachronic tendency

For the negative pole, the relevant decades are those with the **lowest mean f8 scores**. The lowest mean is in the:

- **1960s: 0.45**
- followed by the **1950s: 0.63**
- then **1990s: 0.69**
- and **2020s: 0.91**

The examples are all from the **1960s**, making that decade the clearest driver of this pole. This suggests that the negative pole captures a visual-commercial style especially characteristic of 1960s advertising: explicit product demonstrations, household tests, close-up evidence, and visible claims of efficacy.

The relatively low 1950s score suggests that this discourse may have roots in early television advertising, when commercials often borrowed from live demonstration, presenter-led selling, and print-style product display. The 1960s appear to intensify or crystallize this mode through more elaborate visual proof: animated diagrams, product comparisons, spray effects, laboratory-like tests, and staged domestic trials.

Later decades do not appear to drive this pole as strongly. This implies a diachronic movement away from this highly explicit demonstration style as the dominant visual discourse, although the lower scores in some later decades suggest that product demonstration remains available as a recurring advertising resource.

---

## Summary interpretation

Factor f8 negative represents a discourse of **demonstrated product efficacy through material, visible proof**. It is characterized by demonstrations, product tests, domestic surfaces, sprays, particles, stains, fibers, packaging, containers, and claims of protection, toughness, or technical performance. The pole is driven especially by 1960s commercials, where products are repeatedly shown acting on the world: cleaning, coating, spraying, protecting, resisting cold, stopping leaks, flashing, typing, or otherwise proving their utility before the viewer’s eyes.

Best label:

> **Demonstrated Product Efficacy and Material Proof**

## Interpretation of Factor f1 positive pole

The positive pole of Factor 1 is best interpreted as a discourse of **mobile exterior space**, especially **road-based, vehicle-centred, cinematic movement through urban, suburban, and natural landscapes**. It is strongly driven by commercials whose visual descriptions foreground cars, roads, drivers, routes, city streets, highways, landscapes, weather, and wide moving shots.

### Possible labels for this pole

1. **Cinematic Automobility and Roadscape Movement**  
2. **Exterior Mobility through Urban and Natural Landscapes**  
3. **Vehicle-Centred Travel, Route, and Location Montage**  
4. **Mobile Roadscape Advertising Discourse**  
5. **Cars, Cities, Roads, and Open-Space Movement**

The strongest label would be:

> **Cinematic Automobility and Roadscape Movement**

This captures both the dominant automotive vocabulary in the loadings and the visual organisation of the high-scoring examples: vehicles moving through roads, streets, cities, hills, cliffs, wet surfaces, rural landscapes, and stylised spatial montages.

## Justification from the factor loadings

The highest-loading words point very clearly to a visual field of **driving, vehicles, roads, and exterior movement**:

- Core automobility terms: **drive, driver, road, sedan, vehicle, suv, car, hood, truck, pickup, ride, traffic, bus**
- Route and movement terms: **travel, pass, approach, route, move, continue, run, past**
- Built-environment terms: **urban, street, city, downtown, building, skyline, entrance, pavement, residential, suburban, industrial**
- Open-landscape and terrain terms: **hill, rock, cliff, canyon, rural, grass, sky, cloud, wind, mist, snow, water**
- Camera/viewing terms: **aerial, distant, distance, exterior, view, wide, overhead, angle, montage**
- Commercial end-frame/legal terms: **website, legal, disclaimer, lease, information, safety**

The loadings therefore combine three main semantic domains:

1. **Vehicles and driving**
   - *drive, driver, road, sedan, vehicle, suv, car, hood, truck, pickup, traffic*
2. **Exterior spatial settings**
   - *urban, city, street, suburban, residential, rural, hill, cliff, canyon, sky, cloud*
3. **Dynamic visual presentation**
   - *aerial, distant, pass, approach, move, continue, montage, wide, angle, view*

This is not merely a “car” factor in a narrow product-category sense, although automobile advertising is clearly central. More broadly, the pole indexes commercials whose visual grammar is organised around **movement through space**: cars moving along roads, pedestrians and vehicles passing through streets, shots shifting from city to countryside, and wide or aerial views establishing routes and landscapes.

## Justification from the example excerpts

The high-scoring examples strongly illustrate this interpretation. Most of them are from the **2000s**, and many are explicitly automotive or mobility-oriented.

### Vehicle and road-centred commercials

Several examples are straightforward car or vehicle commercials:

- **Volvo in stormy coastal terrain**  
  The second excerpt shows a Volvo moving through rain, wet roads, cliffs, rocks, waves, mist, and open coastal landscapes. The loading words include **drive, driver, road, aerial, vehicle, hill, street, car, rock, wind, approach, cliff, wet, cloud, sky, mist, view, move, wide, open**.  
  This is a prototypical case of the pole: vehicle motion, hostile weather, terrain, and cinematic exterior space.

- **Mobil motor oil and stylised driving environments**  
  The third excerpt presents a silver sedan moving through suburban streets, mountains, cliffs, tunnels, winding roads, and a miniature-like route system. Loading words include **drive, driver, road, aerial, sedan, vehicle, street, car, entrance, pass, cliff, suburban, traffic, route, move, structure, wide, open**.  
  Here, the product is motor oil, but the visual discourse is still road-based automobility.

- **Mercedes-Benz C-Class lifestyle travel montage**  
  The fifth excerpt links the car to city streets, skylines, waterfronts, bridges, beaches, and open roads. Loading words include **drive, driver, road, travel, sedan, street, car, city, building, skyline, pedestrian, sky, montage, move, wide, open**.  
  This example shows the aspirational side of the pole: mobility becomes lifestyle, leisure, urban sophistication, and freedom of movement.

- **BMW SUV split-screen terrain montage**  
  The sixth excerpt is organised around an SUV travelling through different surfaces and conditions: wet roads, snow, dry terrain, mountains, grass, and open routes. The split-screen design visually multiplies driving environments. Loading words include **drive, driver, road, vehicle, suv, car, distant, approach, exterior, wet, cloud, sky, grass, route, montage, move, different, wide, open, angle**.

- **Ford Focus mountain-road driving**  
  The tenth excerpt shows a car on winding rural mountain roads, with close-ups of wheels, badges, roads, sky, and drivers. Loading words include **drive, driver, road, vehicle, hill, car, wind, exterior, model, cloud, sky, rural, badge, move, continue, wide, open, angle**.

These examples make clear that the pole encodes a highly visual, kinetic form of advertising: the product or brand is associated with **movement, control, terrain, scenery, and exterior expansiveness**.

### Non-automotive products using automobility and location movement

Importantly, some high-scoring examples are not simply car advertisements, which confirms that the factor is broader than “automobile commercials.”

- **Tide/Downy laundry journey**  
  The first excerpt begins in a domestic laundry room but then shifts outside: a woman carries laundry to a car, the car drives through city, suburban, residential, and rural spaces, and the final product shot appears in open grass under a blue sky. Loading words include **urban, drive, driver, road, vehicle, hill, street, car, city, suburban, residential, cloud, sky, rural, grass, fence, route, view, website, move, carry, continue, wide, open**.  
  The product is laundry detergent, but the visual discourse is still one of **journey, exterior movement, and spatial expansion**.

- **Computer Associates chickens in the city**  
  The fourth excerpt contains no central consumer vehicle product, but it is rich in urban exterior space: wet city streets, tall buildings, taxis, buses, subway entrances, plazas, skylines, and overhead city views. Loading words include **urban, road, aerial, vehicle, street, city, entrance, building, skyline, wet, pavement, night, bus, canyon, view, run, move, wide, open, angle**.  
  This supports an interpretation involving **urban spatial spectacle and movement**, not just cars.

- **Dr Pepper street-neighbourhood scene**  
  The eighth excerpt foregrounds an urban street community: crosswalks, storefronts, brick apartment buildings, pedestrians, an ice-cream truck, a taxi driver, fences, sidewalks, and people moving through the street. Loading words include **urban, driver, street, city, entrance, truck, building, pedestrian, outside, fence, move, carry, brick, wide, open, nearby**.  
  Again, the pole includes commercials that construct products through **public exterior environments and street-level movement**.

Thus, the examples show that the pole captures a discourse of **commercials as moving through places**. Automotive advertising is the most obvious manifestation, but the dimension also includes urban spectacle, pedestrian street life, route-based product stories, and exterior location montage.

## Decades driving the pole

The decade scores show a clear diachronic pattern:

| Decade | Mean f1 score |
|---|---:|
| 1950s | 2.90 |
| 1960s | 4.28 |
| 1970s | 3.97 |
| 1980s | 3.50 |
| 1990s | 4.31 |
| 2000s | 9.28 |
| 2010s | 8.54 |
| 2020s | 8.49 |

The pole is most strongly associated with the **2000s**, followed closely by the **2010s** and **2020s**. The earlier decades score much lower, with means between roughly 3 and 4.3. The major shift occurs between the **1990s** and the **2000s**, where the mean more than doubles.

This suggests that from the 2000s onward, the visual descriptions in the commercial subcorpus become much more strongly characterised by:

- exterior settings,
- road and vehicle imagery,
- urban and suburban movement,
- landscape travel,
- cinematic montage,
- wide/aerial shots,
- weather and terrain variation,
- route-based visual storytelling,
- end-card features such as websites, legal text, and model/price information.

The high-scoring examples are all from the **2000s**, which fits the decade table: this decade appears to be the strongest driver of the pole. However, the sustained high means in the **2010s** and **2020s** indicate that this is not a one-decade anomaly. Rather, the pole seems to represent a broader late-modern advertising style that emerges strongly in the 2000s and remains prominent thereafter.

## Discourse encoded at this pole

This pole encodes a discourse in which commercials present brands through **mobility, spatial range, and cinematic exterior movement**. Products are placed in relation to roads, routes, vehicles, cityscapes, landscapes, weather, and movement across locations.

The dominant visual meanings include:

- **freedom of movement**: open roads, wide landscapes, travel, routes;
- **control and performance**: drivers, vehicles, curves, wet roads, rugged terrain;
- **urban modernity**: streets, skylines, buildings, downtown spaces, traffic;
- **lifestyle mobility**: bridges, beaches, city streets, leisure destinations;
- **cinematic dynamism**: aerial views, montage, wide shots, motion blur, changing angles;
- **environmental adaptability**: rain, wind, snow, cliffs, hills, rural and urban settings;
- **brand as movement through the world**: even non-vehicle products are embedded in journeys or public exterior spaces.

In short, Factor f1 positive represents a visual-commercial discourse of **moving brands through exterior worlds**. The pole is especially characteristic of the 2000s and remains strong in the 2010s and 2020s, suggesting a diachronic move toward more cinematic, spatially expansive, mobile advertising visuals.

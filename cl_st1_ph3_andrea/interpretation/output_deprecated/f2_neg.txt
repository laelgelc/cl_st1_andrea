## Factor f2 (negative): discourse dimension interpretation

The negative pole of Factor 2 appears to encode a discourse of **archival analogue commercial imagery**, especially visually degraded or soft-focus product advertising from the earlier television-commercial era. It is most strongly associated with the **1970s** in the example set, and more generally with the **earliest decades** in the mean-score table, since the lowest decade scores occur in the 1970s, 1950s, and 1960s. The pole becomes progressively less characteristic over time, with the 2000s, 2010s, and especially 2020s moving away from it.

### Possible labels for this pole

1. **“Archival analogue advertising aesthetics”**  
2. **“Soft-focus vintage product vignettes”**  
3. **“Degraded archival commercial footage and end-card branding”**  
4. **“1970s-style analogue montage and product-display discourse”**  
5. **“Retro commercial imagery: blur, exposure, scenery, and brand cards”**

The strongest overall label would be:

> **Archival analogue advertising aesthetics**

because the pole combines degraded visual-quality lexis, vintage commercial settings, product/end-card terminology, and example texts dominated by older, archive-watermarked, blurred, dark, or double-exposed footage.

## Justification from the factor loadings

The loadings are heavily concentrated in words describing **analogue image quality and archival visual degradation**:

- **blurry**
- **grainy**
- **exposure**
- **underexpose**
- **hazy**
- **indistinct**
- **distant**
- **double**
- **dissolve**
- **archival**

These terms point to footage that is not visually crisp or digitally clean. The pole therefore seems to capture commercials whose visual descriptions mention soft focus, poor visibility, exposure problems, layered images, archive watermarks, and older film/video textures.

A second lexical cluster relates to **older product-display and brand-identification practices**:

- **advertisement**
- **classic**
- **identity**
- **division**
- **ford**
- **automobile**
- **bumper**
- **whitewall**
- **lease**

These items suggest conventional commercial imagery involving cars, corporate divisions, product identity, and old-style branding/end cards. The presence of **Ford**, **automobile**, **bumper**, and **whitewall** gives the pole a particularly vintage automotive-commercial flavour, though the examples show that the dimension is not limited to cars.

A third cluster evokes **scenic or lifestyle settings**, often outdoors or in leisure environments:

- **sea**
- **deck**
- **cliff**
- **hill**
- **rock**
- **ledge**
- **resort**
- **poolside**
- **farm**
- **pavement**
- **daytime**
- **restaurant**
- **diner**

These words suggest older commercials using recognisable lifestyle backdrops: beaches, roads, diners, resorts, city streets, farms, or scenic landscapes. In the examples, this appears in the beach-horseback wine commercial, the Hershey’s city/waterfront scenes, carnival scenes, street scenes, and car imagery.

A fourth group involves **surface detail, texture, and small product objects**:

- **splash**
- **rough**
- **grime**
- **speck**
- **chip**
- **straw**
- **utensil**
- **basin**
- **sock**
- **deodorant**
- **application**
- **taste**
- **cold**

These items point toward concrete, material, product-oriented visual description: household goods, food, cleaning, clothing, bodily products, and small objects. This supports an interpretation of the pole as commercial-product visuality rather than, for example, narrative drama alone.

## Justification from the example excerpts

The examples strongly reinforce the interpretation of this pole as **archival, analogue, and vintage-commercial in visual style**.

Several excerpts explicitly mention poor image quality, dark frames, archive marks, blur, or exposure problems. For example:

- f2_neg_001 begins with “a very dark, indistinct frame” and includes “archival watermarking,” “motion blur,” and “double exposure.”
- f2_neg_002 describes a “dark and somewhat blurry” sports montage with “motion blur, double exposure, and bright light flares.”
- f2_neg_008 describes children’s faces in “soft-focus,” “grainy,” “washed out,” and heavily blurred close-up imagery.
- f2_neg_004 begins with a “nearly black frame” where only archive watermarks are visible.

These examples directly instantiate the loading words **archival**, **blurry**, **grainy**, **exposure**, **double**, and **indistinct**. The visual discourse is not just about what appears in the commercials, but about the condition and style of the recorded image itself.

The examples also show a recurring pattern of **brief lifestyle vignette followed by product or logo end card**:

- The HFC commercial ends with a static title card: “People use our money to get the most out of life.”
- The PRO-Keds commercial ends with a black end card and slogan: “When we let you down, we let you down easy.”
- The Ford commercial ends with a graphic layout reading “FORD FAIRMONT FUTURA” and “FORD DIVISION.”
- The Clorox commercial ends with a large CLOROX logo.
- The Dixie Cups commercial ends with a centred “DIXIE CUPS” logo.
- The Chicken of the Sea example ends with a can product shot.
- The L’eggs example ends with a package shot and slogan.

This supports a label involving **vintage product-display discourse** or **end-card branding**. The commercials are visually structured around short scenes or montages that culminate in a product, slogan, logo, or corporate identity card.

The examples also reflect the scenic/lifestyle component of the loadings. The **sea** loading appears in the Chicken of the Sea example and in the beach/wine commercial. The Hershey’s examples use city streets, waterfront views, carnival spaces, and performance settings. The PRO-Keds example uses athletic movement and product close-ups. The Ford example uses a dark studio car display. These are not highly realistic documentary descriptions so much as compact, stylised commercial tableaux.

## Decades driving the pole

The mean decade scores indicate that this pole is associated most strongly with the **lowest-scoring decades**:

- **1970s:** 1.34  
- **1950s:** 1.46  
- **1960s:** 1.47  
- **1980s:** 1.97  

The 1970s have the lowest mean score and all ten high-scoring negative-pole examples come from the 1970s. This makes the 1970s the clearest driver of the pole in the excerpt set. However, the 1950s and 1960s are also close to the 1970s in the mean-score table, so the broader diachronic pattern suggests that this pole belongs to an **early-to-mid television-commercial visual regime**, rather than to the 1970s alone.

From the 1990s onward, the mean scores rise steadily:

- 1990s: 2.35  
- 2000s: 3.56  
- 2010s: 5.39  
- 2020s: 6.70  

This suggests a diachronic movement away from the negative-pole discourse. The pole is therefore characteristic of older visual-commercial material: analogue footage, archive transfers, soft or degraded images, conventional product cards, and compact lifestyle/product vignettes.

---

## Overall interpretation

The negative pole of Factor 2 represents a discourse dimension of:

> **archival analogue commercial visuality, characterised by blurry or grainy footage, exposure problems, double exposures and dissolves, scenic lifestyle vignettes, vintage product displays, and logo/slogan end cards.**

It is especially visible in the 1970s examples, where commercials often begin with dark or indistinct archival footage, move through short lifestyle scenes or stylised montages, and finish with a static product, brand, or slogan card. The loading words show that this is not merely a “1970s” factor, but a broader lexical pattern involving analogue image quality, older advertising conventions, product identity, cars, scenery, and material consumer objects. Diachronically, the pole is strongest in the earlier decades and weakens markedly in later decades.

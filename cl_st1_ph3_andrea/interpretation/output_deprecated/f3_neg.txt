## Interpretation of Factor f3 (negative pole)

### Preferred label  
**Everyday household utility and practical consumer-service discourse**

### Other possible labels  
- **Domestic maintenance, care, and provisioning**
- **Functional home-and-service consumerism**
- **Practical household problem-solving and product display**
- **Everyday suburban/domestic utility discourse**

## Rationale

This negative pole appears to encode a discourse of **ordinary practical consumption**: products and services are visually situated as aids for managing the home, maintaining cleanliness, caring for bodies and families, shopping, delivery, mobility, and everyday domestic or suburban life. The pole is not primarily about spectacle or lifestyle fantasy; it is more strongly associated with **useful goods, household spaces, local service environments, and product-focused demonstrations or displays**.

### 1. Lexical loadings: home, maintenance, shopping, service, and everyday utility

The strongest loading words point to several overlapping semantic fields.

#### a. Domestic and suburban settings  
Words such as **porch, lawn, residential, suburban, neighborhood, walkway, house, home, staircase, outside, poolside, resort** suggest visual scenes located in or around ordinary living spaces. These are not abstract brand worlds but recognizable environments: homes, yards, streets, porches, poolsides, and neighbourhood settings.

#### b. Household maintenance and cleaning  
Loadings including **detergent, grime, wipe, remove, trash, utensil, solid, supply** point to a discourse of cleaning, repair, maintenance, and practical household management. These words frame the advertised product as solving everyday material problems: dirt, laundry, waste, mess, or domestic upkeep.

#### c. Care, health, safety, and family life  
Words such as **medical, help, warning, safety, baby, pet, life** suggest products or services connected with care, protection, bodily comfort, family responsibility, and risk management. The word **help**, one of the strongest loadings, is especially important: the product is positioned as a helper in ordinary life.

#### d. Shopping, delivery, and consumer logistics  
The pole also includes **shopping, store, customer, pay, delivery, pickup, rack, stock, cardboard, truck, receive, employee**. These words bring in the practical infrastructure of consumption: shelves, stores, supply chains, purchases, deliveries, pickups, packaging, and customers. This broadens the pole from domestic space alone to the whole everyday system through which household goods are obtained.

#### e. Contemporary practical technology  
Modern terms such as **app, smartphone, mobile, user, page, photo, respond, participate** indicate that this discourse is not only an early-television phenomenon. In later decades, the same practical-consumer orientation can appear through app-based shopping, delivery, mobile interaction, user participation, or digital service interfaces.

Overall, the lexical loadings describe a world of **homes, services, chores, care, shopping, mobility, and practical assistance**.

## 2. Example excerpts: early product demonstration and utilitarian pack-shot advertising

The example excerpts are all from the **1950s**, which is the decade with the lowest mean score and therefore the strongest association with this negative pole. They show an early-TV version of the discourse: black-and-white, product-centred commercials that often move from a simple domestic or staged scene to a clear product display or brand end card.

The explicitly marked loading words in the examples are relatively sparse — especially **detergent**, **help**, and **various** — but the visual content strongly supports the broader interpretation of practical household utility.

### Domestic cleaning and laundry

Several examples centre on laundry, fabric, appliances, and detergents.

- In **f3_neg_001**, the commercial ends with a box of **Dash** detergent and the claim:  
  **“Dash Gets clothes cleaner.”**  
  The loading word **detergent** appears directly. The visual discourse is one of household cleaning efficacy: the product helps produce clean laundry.

- **f3_neg_003** presents women inspecting fabric in a bedroom/laundry-related setting and then shows **Ivory Snow** being poured, ending with a product display. Even though no f3-negative loading word is explicitly marked for this excerpt, the scene fits the pole’s larger semantic field of laundry, domestic care, fabric, purity, and household maintenance.

- **f3_neg_005** focuses on a washing machine or laundry appliance. The sequence moves from indistinct appliance imagery to a final studio-style product shot. This aligns closely with the loading cluster around home utility, cleaning, and practical domestic technology.

### Health and remedial help

- In **f3_neg_002**, the commercial ends with a product display for **TRIACTIN chewable tablets** and the text:  
  **“Helps Stop Excess Acid Upset.”**  
  The loading word **help** appears here. This is a clear example of the product-as-helper discourse: the visual focus is not on entertainment or narrative complexity but on a remedy for a practical bodily problem.

### Packaged goods, grocery access, and household provisioning

- **f3_neg_006** ends with a display of Pillsbury refrigerated-dough cans and the sign:  
  **“at your grocer’s dairy case.”**  
  This connects the product to everyday shopping and household provisioning. Even where specific loading words are not marked, the commercial visually participates in the same discourse of practical consumer access: here is the product, here is where to find it.

- **f3_neg_004** includes shelves or a retail-style display of packaged items, then a closing card for **Dixie Cup**. Again, the emphasis is on recognizable consumer goods, product availability, packaging, and brand identification.

### Cars, service stations, appliances, and practical modern goods

- **f3_neg_008** shows a car on a road and ends with a **Mobil** service-station sign or display. This relates to the loading field of **auto, truck, pickup, utility**, and everyday mobility/service infrastructure.

- **f3_neg_007** ends with an **RCA Victor** promotional end card, while **f3_neg_010** features a woman presenting or examining a large appliance-like object. These examples show mid-century consumer modernity through practical durable goods, appliances, and brand trust.

Taken together, the excerpts illustrate a visual style in which commercials often culminate in **static product displays, packaging shots, brand cards, or direct product claims**. The dominant discourse is practical and utilitarian: the product cleans, helps, supplies, relieves, stores, cooks, washes, or supports everyday living.

## 3. Decade pattern: strongest in the 1950s, with later partial returns

For a negative pole, the relevant decades are those with the **lowest mean Factor 3 scores**. The lowest mean is:

- **1950s: 2.00**

The next relatively low-scoring decades are:

- **2010s: 2.33**
- **1970s: 2.42**
- **2020s: 2.80**
- **1960s: 2.81**
- **1980s: 2.88**

The 1990s have the highest mean score, so they are least associated with this pole.

The decade pattern suggests that this discourse is especially characteristic of the **1950s visual-commercial subcorpus**, where early television advertising frequently relied on clear product demonstrations, domestic interiors, household goods, appliances, and closing pack shots. The example set confirms this strongly: all the supplied high-scoring pole examples are from the 1950s.

However, the loading list also contains many words that point to **later forms of the same practical-consumer discourse**, especially **app, smartphone, mobile, delivery, pickup, user, customer, page, photo**. This suggests a diachronic extension: the pole may originate strongly in mid-century product-demonstration advertising, but in later decades similar functional discourse reappears through digital services, delivery platforms, shopping apps, home services, and suburban lifestyle logistics.

So the tendency is not simply “old advertising.” Rather, the pole captures a recurring commercial discourse of **practical everyday utility**, with a particularly strong 1950s manifestation and later rearticulations in app-based, delivery-based, and service-oriented consumer contexts.

## Summary interpretation

Factor f3 negative is best interpreted as a discourse of **everyday household utility and practical consumer service**. Its vocabulary foregrounds homes, lawns, porches, neighbourhoods, cleaning, shopping, delivery, care, safety, vehicles, mobile apps, users, and customers. Its examples show 1950s commercials built around domestic scenes, detergents, appliances, remedies, packaged goods, grocery access, service stations, and final product displays. Diachronically, the pole is strongest in the 1950s but appears to have later echoes in contemporary domestic-service, shopping, mobile, and delivery-oriented advertising.

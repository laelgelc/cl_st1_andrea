## Interpretation of Factor f5 (negative pole)

The negative pole of Factor 5 appears to encode a discourse of **archival, material, and spatially staged visuality**, especially associated with older commercial footage and with descriptions that foreground visible texture, physical surfaces, simple graphic advertising forms, and concrete spatial settings.

This pole is driven most clearly by the **1950s**, which has the lowest mean Factor 5 score in the decade table. The 1990s and the 1960s–1980s sit relatively low compared with the 2000s and especially the 2010s, but the examples supplied for this pole are overwhelmingly from the **1950s**, suggesting that early television-commercial visual style is central to the interpretation.

### Possible labels for this pole

1. **“Archival product-display visuality”**  
2. **“Grainy early-commercial staging and display”**  
3. **“Material-textural advertising scenes”**  
4. **“Static display, surface texture, and spatial setting”**  
5. **“Older filmic/graphic commercial visual discourse”**

The strongest concise label would be:

> **Archival product-display visuality**

This label captures both the decade pattern and the lexical evidence: the pole is associated with older-looking commercial imagery, visible film texture, static advertising cards, product displays, and staged scenes in which surfaces, signs, and spatial arrangements are salient.

---

## Justification from the decade scores

For a negative pole, the relevant decades are those with the **lowest Factor 5 means**. The decade scores show that this pole is strongest in:

- **1950s: 2.65** — clearly the lowest mean score
- **1990s: 3.01**
- **1980s: 3.38**
- **1960s: 3.43**
- **1970s: 3.51**

The **1950s** are the main decade driving the pole. This is also reflected in the example excerpts, all of which come from 1950s commercials. The higher scores in the **2000s, 2010s, and 2020s**, especially the 2010s, suggest that this discourse becomes less characteristic of the visual subcorpus over time, with the pole most strongly anchored in early television-commercial visual description.

Diachronically, the pole therefore suggests an **early-commercial visual mode**: black-and-white footage, static product displays, title cards, simple studio staging, visible film degradation, and direct visual branding. It is not exclusively “1950s” in lexical terms, because the loadings include broader spatial and material vocabulary, but the 1950s are the clearest empirical driver.

## Justification from the factor loadings

The loading words fall into several related semantic clusters.

### 1. Archival/filmic surface texture

Important loadings include:

- **speck**
- **grain**
- **mist**
- **cloudy**
- **grime**
- **substance**
- **bleach**

These words suggest a discourse attentive to the **visible material condition of the image** or of surfaces within the image. In the examples, this is especially clear in descriptions of “visible grain,” “small specks,” “soft and slightly grainy” imagery, dark openings, low contrast, blur, and degraded black-and-white footage.

This supports an interpretation of the pole as involving **material visual texture**, including both filmic artifacts and product/surface textures.

### 2. Static advertising and graphic display

Relevant loadings include:

- **advertisement**
- **header**
- **printed**

The pole therefore includes a strong **advertising-display discourse**, where commercials are described as arrangements of text, packages, brand emblems, and promotional graphics.

This means the pole should not be interpreted only as “film grain” or “old footage.” It also includes a broader tendency to describe **objects and people as positioned in physical space**, often in relation to surfaces, routes, pathways, and staged scenic arrangements.

## Overall interpretation

Factor f5 negative represents a visual discourse in which commercials are described through **material surfaces, archival image qualities, graphic advertising displays, and concrete spatial staging**. The pole is especially associated with **1950s television-commercial visuals**, where black-and-white footage, film grain, specks, title cards, product pack shots, logos, and promotional text are prominent.

The loading words broaden the interpretation beyond the examples: they show that this pole also includes descriptions of physical environments and movement spaces — downtown areas, paths, walkways, concrete, grass, bikes, rides, canyons, and weather conditions. However, the examples indicate that in the high-scoring negative-pole texts, these lexical tendencies are realised especially through **early commercial display practices and archival visual description**.

A suitable label is therefore:

> **Archival product-display visuality**

Alternative labels include:

- **Grainy early-commercial staging**
- **Static product-card and surface-texture discourse**
- **Material-textural advertising display**
- **Older filmic advertising visuals**

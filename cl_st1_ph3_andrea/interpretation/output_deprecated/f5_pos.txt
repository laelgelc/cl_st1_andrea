## Factor f5 (positive pole): interpretation as a discourse dimension

The positive pole of Factor 5 appears to encode a visual discourse of **interpersonal, character-based commercial scenes**, especially scenes built around **adult social interaction, facial reaction, gesture, role identity, and situated dialogue-like exchange**. The pole is strongly driven by the **2010s**, with elevated scores also in the **2000s and 2020s**, suggesting a late-2000s/2010s intensification of commercials that visually operate as short acted vignettes or comic/social scenarios rather than as purely product-display advertising.

### Suggested labels for this pole

Possible labels include:

1. **“Interpersonal Reaction-Shot Vignettes”**  
2. **“Character-Based Social Interaction and Response”**  
3. **“Conversational Role-Play in Everyday/Institutional Settings”**  
4. **“Male-Centred Situational Skit Discourse”**  
5. **“Facial-Expression and Gesture-Driven Commercial Narrative”**

The strongest overall label would be:

> **Interpersonal Reaction-Shot Vignettes**

This label captures both the lexical pattern and the high-scoring examples: these are commercials visually organised around people looking, reacting, gesturing, speaking, addressing one another, and occupying recognisable social roles in restaurants, courtrooms, offices, airports, gyms, homes, cars, and other semi-public settings.

## Evidence from the factor loadings

The loadings combine several mutually reinforcing lexical clusters.

### 1. Facial attention, reaction, and emotional stance

Highly loaded words include:

- **attentive** (.94)  
- **uncertain** (.89)  
- **neutral** (.77)  
- **react** (.68)  
- **serious** (.62)  
- **focused** (.48)  
- **concerned** (.44)  
- **expression** (.44)  
- **surprised** (.43)  
- **reaction** (.42)  
- **amused** (.39)  
- **tense** (.34)  
- **look** (.34)  
- **turn** (.33)  
- **watch** (.60)

This is one of the clearest semantic groupings on the pole. The visuals are not just populated by people; they are described in terms of **how people attend to one another**, how they **look**, **watch**, **turn**, and **react**, and what their facial expressions communicate. The pole therefore seems to capture commercials that depend on visible interpersonal response: surprise, scepticism, concern, amusement, seriousness, uncertainty, or evaluative attention.

This is especially consistent with the examples, where much of the visual narration consists of alternating close-ups of characters watching or reacting to each other.

### 2. Speech-like interaction and embodied communication

Another strong cluster involves communicative action:

- **speak** (.58)  
- **talk** (.53)  
- **gesture** (.52)  
- **address** (.51)  
- **conversation** (.51)  
- **approach** (.43)  
- **gather** (.34)

These words indicate that the visual discourse is frequently organised as **interactional staging**. Even though this is the visual subcorpus, the descriptions repeatedly register visual evidence of speaking, addressing, gesturing, and conversational exchange. The pole therefore reflects commercials in which visual meaning is carried by acted social encounters rather than simply by products, slogans, or abstract imagery.

### 3. Adult male characterisation and visible personal attributes

A very prominent part of the loading profile concerns adult male figures and their appearance:

- **bearded** (.91)  
- **mustache** (.81)  
- **bald** (.75)  
- **male** (.64)  
- **man** (.41)  
- **beard** (.43)  
- **old** (.44)  
- **tan** (.74)

There are also clothing and role-marking terms:

- **vest** (.64)  
- **pant** (.60)  
- **plaid** (.57)  
- **collar** (.56)  
- **uniform** (.54)  
- **tie** (.47)  
- **apron** (.46)  
- **shirt** (.46)  
- **coat** (.43)  
- **suit** (.41)

This suggests a discourse in which commercials rely on **recognisable characters**, often adult men, whose social identity is visually marked through facial hair, age, dress, uniforms, suits, ties, vests, plaid shirts, collars, or occupational clothing. These are not anonymous bodies; they are visually individuated social types: the suited spokesman, the uniformed employee, the bearded driver, the bald bystander, the staff member, the customer, the worker, the diner.

The male-centredness should not be overextended into a claim that women are absent, since the examples do include women. However, the loading profile strongly suggests that this pole is disproportionately associated with **male-presenting character figures and male-coded visual description**.

### 4. Social, service, and semi-public settings

The setting vocabulary also matters:

- **diner** (.85)  
- **restaurant** (.82)  
- **employee** (.69)  
- **staff** (.66)  
- **customer** (.52)  
- **seating** (.48)  
- **shop** (.48)  
- **table** (.35)  
- **worker** (.40)  
- **industrial** (.39)  
- **shelving** (.39)

These terms point to visually specific settings where people interact in recognisable social roles: restaurants, diners, shops, airports, workplaces, institutional interiors, suburban homes, traffic scenes, or public-service environments. This supports an interpretation of the pole as **situated social performance**: the commercial creates a small world populated by people in roles, and the message emerges through their interaction.

### 5. Spatial orientation and shot structure

Words such as:

- **opposite** (.72)  
- **direction** (.69)  
- **alternate** (.54)  
- **partition** (.53)  
- **widen** (.37)  
- **other** (.32)

suggest visual organisation through **shot alternation, eyelines, facing positions, and spatial relations among characters**. These are the kinds of words that appear when descriptions track who is looking toward whom, who sits opposite whom, who turns in which direction, or how a scene widens to show a group or setting. This again fits a reaction-shot or vignette structure.

## Evidence from the high-scoring examples

The examples are all from the **2010s**, which is also the decade with the highest mean score. They strongly illustrate the factor’s discourse pattern.

### Example 1: car/restaurant rewards commercial

The highest-scoring text contains many of the pole’s strongest words: **attentive, bearded, uncertain, diner, restaurant, mustache, neutral, bald, tan, opposite, chip, seatbelt, globe, male, vest, serious, plaid, collar, gesture, focused, shirt, expression, beard, man, table, look, turn**.

This example combines several of the factor’s main domains:

- a **bearded male driver** and a **mustached male driver** in adjacent cars;
- visual attention and eyeline work: the men look across, turn, watch, and react;
- a shift to a **restaurant table** with diners, plates, drinks, a bill folder, and a card;
- facial expressions: serious, uncertain, attentive, composed;
- a final product/card display involving a **chip** and graphic branding.

The commercial is not described mainly through product features. Instead, the visual sequence depends on people watching each other, reacting to social situations, and interacting around shared spaces such as cars and restaurant tables.

### Example 2: airline-fee courtroom commercial

This example includes **attentive, neutral, bald, react, vest, serious, watch, uniform, gesture, address, tie, shirt, expression, old, surprised, reaction, man, suit, amused, look**.

The courtroom is an institutional setting filled with socially marked roles: judge, officer, jury-like panel, airline workers, suited man, younger man. The key visual actions are:

- people **addressing** the court;
- the judge watching attentively;
- panel members reacting;
- expressions shifting from serious to amused or surprised;
- uniforms, suits, ties, vests, and role-specific dress.

This is a prototypical case of the pole: a staged social scenario built from role-play, visible reactions, and alternating close-ups.

### Example 3: classroom with suited man and children

Here the loading words include **attentive, react, serious, watch, speak, plaid, gesture, address, tie, shirt, expression, man, suit, table, look**.

The scene is again interactional: a suited adult man sits at a table with children who speak, gesture, and react. The suited man watches seriously. The visual interest lies in the mismatch between character types and the reactions produced in the staged encounter. The setting is a classroom/art room rather than a restaurant or court, but the discourse pattern is the same: a short situational vignette organised around participants, expressions, gestures, and social exchange.

### Example 4: futuristic Intel cafeteria

This example contains **uncertain, diner, neutral, chip, react, speak, tie, shirt, concerned, expression, coat, man, widen, table, look, turn**.

The setting is a cafeteria/office dining area, with men at a table reacting to a robot. The commercial’s visual logic is built around:

- seated diners/workers;
- expressions of surprise, uncertainty, concern, and amusement;
- turns of the head and glances toward the robot;
- a table with food, chips, cups, and plates;
- technologically branded setting and product association.

Again, the factor does not simply pick out “technology”; rather, it picks out the **human reaction scenario** through which the technology is made meaningful.

### Example 5: airport/Capital One miles commercial

This example includes **direction, seatbelt, staff, member, uniform, gesture, address, seating, shop, shirt, man, suit, look**.

The commercial moves through airport counters, seating areas, a shop/newsstand, an airplane cabin, and travel signage. It foregrounds a suited man addressing others, staff members, uniformed workers, passengers, and directional signage. This text illustrates the pole’s link to **service environments**, **public/institutional spaces**, and **role-based commercial skits**.

### Examples 6–10: further confirmation

The remaining examples reinforce the same pattern:

- an old-fashioned rural/suburban scene with a suited man, a boy, and a wagon driver looking and gesturing toward each other;
- a doorstep conversation involving two men, a smartphone, and a third bald man near a car;
- a gym scene with a staff member and a tanned muscular man talking and reacting;
- a fantasy angel/devil scene with men in costume reacting across a desk;
- a living-room scene with concerned expressions, a suited man, a product box, and table display.

Across these examples, the details vary widely, but the visual discourse remains consistent: **characters are placed into staged situations, visually marked by clothing and role, and the commercial proceeds through looks, turns, expressions, gestures, and reactions**.

## Decade pattern and diachronic tendency

The mean decade scores are:

- 1950s: 2.65  
- 1960s: 3.43  
- 1970s: 3.51  
- 1980s: 3.38  
- 1990s: 3.01  
- 2000s: 4.99  
- 2010s: 6.81  
- 2020s: 4.51

The strongest decade is clearly the **2010s**, with the **2000s** and **2020s** also relatively high. Earlier decades score lower, especially the 1950s and 1990s.

This suggests that the discourse captured by this pole becomes especially prominent in the contemporary period, particularly in the 2010s. The rise from the 2000s into the 2010s may reflect a broader advertising style in which commercials increasingly use **mini-narratives, comic sketches, social awkwardness, workplace or service encounters, and reaction-based humour**. The 2020s remain high but lower than the 2010s, perhaps suggesting some continuation of this style with partial diversification into other visual strategies.

The examples being all from the 2010s is important: they show that this pole is strongly associated with the 2010s’ preference for visually scripted scenarios in which brand messages are embedded in short acted scenes, often involving absurd, humorous, or socially awkward exchanges.

---

## Overall interpretation

Factor f5 positive represents a visual discourse of:

> **staged interpersonal commercial vignettes centred on adult characters, visible social roles, facial expressions, gestures, and reaction shots in everyday, service, domestic, or institutional settings.**

The loading words show that this pole is not merely about people appearing on screen. It is specifically about people as **socially interpretable characters**: bearded men, bald men, suited men, uniformed staff, customers, diners, employees, workers, and other role-bearing figures. These characters are visually described through clothing, facial hair, posture, expression, and interactional behaviour.

The examples confirm that high-scoring texts often unfold as short scenes: courtroom parody, restaurant payment moment, cafeteria encounter with a robot, airport miles scenario, doorstep conversation, gym interaction, or living-room product exchange. In each case, the commercial’s persuasive work is carried by **looking, reacting, gesturing, addressing, turning, and displaying emotion**.

A concise label for this pole would therefore be:

> **Interpersonal Reaction-Shot Vignettes**

Alternative labels such as **“Character-Based Social Interaction”** or **“Conversational Role-Play in Everyday Settings”** would also be appropriate, but the first best captures the combination of lexical loadings, high-scoring examples, and the strong 2010s association.

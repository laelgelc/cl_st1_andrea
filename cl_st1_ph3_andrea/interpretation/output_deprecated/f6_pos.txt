## Interpretation of Factor f6 positive pole

Factor f6 positive is best interpreted as a visual discourse of **demonstrated cleaning, stain removal, and surface-maintenance efficacy**, especially in contemporary commercials. The pole is strongly associated with images of household messes, dirty surfaces, product application, wiping/scrubbing/vacuuming, before–after or side-by-side comparison, and final product displays or efficacy claims.

### Possible labels for this pole

1. **“Demonstrated Cleaning and Stain-Removal Efficacy”**  
2. **“Mess-to-Clean Product Demonstration”**  
3. **“Comparative Household Surface-Care Discourse”**  
4. **“Practical Cleaning, Application, and Removal Visuals”**  
5. **“Contemporary Domestic Cleaning Proof-and-Performance Discourse”**

The strongest label is probably:

> **Demonstrated Cleaning and Stain-Removal Efficacy**

because it captures both the high-loading lexis and the visual organization of the high-scoring excerpts: dirt, stains, grease, spills, residue, grime, and cloudy surfaces are shown being removed through product action, often with explicit comparison or claim-making.

The loading words cluster around several related semantic fields.

- **substance** .39  
- **wet** .49

These words point to visual representations of soiled, dirty, greasy, or contaminated surfaces. The inclusion of **fry** is revealing: in the Shark example, food debris such as fries and pasta is used as a visible mess for suction/removal demonstrations. The pole is therefore not just about “clean homes” as a setting, but about **visible contaminants as demonstrable problems**.

These are procedural action verbs. They describe hands pressing sponges or paper towels, cleaners being applied, pods being dropped into washers, tanks being removed, or stains being lifted from surfaces. This suggests a discourse of **process visibility**: the commercial shows the product doing work.

## Evidence from the high-scoring examples

The excerpts strongly confirm the loading-based interpretation. Although all examples shown are from the 2020s, they illustrate the kinds of texts that score highly on this pole.

### Shark portable cleaner: stain, tank, rug, nozzle, comparison

The highest-scoring excerpt, the Shark commercial, is almost a prototypical realization of this pole. It contains many loading words: **stain, comparison, remove, tank, rug, kneel, transparent, floor, clean, fry, nozzle**.

Visually, it moves through a sequence of messes and cleaning demonstrations:

- spills on rugs and wooden floors;
- food debris on furniture;
- a user kneeling beside a cleaning machine;
- a nozzle moving across a wet spill;
- a transparent extraction tool removing stains;
- dirty water and debris collected in the machine’s tank;
- side-by-side comparison of suction and stain extraction;
- a final product lineup.

This example combines nearly all major elements of the pole: visible mess, cleaning technology, removal process, transparent proof, comparison, and product claims.

### Dawn dish soap: sink, wipe, residue, cloudy, comparison

The Dawn example also fits the dimension very strongly. It includes **sink, wipe, remove, residue, lift, rack, clean, press, cloudy, kitchen**.

The ad uses dishes, grease stains, foam, and side-by-side comparison between ordinary dish soap and Dawn. The visual contrast between a cloudy container and a clearer container after washing is a classic “proof of performance” device. Here the pole is realized through **grease removal and dish-cleaning demonstration** rather than carpet or floor cleaning.

### Blueland bathroom cleaner: glove, bathroom, supplies, clean

The Blueland excerpt foregrounds bathroom cleaning. Loading words include **stain, tank, glove, kneel, towel, bathroom, supply, transparent, floor, clean**.

The visuals show a man in gloves kneeling beside a toilet, surrounded by cleaning supplies, holding spray bottles and cloths. This example emphasizes the bodily/manual side of the pole: kneeling, wearing gloves, spraying, wiping, and handling cleaning products in a bathroom environment.

### Mr. Clean professional: grime, floor, comparison, kitchen

The Mr. Clean Professional excerpt adds a commercial or institutional cleaning context. Its loading words include **comparison, remove, glove, supply, grime, rack, floor, clean, kitchen**.

The key visual is a side-by-side mop comparison on a dirty floor, with text claiming that the product “REMOVES GRIME IN HALF THE TIME.” This is a concentrated expression of the pole’s comparative proof discourse: dirty surface, product action, competitor comparison, explicit performance claim.

### Bounty paper towel: wipe, remove, rug, towel, drop, press

The Bounty commercial shows a spill being absorbed and wiped from a surface, followed by a comparison with a leading ordinary brand. Loading words include **comparison, wipe, remove, rug, kneel, towel, drop, clean, press**.

Here the cleaning technology is not a machine or chemical cleaner but an absorbent towel. Nonetheless, the visual discourse is the same: a spill appears, a product is pressed onto it, the mess is removed, and the product is compared with a competitor.

### Tide laundry: residue, drop, comparison, lineup, clean

The Tide excerpt extends the pole from visible household surfaces to fabric hygiene. Loading words include **comparison, patch, residue, drop, lineup, clean**.

The commercial uses UV-style visualization of “invisible dirt,” a pod dropped into the washer, and before/after comparison. Even when dirt is not visible to the naked eye, the ad creates a visual proof system to make residue detectable and removal demonstrable.

### Less central but still related examples

Some high-scoring examples are less purely about cleaning but still activate parts of the same lexical field.

The Home Depot example includes **supply, floor, clean, reach, pull**, and involves home improvement, flooring, ordering supplies, and project work. This connects to the broader surface-maintenance and domestic-task domain.

The Icy Hot example includes **comparison, patch, direct, apply**, which overlaps with product application, claim-making, and comparative package visuals, though its semantic domain is pain relief rather than cleaning. This suggests that part of the pole also captures **demonstrative product application and efficacy proof**, not only cleaning.

The Walmart fire-station example includes **sink, supply, rack, kitchen, reach**, reflecting the kitchen/supply setting rather than an explicit cleaning demonstration. This shows that some texts may score through the domestic/service-space vocabulary of kitchens, racks, sinks, and supplies, even without a full stain-removal sequence.

## Decades driving the pole

The decade scores show a clear diachronic increase:

- 1950s: 0.78  
- 1960s: 1.09  
- 1970s: 1.11  
- 1980s: 1.00  
- 1990s: 1.43  
- 2000s: 1.61  
- 2010s: 2.12  
- 2020s: 2.67  

The strongest decade is the **2020s**, followed by the **2010s**, then the **2000s** and **1990s**. Earlier decades score positively but much less strongly. This suggests that the discourse encoded by this pole is present across the whole television-commercial visual subcorpus, but becomes increasingly prominent in later decades.

The supplied high-scoring examples are all from the 2020s, which is consistent with the decade means. The 2020s appear to drive the pole especially strongly through commercials that rely on:

- close-up demonstrations of product action;
- side-by-side competitor comparisons;
- visible stains, spills, grease, grime, and residue;
- transparent tools, tanks, and containers;
- on-screen claims such as “30X MORE,” “REMOVES GRIME,” “BETTER GREASE GETTER,” or “10X ACTIVE INGREDIENTS”;
- final product lineups against clean graphic backgrounds.

Diachronically, the pole seems to capture a movement toward **highly visualized, evidence-oriented product performance advertising**, especially for cleaning, hygiene, household maintenance, and related practical-use products. Later commercials appear more likely to show the product solving a visible problem through staged demonstrations, split screens, close-ups, graphic overlays, and explicit comparative claims.

---

## Summary interpretation

Factor f6 positive represents a discourse of **practical product efficacy made visible through cleaning and removal demonstrations**. Its core imagery is domestic or service-space surfaces—rugs, floors, sinks, kitchens, bathrooms, dishes, upholstery, laundry, toilets, and counters—being soiled, treated, wiped, scrubbed, vacuumed, or otherwise restored. The pole also includes the advertising grammar of proof: comparison panels, product claims, transparent tanks or tools, before/after contrasts, and final product lineups.

A concise label would be:

> **Demonstrated Cleaning and Stain-Removal Efficacy**

A slightly broader label, allowing for examples such as Icy Hot, Home Depot, and Walmart, would be:

> **Practical Product Application and Surface-Care Demonstration**

But given the strength of loadings such as **stain, sink, wipe, remove, tank, rug, glove, towel, bathroom, residue, grime, bleach, nozzle, detergent, kitchen, clean**, the cleaning/removal interpretation is the central one.

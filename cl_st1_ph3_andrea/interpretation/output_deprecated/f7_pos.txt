## Interpretation of Factor f7 positive pole

### Proposed label

**“Vintage soft-focus product-display montage”**

Alternative possible labels:

1. **“Archival dissolve-and-superimposition product discourse”**  
2. **“Print-like product demonstration and end-card display”**  
3. **“Older broadcast commercial visuality: soft, layered, domestic product presentation”**  
4. **“1970s-style archival product montage with textual overlays”**

The most suitable concise label is:

> **Vintage soft-focus product-display montage**

---

## Core discourse encoded at this pole

The positive pole of Factor 7 captures a visual discourse strongly associated with **older television-commercial aesthetics**, especially commercials that are described through:

- **soft, hazy, indistinct, or degraded image quality**;
- **dissolves, fades, superimpositions, overlapping images, and montage transitions**;
- **printed or written material on screen**, including title cards, product labels, prices, slogans, cursive script, inset pictures, and final product displays;
- **domestic, household, food, beauty, hygiene, appliance, and everyday product settings**;
- **static or composed closing shots** in which the product is displayed prominently with text.

This pole is therefore not simply about one advertised product type. The examples include cars, cameras, mayonnaise, cosmetics, inhalers, disinfectant, perfume, and baking products. What unites them is a **visual mode of presentation**: the commercial is described as a sequence of soft, often archival-looking images that move through dissolves or superimpositions toward a clear product shot, slogan, label, or printed end frame.

The strongest decade is the **1970s**, and all ten high-scoring excerpts supplied are from the 1970s. The 1950s and 1960s also score highly, suggesting that the pole indexes a broader older-commercial visual style, but the examples indicate that the 1970s are especially central in this factor.

Diachronically, the scores decline sharply after the 1990s, reaching very low means in the 2010s and 2020s. This suggests that the pole is associated with **mid-century to late-analogue television advertising conventions and/or their archival presentation**, rather than with more recent commercial visual styles.

The loading words cluster into several mutually reinforcing semantic groups.

- **unclear** .39  
- **vague** .34  
- **blurry** .31  
- **faint** .30  
- **soft** .32

- **photograph** .38  

These words indicate that the pole is strongly associated with descriptions of commercials as **old, transferred, low-resolution, blurred, or visually mediated artefacts**. The repeated presence of *watermark*, *classic*, and *archival* is particularly important: part of this dimension reflects not only the original advertising style but also the fact that many high-scoring texts describe commercials preserved through archive channels, with visible watermarks and degraded video quality.

This is repeatedly illustrated in the examples, which mention “Classic Commercial Archives” watermarks, faint archive marks, hazy frames, color bleeding, overexposure, soft focus, and unclear objects.

### 2. Dissolve, fade, superimposition, and layered montage

High-loading editing terms include:

- **dissolve** .63  
- **superimpose** .60  
- **fade** .54  
- **overlapping** .44  
- **unfold** .41  
- **opening** .37  
- **closing** .63  
- **moment** .57  
- **beginning** .43  

This group points to a discourse of **sequenced visual unfolding**, where commercials are described as moving from one image to another through dissolves, fades, and overlays. The visual flow is not primarily described as fast digital cutting or cinematic realism, but as a layered analogue montage.

The examples strongly support this. The Fiat commercial “unfolds as a fast, soft-focus montage” with frequent dissolves. The Polaroid commercial uses “quick dissolves and superimposed imagery.” The Hellmann’s example overlays the smiling woman’s face over the picnic scene. The Vicks inhaler commercial superimposes product graphics over narrative scenes. The Nestlé example crossfades between a man, baked goods, a child, and the product package.

This gives the pole a recognisable **analogue-TV transition aesthetic**.

The examples repeatedly end with product displays or text-heavy frames:

The positive pole therefore encodes a discourse in which the commercial image often behaves like a **moving print advertisement**: a soft narrative or demonstration sequence culminates in a clear labelled product shot.

## Evidence from the examples

The high-scoring excerpts all strongly instantiate the loading patterns.

### f7_pos_001: Fiat Strada

This example has many of the pole’s central features: *dissolve, picture, moment, watermark, classic, decorative, overlapping, unclear, hazy, prominent, faint*. The commercial is described as a soft-focus montage with archive watermarks, dissolves, unclear frames, and a final product-text shot. Even though the product is a car, the visual discourse is still one of **archival montage plus final printed product identification**.

### f7_pos_002: Polaroid

This excerpt directly combines *printed, dissolve, superimpose, fade, indistinct, overlapping, photograph, object, soft*. It is highly representative because the product itself is photographic, and the commercial contains instant-photo imagery, crowds with cameras, layered portrait images, and a price/title card. This is almost a prototypical case of the pole’s **picture/photograph/printed-display** discourse.

### f7_pos_004: Hellmann’s

This example brings in the domestic-food side of the pole: *appliance, dissolve, superimpose, leafy, serve, object, spoon, soft*. The commercial moves from kitchen preparation to outdoor picnic, with salad ingredients, a jar, a spoon, and a final product shot. It shows how the pole combines **domestic demonstration**, **food serving**, and **soft dissolve-based montage**.

### f7_pos_005 and f7_pos_007: cosmetics and feminine-care imagery

These examples foreground *script, cursive, decorative, blouse, object, prominent, faint, soft*. They show the pole’s association with beauty and personal-care advertising presented through soft-focus femininity, product arrangements, handwritten or decorative text, and final labelled displays.

### f7_pos_006: Vicks inhaler

This excerpt illustrates the explanatory-product component: the commercial uses superimposed graphics, a line drawing of the nasal passage, a product tube, and slogan text. The loadings *dissolve, superimpose, fade, object, soft* are central. This suggests that the pole also includes commercials where product function or mechanism is visually explained through simple graphic overlays.

### f7_pos_008: Lysol

This example strongly supports the household/bathroom-product interpretation. It includes a bathroom, sink, toilet, brown bottle, product package, headline text, and inset image. The loading words *picture, inset, prominent, object, soft, classic, watermark* match the factor closely.

### f7_pos_010: Nestlé

This example combines food, packaging, utensils, superimposition, and a final product composition. The commercial ends with a package and text naming the recipe/product use, again showing the print-like end-card style.

## Discourse dimension

This pole can be interpreted as a discourse of:

> **older, analogue-era product presentation in which soft or degraded moving images, dissolves, superimpositions, domestic/product-use scenes, and printed end-card displays work together to identify and sell the product.**

The commercials at this pole often present the product as a **clearly labelled object**. They may begin with narrative, lifestyle, or domestic scenes, but they typically move toward a composed product shot, package, slogan, price, or written claim. The visual rhetoric is one of **display, identification, and demonstration**, mediated through the aesthetics of archival television footage.

---

## Diachronic interpretation

The factor is clearly associated with earlier advertising decades, peaking in the **1970s** and remaining high in the **1950s and 1960s**. The 1980s and 1990s still show moderate association, but the pole becomes much less characteristic of commercials from the 2000s onward.

This diachronic pattern suggests that the positive pole indexes a historically older commercial style: analogue video, soft focus, dissolves, visible product cards, household demonstration, and print-like closing frames. The very low scores in the 2010s and 2020s imply that this discourse becomes much less prominent in more recent visual-commercial descriptions.

---

## Final recommended label

**Vintage soft-focus product-display montage**

This label captures the main components of the pole:

- **Vintage / archival**: watermarks, classic, faint, hazy, indistinct, soft image quality.
- **Soft-focus montage**: dissolve, fade, superimpose, overlapping, unfold.
- **Product-display**: printed text, script, labels, photographs, insets, prominent objects, closing product shots.
- **Domestic/product demonstration**: appliances, utensils, soap, sinks, bathrooms, food serving, household and personal-care objects.

A slightly more technical LMDA-style label could be:

> **Archival analogue product-display discourse**

or, if foregrounding the visual mechanics:

> **Dissolve-based print-and-product montage**

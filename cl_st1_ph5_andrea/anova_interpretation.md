## Interpretation of the verbal ANOVA by decade

This ANOVA table tests whether each **verbal discourse dimension** varies significantly across decades. In other words, it asks whether the mean scores for each verbal dimension are stable over time or whether they show diachronic change across the commercial corpus.

The key values are:

- **F**: strength of the between-decade difference relative to within-decade variability;
- **p**: statistical significance of the decade effect;
- **R²**: proportion of variance explained by decade;
- **R² %**: the same effect size expressed as a percentage.

---

### Overall pattern

The results show that **four verbal dimensions vary significantly by decade**:

| Dimension | p-value | R² % | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| `ver1` | `< .0001` | 35.55% | Strong diachronic variation |
| `ver3` | `< .0001` | 6.40% | Moderate diachronic variation |
| `ver7` | `< .0001` | 3.75% | Small but significant diachronic variation |
| `ver8` | `.0014` | 2.85% | Small but significant diachronic variation |

The remaining dimensions do **not** show statistically significant decade effects at the conventional `.05` level:

| Dimension | p-value | R² % | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| `ver2` | `.3224` | 0.99% | No significant diachronic variation |
| `ver4` | `.1705` | 1.26% | No significant diachronic variation |
| `ver5` | `.0634` | 1.63% | Marginal tendency, but not significant at `.05` |
| `ver6` | `.9116` | 0.33% | No diachronic variation |

---

### Strongest result: `ver1`

The most important result is clearly **`ver1`**:

\[
F = 63.9900,\quad p < .0001,\quad R^2 = .3555
\]

This means that **decade explains 35.55% of the variance** in `ver1`, which is a very large effect compared with the other verbal dimensions.

Substantively, this suggests that `ver1` is the verbal discourse dimension with the strongest historical shift across the corpus. It is not merely statistically significant; it is also the only verbal dimension where decade accounts for a large share of the variation.

So, for interpretation, `ver1` should be treated as the **main verbal diachronic dimension**.

---

### Secondary significant dimensions

#### `ver3`

\[
F = 7.9300,\quad p < .0001,\quad R^2 = .0640
\]

`ver3` also varies significantly by decade, but the effect is much smaller than for `ver1`. Decade explains **6.40%** of the variance. This is still meaningful, especially in discourse data, but it should be interpreted as a **secondary diachronic pattern**.

#### `ver7`

\[
F = 4.5200,\quad p < .0001,\quad R^2 = .0375
\]

`ver7` shows a statistically significant decade effect, but the explanatory power is modest: **3.75%** of the variance. This suggests a real but relatively small diachronic pattern.

#### `ver8`

\[
F = 3.4000,\quad p = .0014,\quad R^2 = .0285
\]

`ver8` also varies significantly across decades, but again with a small effect size: decade explains **2.85%** of the variance.

Together, `ver3`, `ver7`, and `ver8` indicate that several verbal dimensions change over time, but none approaches the strength of `ver1`.

---

### Non-significant or marginal dimensions

#### `ver2`, `ver4`, and `ver6`

These dimensions show no statistically reliable decade effect:

- `ver2`: \(p = .3224\)
- `ver4`: \(p = .1705\)
- `ver6`: \(p = .9116\)

Their R² values are also very low, all around or below **1.26%**, meaning that decade explains very little of their variation.

This suggests that these verbal discourse patterns are comparatively **stable across decades** or vary in ways that are not systematically captured by decade.

#### `ver5`

`ver5` is close to significance:

\[
F = 1.9200,\quad p = .0634,\quad R^2 = .0163
\]

This may suggest a weak tendency toward diachronic variation, but it should not be treated as statistically significant at the conventional `.05` threshold. It can be described as **marginal** or **suggestive**, but not as strong evidence of change.

---

### Interpretation for the study

The verbal ANOVA results show that **diachronic variation is not evenly distributed across all verbal discourse dimensions**. Instead, it is highly concentrated in `ver1`.

This has two important implications:

1. **Verbal discourse change is selective**  
   Some verbal dimensions change substantially over time, while others remain relatively stable.

2. **`ver1` is the main verbal marker of historical change**  
   Since `ver1` has both the strongest F statistic and by far the largest R², it should receive priority in the interpretation of diachronic verbal discourse variation.

The smaller but significant effects for `ver3`, `ver7`, and `ver8` suggest additional historical shifts, but these are secondary compared with the dominant pattern represented by `ver1`.
